
by Alcuin of York
Patrologia Latina Volume 

Let us mourn for our cuckoo, sweetest Daphnis,
whom a cruel stepmother has suddenly snatched away from his own people.
Let us mourn for him together with voices of lament;
please begin first, elder Menalcas.
Alas! The cuckoo used to sing for us,
but what unspeakable hour has now seized you from your own?
Alas! Cuckoo, cuckoo, in what land have I left you behind?
That day was unfortunate for us.
When every kind of person mourns the cuckoo everywhere,
he is lost; look, alas, my cuckoo has perished!
May the cuckoo not perish! He will come in the springtime,
and coming to us, he will stir up joyful songs.
Who knows if he will come? I fear he has been submerged in the waves,
seized by whirlpools and drowned in the waters.
Woe to me! If Bacchus has plunged the cuckoo into the waves,
he who seizes young men in his deadly whirlpool.
If he lives, may he return and run back to his nurturing nests,
and may no raven tear the cuckoo apart with savage claw.
Alas! Who is now snatching you, cuckoo, from your father's nest?
Alas! He has been seized, seized! I do not know if he will come.
If you care for songs, cuckoo, while you are still able to run,
your young Daphnis longs to have you back.
The time of spring is here, cuckoo; now break your slumber!
Both the elder and father Menalcas desire you.
Look, at last the meadows of our books await the young oxen,
but the cuckoo alone is absent. Who, I ask, is feeding him?
Alas! That impious Bacchus is feeding him badly, I think,
he who desires to turn all hearts to evil beneath him.
Mourn now for the cuckoo; let everyone now mourn for the cuckoo!
He departed rejoicing, but I think he will return weeping.
Nevertheless, I hope that we may have that cuckoo back, even if weeping,
and that we may lament together with the cuckoo.
Weep for your misfortunes with tears, noble boy, weep!
And let your whole heart mourn for your misfortunes.
If hard flint did not give birth to you, weep, we beg you;
remembering yourself, perhaps you are able to weep.
Sweet love for a son compels the parent to weep,
when the son is suddenly torn from his embrace.
When a brother loses his beloved brother,
what else should he do now but always weep?
Once we were three, whom one spirit joined together,
but now scarcely two remain together; that third one has fled.
Alas! He will flee, he will flee, and therefore bitter lamentation
now remains for us; the dear cuckoo departs.
Let us send songs after him, songs of mourning;
songs perhaps, I believe, will lead the cuckoo back.
May you always be happy, wherever you go,
and be mindful of us. Farewell always and everywhere!
