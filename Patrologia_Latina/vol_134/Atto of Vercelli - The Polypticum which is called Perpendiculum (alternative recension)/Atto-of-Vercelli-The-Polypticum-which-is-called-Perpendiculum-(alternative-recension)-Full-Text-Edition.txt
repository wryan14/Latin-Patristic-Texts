Here begins the letter. To the very reverend bishop, so-and-so, who is worthy of reverence in my eyes.

So-and-so, desiring me thus to have a blessed end, urges me like the most blessed shepherd Sylvester, though dead to some, yet not truly dead but alive—indeed you are living though dead, yet alive in me. As we run through the path of this labyrinth, teach me either how to avoid the abyss by careful treading, or how those lifted up in health might reach its delights. What I feel in this matter you will discern, but what I perceive and what you perceive are compared alike. They measure out like flour for weighing. Behold, what I give to you, return happily. Amen. Here ends the letter.

Here begins the argument.

Sophists do not explain chaos; I shall pursue the persistent droning of affairs throughout the world. No leisure pursuits shake this pen into mystic obscurity, nor will idle trifles grind it down. Already wearied, I shall now set forth my measuring standard. Its voice cannot be restrained by sharpness, nor can Attic Rome silence it with eloquence. Here ends the argument.

Here begins the Polypticum, which is called the Perpendiculum.

The fragile pomp of this age is weakness, and yet it is sought after; it is a plague, and yet it is pursued in every way. It smiles upon approach, it delights as it creeps along, in its youthful vigor there is immense toil, when it swells up it casts down, and when it departs it is cursed—because it tends to make its clients ignoble, while making those who despise it glorious. This is no wonder, since up to now it has been the ruin of nearly all who seek it out, yet the arrogant do not diminish, nor does the bitterness of self-advancement grow moderate. They deceive themselves, thinking themselves superior to the ancients, whom prudent judgment accuses of being more sluggish. Therefore, if the bundles of authority belonging to these men are carefully examined step by step, I judge it worthy to mark out in advance those whom they display adorned with a stiff garland. Do not the chronicles expose this, which particularly proclaim the uttermost woe and cry out against all their excesses? The throng does not loudly proclaim those who, by climbing the tangled hill, led their followers according to the direct rubric, though they paint an inextricable pattern—men who, though enduring, neither lived as survivors nor rejoiced as wretches. But I do not think it genuine to record privately the deeds accomplished, nor to record them because their pupils in their youthful age now see these things clearly and have drunk in what was frequently recited by their fathers, and they retain more certainly what has been impressed upon them. Now it is ready at hand to examine the members of this aforementioned wickedness, according to what the care licking over the matter has represented with a melancholic pen.
This matter which we are unraveling climbs to its summit by a threefold distinction. Some add their support either when a leader designates someone by name with clear nods, or when the same voice calls out on behalf of all, or when the same voice calls out for one person, or when by a parent's strong right it is lawful to enjoy the throne. But wicked toil comes about when insane battles meet triumphs, and they rush to rise up against another whom the herald had already proclaimed, and those with unhallowed hands abominably favor him, though they have not sworn allegiance. What happens? The rival is thoroughly emptied out, by whose favor the first skirmishers are enriched, and they do not seek an inviolable covenant in which their master's sleep might find sweetness. But meanwhile it pleases them to pass the time more pleasantly in relaxation and to take possession of what they have agreeably obtained, so that both princes may be terrified by opposing lawsuits and the powerful may not dare to stamp their feet in rage. On the other side, nothing remains for the soldiers except to deny anything, so that the princes may flourish in name only, miserably, so that they are not truly in control but rather masters of private affairs only. Then they diligently ruminate and with secret care repay these tricks to their allies. For this reason they do not grow weary of secretly meeting one another in separate branches through cautious messengers, who cleverly have not announced things frivolously to the loyal ones, through which means the soldiers are first instigated, supported by investigation. They also ask foreign leaders for connivance, and heap up gifts upon them, and quickly set down sacrificial offerings. Why does fortune promise to outsiders the power of the mighty, not yet wrested away, which these intermediaries eagerly desire to taste, even briefly—so that their aid may burn to subdue the proud along with them, or if they grow idle and sluggish, then to rage through their own weapons and deny aid to the oppressed, for they themselves will rage.
Therefore there remains an example of the deceit by which affected tribunes are crushed at the bottom, when their soldiers seek the flattering royal breezes; and the designated fortifiers return from there to their own places with laden shoulders, and they will not tolerate leaders as their masters or primary arbiters, even in bestowing benefits—they will put themselves forward, whether benefits are scarce or abundant. And they lend money free from their own resources, and they transport loyalists to themselves at another's loss. They prefer those through whom they receive benefits, but not those from whom they receive them. If chance separates these people, they themselves will serve until they learn from one another things conveyed between them that would better be kept silent. Indeed, they determine to spy on these people and to instruct themselves. From among them they scatter throughout individual households like seeds, from which they often reap trifles and often learn in advance what is expedient. From this point the soldiers dissolve their first loyalty to their lords and firmly resolve to keep their second loyalty to princes—which loyalty will already fail. Then they mark themselves with infamy, they undermine their lords, and they do not refuse to deny the highest authority. Thus enticed by foul desire, they sometimes hope to provide light relief to the sorrowful if chance brings any danger—however much they heap up from their own resources, they overthrow by sharing. Therefore princes additionally fortify rough places and all scraped-away areas through erosion, so that no one can convey anything unknown to themselves. And those for whom very strong fortifications exist, they either oppress with hostages or strive to remove. In whose trust, if perhaps you believe you should commit your estate so that they may be zealous for you, or if you should care to impose any title, then to measure out the inscription for your own heirs—whatever is useful they claim entirely for themselves, and they do not undertake to distribute even a part among those things which they once prepared. They build strongholds of counsel so that there may be no raid that terrifies them, but they may calmly be able to render to anyone what they have meditated upon. In this they establish foundations and construct three-storied buildings. But after they have firmly fixed their slipping footholds, then they arrange quarrels digested among the minds of nobles, and they affirm that they are allies of both opposing sides, and these kidnappers accuse rivals to rivals as unfaithful, because they act—and they themselves are as perverse as those others. They accuse those set apart with such great crime, lest the wicked quiver be revealed and lest it bite anyone that he will be ungrateful indifferently. They sing of the summoned as very famous and they prolong the dissension, calling upon sacred things that they not return together with them to the former state, until they are turned to the lowest point and the judges themselves, striving, are not separated from these crafty concerns and whisperings. It will prevail if these things have been abandoned unaccomplished. They do not cleanse the innocent, whom judgment does not weary. Then the throat is pressed with horror, and the voice urgently supporting the edicts escapes to the courts with polished deceits of exile, with doors squealing and pupils livid. This champion of truth is marked with a rabid stigma, as prey to be quickly torn apart. Just as when the fur-clad Parthians aim their goat-stags, and Scorpus, going ahead, has caught the breezes of death, then, with himself as leader, turning about, he drives herds of cattle through the air—to him each archer attributes the fact that not all were laid low. Cursing him alone, they blaspheme and weary themselves trying to restrain him. Thus whoever is able to recognize the frauds of leaders, if he brings them into the open—but he commands his allies to be hidden in gleaming moldings, and frequently they call upon warriors both in beaten paths and in purchases, so that they may release themselves, hidden while they are—and to those with horrible morsels they eagerly study to separate them from blood, or whatever they leave behind for those they destroy. They provide advancement for these until— but from the loss of others. Among whom there are also certain ones who, while they envy the fortune of others obtained for themselves, wear them down to nullify them with these deceits—they display enticing sorrows, and they bring forth that the princes who had helped them had inflicted hardships upon them—and they betray them as worthy of being mocked, and they reject vindicating these things, and they argue forcefully. Those men, while they strive to accomplish this, rather seek ways to speak ambiguously about such matters—those who, if they perceive that they have already obtained means by which they can crush leaders, then seek profit from that source. But if they make themselves cautious, to whom they also expose their allies and dissolve their schemes. And they teach first that they were called into these matters by others and soon fled to them in order to betray, and they demand that what the rebels possessed be attributed to themselves as loyal ones, so that the gifts of betrayal may remain. They look to tear away what then yields, and the ancient things—when the auxiliaries have been scraped away by diverse battle lines and by diverse men who flourish in skill—then these also pay penalties, and with sad outcome they are made exiles from advantages and are not refreshed by anyone. In turn they lament; nor will there be lacking a title of crime by which misery is instigated, as with the others which—alas, woe is me!—rapacious gluttons have made public. Then also the death and ruin of others is cast at these men, and the leaders who have done foul deeds through all accuse these men as kindling of crimes. They are ashamed, and they do not try to bring back those who have been led astray. But these things only so that they can cast in hatreds. Rumor spreads about a certain bitter bird, which, while it chirps with swelling genital heat and caresses with wings relaxed, a mate of different kind, not nature, comes to help. When the bird has accomplished this and has sensed itself pregnant, it pursues joyfully to tear apart the endowed entrails of its spouse. Thus leaders grow fat on spoils; they gape to rise up against one another, lest empty lust be impeded from any crime it desires.
While unpeaceful guardians drag different things, hating one another—neither do two feel as one, nor can individuals resist—they proceed with headbands wherever it is only necessary to wait for what the urn will determine. Anyone is ashamed to breathe his very spirit through the upper air, and whatever things remain ungrateful to each person while they are held must be taken away—more blessed now is he who has been betrayed, who would have been immune. From this point some exiles reject kisses and do not fear to deprive their children. And others indeed pierce pallid entrails with the paschal sacrifice. And there are idle ones watching the knot of the pyramid and the cave of the icon or the pit. And there remain those from whom the sight of tyrants is defrauded, or those whose father's name has been cut off—the youth seems to preserve it. From others is taken away the part which, experiencing, carries four senses, and, torn away, restrains another. Nor do hatreds thus suppress things lulled to sleep, but they pursue whatever remains of the offspring. But raging desire, while it enjoys the guilty—lest it grant anything to be received as wickedness—rages unbridled into the precipice even through upright men themselves. And after distinguished men have been torn down, their mockers, who restrain themselves from gaping at the profit, are entangled in troubles. And those who once renounced them with empty faith then seek seduced right hands, now to be endowed with no estates or resources—hands which they would earlier have pleasantly committed to the aforementioned lords if they had kept the first covenants. And if origin has brought forth these men as well-born, continuous murders obey wickedness. Afterward they compel themselves to go as shipwrecked men with fir-tree feet, and they plead as surrendered ones, so that even unwillingly they may fulfill what they had promised as moneylenders when rewarded.
Who are chosen as arbiters? Those whom it does not please to report what has been observed, nor does it please to observe what has been inserted into ancient volumes. They thrust forward hereditary nods that have been contrived, and thus they fashion laws tainted with bribery, so that the balanced scale of justice weighs no one gracefully. Nor do they love to judge for all—he himself will go out unharmed, and although he may excuse the one whom they send forth into exile, even one holding fortune will succumb, either broken by force or driven away by treachery. Nor does the judgment, lasting the same, often respond with one sentence—that which now accuses, soon again puffs up with praises, and again proclaims the same things profane and even worse. But the founder of the lofty citadel, indeed crowned and leaning on his throne, is raised up in abundance, and he is made fertile to mock the remaining cohort with crude syllogisms. He rejoices if the final day delivers some while the heir enters. And to the widows who survive from them, no choice is given whether they remain thus or to whom they should prepare themselves at the crossroads, unless they redeem those whose unsuitable marriages are either purchased from the undeserving, or they raise up needy clients of the king, who do not hesitate to overshadow stepsons or to exclude wasteful mothers. And the leader sometimes takes women marked with figures into the stone-paved hall for shameful uses. Then he compels the other native people brought forth to live privately, and lets needy foreigners lie with chosen ears, because he hopes generously that they will be his.
And if only the citizens serve as empty chatterers, with sacred mammon acting as intermediary, so that they might rather celebrate servile duties and obey commands in all things. Nor let them boast: genuine lineage poured forth these great deeds for us. Because they applaud in astonishment but are ignorant of what gleams through the ceremonial robe, nor has the purple-bordered toga taught them. The doorkeeper deceives these people, and they are accustomed to prefer the liquid that heat has driven from the cave to the pebbles of India and the Caucasus. And while they lift up the badges of rank with goat-hair cloth, they do not know how to fit them properly in turn. And when the common people consult these men, gnashing their teeth they press down with braying their barbaric followers. They disdain what the laws echo back, and with the coldness of their trumpeting they terrify their subjects. And first they rejoice to pass along pomp with unknown successions, but truly when they begin to examine the character of fortune—although outwardly they postpone cares for the next day, still the wound is examined under the breast. Thus after they have given themselves to rest on high supports, then they also take up the garland under the open sky and frequently attend water clocks in mourning, to see if they deceive in their measurement. Their spirits fluctuate while they investigate unusual things, now stolen wealth, now also they fear the ruin of the degraded prince, as previous examples taught them while they believed in tranquility.
Which they do not consider pardonable, nor does any manner excuse passing over while every servitude is shaken off by them as if by undeserving stewards, and the authority of the seat does not excuse anyone by its own regard, nor does glory drive away the insult from those whom dignity and the praises of ancient men engraved in catalogues hold back. If origin has given a blemish, they prepare to cover it over and to shine forth only from the dignity they have assumed. With this they goad themselves. But while conscience vexes the spirits of degenerate offspring, whom terror more tightly entangles, they revolve in their minds their shameless master, and they reconsider their predecessors and consuls, and they examine the territories they have subdued, and they fear to go out from where they demand that companions be fulfilled by threat. While now long security incautiously prepares a fall, as the teaching series depicts. Nor do they make clear the compacts while they force the led-in soldiers into a single ball, so that no one may seek to resist lest he be exiled. Then with one face they burst forth to accuse the one to whom they grant indulgence, and they keep chaste faith if it is permitted to depart. Whom also, if perchance he opposes freckles to the people, or if he murmurs to prisoners, they will compel him to compensate for his silence, or troublesomely they draw him into the light and force him to experience a bitter journey and to wander through unknown ages.
From this point they flow forth in their spirits while, as masters of inheritance, they become prostrate before the king. From which source they often cultivate cleverness by displaying proud things, and they hire themselves out in unreasoning ways to sip at wine-mixing bowls, and they distribute cups to one another and give back Sabaean half-girdles weighted with gleaming bosses. Again they redouble their binding of themselves with new faith, their lineages mutually giving brides to each other in turn, and reciprocally they receive for themselves pledges to be preserved in the shrine through clientage, and they persist in compacts by means of such black bile. The rest swear by the Lord of spirits and by the urns of his most faithful witnesses hidden in the polished receptacle. Therefore they distinguish among themselves race and fatherland, and they overturn the poor, and reckless men do not hate any sacred things whatsoever. The report is that when the antelope begins to crush the mane of the hedgehog, it entangles itself in death and finally, finally betrays itself by its own voice. Thus it happens to those who presume to violate sacred things. Meanwhile punishment does not cease, although they are believed to have been forgotten—those things which they long ago bore with difficulty, or if those things which they commanded were not obtained—opportunity avenges upon them.
Moreover, threefold they add to accomplishing the destruction of guilty ones for whom they once pretended to repel the light. For whom the plunder is furniture, about which the court again proclaims, when there is no one who mutters about the court. Wealth considers from afar and does not prophesy among these a share with itself—those whom new-fangled buzzards surround, with whose applauding crowd you will hear the driveler, if he wishes to bring forth sumptuously with them, with truth made obsolete. O times, which drag no celebrated man to a judge, yet peasants persist. I recall the funeral pyres, but I do not approve as consuls men such as these who thus look ahead to weigh all things in prosperous circumstances, while they draw out drinking parties with music through the city, swollen with melodies, both echoed and beaten with burst-forth strings—from there a singing voice supplies. Istria provides leaps. Cup-bearers keep vigil perpetually to mix together at once clarified wines with nectar, and they serve beer and cider so that they may put to flight the frugal and dissolve drunken bodies—alas, the wickedness betraying from this source! They make a loud noise everywhere with wanton laughter, and they mutually incite themselves by hurling lascivious tales. Then enticements suggest debaucheries and incests; and slaves, by gratifying their masters, spend the night in these things, reveling all around.
But they will not be able to enjoy these things for long without someone's jealousy, since they are equal to others in rank and excel in such great dignity. For toil prepares concord, dissension prepares insolence, and the lesser nobles separately hasten to unite themselves equally because of the injury of the greater ones, and they argue that more things are owed to them—things which they retain and punish as needing to be carried out. These excel in battle lines, and virtue burns with full strength. For those others, more mature judgment flourishes, and they excel in wealth and are strengthened by the height of their walls. But nevertheless they consider it ineffective to extinguish what boils up unless they establish a prince under whom—but they refuse to establish themselves. Finally they enter into counsel to put forward a stupid man for themselves, through whom they may accomplish what things are advantageous, who may not presume to investigate or refuse them anything, whom they also force to weave these things through divine sacred oaths in vain. For when he has been made prelate, if he has remained dull he will wash away no grief. Indeed, even young men will now powerfully add to derogating from the ancients. Nor do they accept a lord unless he is separated from those men. Those who are eloquent, on the contrary, threaten to inflict worse things on crude men, and they exercise the lord, and the promises are required so that they may rise up against adversaries, and they teach. What will he do? Will he be able to avoid both ruins? But natural talents do not support an incompetent man. It remains that he flee those who hate him and be free as a private citizen.
But if indeed he first makes public that he is cowardly in skill—in which also prudence immediately shines forth with the crown favoring him—then, terrified, they secretly take care to lull hatreds to sleep. And again they mutter about bringing things back into one, so that again they may be able to unweave the hereditary plague in this way—which will not be able to hide from a leader already clear in his knowledge of himself. These trifles he prepares to abolish along with the triflers, whom they themselves also eagerly desire to exclude from the increased throne against him. Those whom first the preceding things hinder, which inform the leader. But if they judge these things to be feared and think only that they themselves are failing in these matters, then also from a distance they send treasures to hire heroes for themselves. From this source they hold consolations to be increased; indeed, they also prepare from there to promote a foreign and strong leader and to submit their necks. Then indeed they cast votes, and first through deputies if it has not been possible to celebrate an assembly. To whom, if these arranged matters are deferred for a little while, they will fail incomplete. Or meanwhile will the shaken rage of the present president cease to rage, and will he grow sluggish in feeding on the springs? Will he not rather more strenuously act to obtain perpetual inheritors for the overthrown?
And if they prepare to help those whom, by often greeting them, they once tried to advance—as soon as he has perceived this hissing of slipping reputation—on the contrary, they turn aside and report to the ears of the flourishing leader that, as a plague, it has been too much sought after against themselves by their own soldiers, to whose minds no one was able to persuade; and they taint with charity what fear had washed away, and with such fabrications they expect returns from his side, although unfaithful nobles. Long ago they sent ahead to these men the prerogatives which they have purchased by degenerating. To whom, however, on behalf of widows the leader extends few things and modestly argues: If these things had been revealed to us more maturely, truly openly, while it would have helped blindly and secretly. And he looks down on their weakened limbs and mocks their rabid hearts. There are also foreign princes who, when they have drunk in the laments of another's soldiers as messengers, immediately demand that the seduced ones be confirmed lest they ever return to the favor of the former leader—to whom also soon by equal reasoning they confirm they will bring aid.
To whose king they then cautiously and very swiftly send legates to reveal what the soldiers had confirmed, but not what they themselves had—so that he may provide charming gifts, and so that what has already been confirmed may be annulled, and so that he may rage against his own people, because they will not be able to sustain peace among themselves. Even if he should try to heap benefits upon them, he will be able to stir up enemies for their destruction while they are believed to be comforting. Then they weave treaties with retaliation set forth, if it is expedient. There are again those who, when asked to invade another's principality, roar and rage and brandish harsh words, and secretly hasten to attack with arms the unprepared leader. But after they have entered beyond his borders and perceive him fortified, if now with a shining company, they stop and grow cold in spirit and seek refuges to withdraw their foot from there. But although they perceive their own battle line to stand firm—if the leader from a distance takes care to proclaim to them peaceful messengers laden with gifts and enclosed, they dispute and complain that they have been seduced, and they declaim that the consuls who had hired them for themselves are treacherous, and they weave frauds, whom they also desert and mark for death, and then they attempt to bring back their steps unharmed and to carry back what they have obtained to private soil.
It is rare, but there is one who, once led in, undertakes to attack a dedicated leader with bare chest, and does not desist until he has obtained either death or the palm of victory, or through the disaster of war divides with the other, and, content with shares, they come together into one treaty, each secure to subdue his own. What will the soldiers profit who had provoked this man? If in such a crisis he breathes his last, let the survivor await punishment for them; and if he has escaped in control, then he compels them to the same thing, and no longer does there exist for him an inviolable throne—remembering such things as these had been—unless they are deprived of authority. There are those over whom a prince of a single province presides, who, even if he has injured them only superficially, nevertheless swell with the rumination of excessive pride, and immediately they approach the one to be revered with the triple crown and incite him to take away what things the unequal leader possesses. But if they accomplish this, will they be able to have recourse to their own lands? And although they have accomplished it, finally they will bear appropriate consequences.
The preeminent one will hire for himself in service diverse battle lines of nations and scattered peoples who do not know how to live frugally but to plunder whatever is best, and to add married women to sacred places as if to a brothel. And when they have perceived that the fatherland has been devastated, he is not immediately led across, because it is ineffective to keep phalanxes long in foreign lands, nor is it fitting for so great a leader to remain remote from his own legion, living indeed with those men whose thin faith he greatly fears. He returns to his own soil, which he rejoices to revisit. Whom, if perhaps those who had solemnly led him in propose to lead back down, they choose mournful exile with perpetual confusion of ruin remaining, or rather they are left ignominiously. Meanwhile the leader of the present fatherland, inferior by thousands, either exiles himself deeply or, remaining shut up within the walls of a very strong city, laments. But when he has learned of the departure of the enemy, on the desired day he calls together into battle line those who had been separated, whom clear faith had marked. And he instructs the centurions, and he arranges to confer on each what is owed: joyful things to the faithful, bitter things to the sinister.
Especially upon those who had summoned the tyrant for themselves is imposed fine and penalty, and from that, punishment. Nor is the leader alone filled with hatred, but also the battle lines that had endured insults inflict hatreds; and not only that, but equally they attempt to impoverish their inferiors, until age gradually demolishes all this over the years—and if perhaps death cuts short these men in these matters, it remains that they cannot rejoice in declining age nor in the future. For this reason the vice does not perish. Again others rise up and are turned by the same wheel. But because there are those who neither had been tonsured nor were able to reach even the hem of fine linen because of the brightness of wisdom, and to whom it is nothing if they are carried to the urn in a poor man's bier—so that it may be permitted to them to carry through an unharmed home-going with laden spoils—these men goad me to challenge thus: You mock, wandering astray, priest, if you will have borne to confound these sealed things of the age, if what you bring forth as worthless will have been exercised or poured out, then let even sterile nature abandon its gifts if you will be able to fashion the same status for the summit and for the camp-follower—you who are able to look ahead to one thing, both of the desert and of the river of lamentation. Why do you derogate from the duties of presidents?
Or are you able to obscure the aid which the most powerful one himself had chosen so that he might support the falling world? Do you not acknowledge the angels, or do you not applaud at all him to whom the harmony of virgins gives the trophy of ten thousand enemies? Do you not rather take up this matter than—you who had called back the course by hidden steps to the hundred preliminary offerings, and do you not report concerning the forecourt of the temple of the gathered ones, who also wiped away abominations? And so that now they may enter the New Testament—in the order of Helena's vigorous son with warlike arms, who, by removing the all-combatant, established for Christians the rights of duty. O leader Theodosius, great with so many triumphs, you are not led by the mouth of this man—you who, rejecting heresies, scattered barbarian weapons and, destroying temples, sanctioned that titles be destroyed lest there remain even an underground chamber in which some remnant of the demon's sacred rite might hide, but that all things might serve one author. O led one, do you not consider that the namesake grandson of this man exists sublime, who, tempering coals with coal and again frosts with frost, tamed lust and fury with doctrine and, faithful to charity, was neither sluggish nor falling into sadness, and repeatedly triumphed by supplicating the eternal one more than by arms?
What does the common multitude accomplish? Nor will it itself subsist, each one exhausted without leaders—which the first ages mark. We note this easily, and it is fitting to apply care from this point. Strong men in arms and limbs had grown from the first seeds of Adam, and fertile offspring prepared rich crowds, growing with frequent successes and rare destruction. But an instructed leader was lacking to train the raw cohorts, so that they might avoid the danger of the flooding water. The very people of Abraham, the people of God, was increased with triumphs while it lived humbly under foundations; because, having gone astray, it despised this, it is exiled far from the threshold and exists as a useless lineage. And this is not only its case, but it remains for every earth-born. And why are only those men marked whose mind—which is the chief thing—we confess is with God? There are also tiny birds, and provident ones, which flourish with skill and to whom it is granted to bear perpetual celibacy through the air, which worship the general within themselves and render what is owed to generalship. Whom the others also show to be safe if they are vigorous. On account of whose death also, if it should occur, they again become squalid. But if he departs from his own, all abandon serious pursuits, with whom it displeases them neither to seek out foreign dwelling places.
Indeed, the little people profit under a diligent leader, and without a Cato as guide the well-ordered region is emptied out. Stop now what you have begun and make use of our senses. It pleases me now to skillfully mock those who have been marked as sinful. O you whom it is enough to tear apart with things poured out by your glitterings! To what end? So that you might be able to be even sharers of great men? In whose retaliation—insofar as trifles are rendered—the charm-maker seems to be the lung while he is constricted. You hasten; it does not last to express only panegyrics. Gluttons—as if a club suspends you from a single meal—soon by singing you take care to pursue far off even a light breakfast, and outwardly to pour libations of intoxicating drink, and from that, by circling, to prepare a dish; or if this is lacking, a penny—you rage if prey is removed by absence or if authority is deprived. Drunkenness is not deferred in any case, while the very pregnant little water-carrier repels the bitter burden. The brotherhood which confesses among you is from you alone agricultural—for whom it would now stand to weed so as not to be borne; but it tends toward what the wound in the breast, consuming these things in thin liquid, does not cease from this point to bring forth black bile.
O that you had formerly taken only undergarments rather than now the many-folded sarcophagi! And if by Pegasus it is desired that you be dragged in these up to the twilight chambers, he does not then look at the handle but at pleasant faces, nor does he illuminate the undertakers—for although it would be powerful for you to stain the face of heaven with flowing streams, it is not possible to stain the sense of a man. What, reveling with grim grimaces, do you threaten to nullify the truth, leading rival mouths to bandy-legged ones and to the light babbler, as the moist Aretines cackled? When they have drunk fire, thus drunkenness thirsts in those fools themselves. I reject what they wink at in silence—their chattering I despise and their babbling I consider worthless. My tongue is not a two-penny one flowing away toward lullabies, but if your writings stir up injury, it stands with my pen that our pages be led to seriousness. My work, which not even an obelus will mark, will deserve that no harmful mark adhere to it. It is permitted now also to calm the rumors which you gave to the peoples by blathering—but if afterward they sprout for any vain boasters, then it will be urbane to defend among them what was promised long ago.
Will the magistrates now be able to perceive with keen vision any little flower of the measuring line, even if it exists? But in the manner of caterpillars they seek to gnaw at it, not because there is taste in the mouth but so that they may destroy by devouring—whom I, as gardener, standing at the barriers, take care to exclude. Let them approach the perfume-seller, let them assign eye-salves and let them treat their hallucinations, so that afterward they may distinguish the bright and the dark. I, suffering from lethargy, do not presume to drive away the duties of presidents, but I correct things not to be treated rashly. Because what I have already reported is serious: to climb foolishly to the summit, to strive, and to subjugate a captive fatherland and to deprive another of his throne. This will be monstrous and prey, and if anyone denies it, it is rather better to lead and to drive out the chief than to take away. Or does he bear it more lightly, who is dragged from his own threshold and pressed down in exile on it like a baron, than he who, as a rustic of another, in that place must witness first ones whom he had built up for himself being concentrated, without whose right he is not little bitten from that—or he who, pledged with coins and kept fasts, while all things are torn down, sees his own wife soothed with kisses, and perceives his grandson violently corrupting the marriage of his household, and indeed also mocking their suitors with the hope of clientage having been seduced?
It is difficult for anyone to invade a kingdom if he accuses him whom the cap delights—what is to arise from this? Perhaps meanwhile he will meet and depart to the abyss; but he who gapes to prepare described sorrows for others either ignores hell or fearlessly awaits it. Whom the very sower of all will roll into judgment, so that ignoble he may depart from this age and undergo the punishment of the future. Therefore, the one whom the Almighty wishes to display as worthy of his predictions, or the one whom alone he seeks to promote to the assembly of all nobles, and the one whom honorable paternal love helps to this—it is lawful for him to ascend the vacant throne. But when he stands before it, let him beware that riches not increase for the people, but that his ascent remove all dispensing of justice—if there is any. Those who by such sanction have escaped to the kingdom, perhaps by blessing the fatherland, will be able to obtain a happy final day. The rest, stripped, will pay the penalty of tyranny, which also by holding they have dragged out. The Pandects will be able to preserve these scrolls for you in the school, whose thousand faces, if it pleases you to turn over, will not place an obstacle to my writings, but will itself come to the aid of witnesses. But so that the pen may return to those very ones whom the work-loving cohort took care to mark in the face—before the offspring of Jesse had sweated to change from feet to a staff, until by the prayer sought, the virtue of the mystical anointing might be brought forth on the head by the seer?
I recall that Hezekiah and Josiah came forth as kings from the royal bloodline, with the people favoring them. Was not his father laid to rest in the royal seat, whose mother was Helena? Who, while providence was acting, wished his son to sit beside him. And Theodosius was born of renowned soldiers of Spain, with the help of the first one from the barbarians who rendered the world safe. By whose signs also he first intended to bathe himself for the heights. I say, seeing himself dressed in the ornament with the cloak of empire and bearing the crown placed upon him. By what investigation was he able to recognize the same one, previously unseen by him with no indicator, when at some point he beheld his face? Who, soon promoted by the Emperor Gratian, is made partner—which vision remained with urgent wars, which he calmed in the meantime. And from that point it was fitting that his namesake grandson, walking equal in merits, calmly took his seat on the throne of father and grandfather. If in this pattern the royal throne comes to anyone, guilt will not hold this one now. And if they sit, if he judges himself sufficient for the name of such great majesty—on account of which, however, let him by no means rejoice that he remains secure. Auspices alone, although they exist as worthy, nevertheless do not suffice to provide happiness, but increases are sought.
From this source it pleases me now to set forth teachings fed to the Cynic reasoning. Saul was raised up from a small lineage so that he might protect Israel. From this point he drove back the Philistines at the beginning of his reign and had begun around the circuit to empty out the other enemy nations with easy triumphs. When greed compelled him to gape at forbidden things, soon his rounded arms, growing sluggish, had ceased from battle. His also terrifying mind, not presuming to look upon the battle line of the enemy, drove himself with his own weapon into Etna's punishments. And the glorious prince bore King Manasseh from revived blood; for after God added fifteen years to Hezekiah's life, afterward this one, Manasseh, was born. Who, succeeding to the throne, decided meanwhile to extinguish what his father had caused to burn—and what he himself brought forth as cut under, this one sanctioned to be supplicated, attacking piety and bringing back the orgies of the demon and temples.
Indeed, Licinius, while he served as colleague in the battle line of soldiers, even by lots obtained to entice their minds so that they might urge him with favors to foolishly obtain a share of the empire—under which they might also sing together that they were unpunished in silence. Which had mocked the diligent Augustus so that he himself also might support him, whom he also joined to the seat, not ceasing to yoke his own sister. He had increased himself then for a time with the rough toga, breathing favor toward the orthodox and rushing into the demolition of idolatry. Then he had strangled the Delphic ones and pressed down the tile-makers, and absolutely he abjured the receivers of vain art, and he had restored the followers and spies whom the ancient rulers had enlisted for themselves so that they might pollute treasuries with those same suitable ones. The year would not now have grown bald, having been begun. But when his presence had been authorized through the regions, he played the fool; indeed, he then added to refuse what had been kindly promulgated recently with bloody oppositions. Now if anyone should confess Jesus, first he destined him to the executioners, and from that to the wool-workers. And soon he granted all to revel for Bacchus and at the same time for Cynthius, and for their accomplices, even to lying down.
Moreover, he then raised the sword against Prince Constantine, from whom he had secured his position in the kingdom, and he also stained the kinship relationship of his family. But immediately when these things were reported to him, resisting, she herself removed him from divine power; indeed, she also established purified rights. Indeed, a worthy appearance scarcely sprouts forth, or never by birth. Nor does it exist either from the expected one; indifferently it comes to the perfect, it comes, but immediately it flows down wicked from the chief kind. And from this source is the crime of rushing to scepters, as those things mentioned before resound. But not even right dedication alone will make anyone great, unless honorable he also extends dignity with morals imbued, insofar as he is able to ascend into the eternal kingdom. And these phylacteries are owed to all the crowns of Christ. To each one to preserve a modest life in his own measure. To strive beyond, let him set aside sloth, and let him be one who repeals, and let him not be a comrade to whom the benefits are sufficient that ancestors justly left clear to him, and what master's generosity bestows, with unblemished service compelling, and what collected agriculture pours forth from proper right, and what a skill assigned properly brings forth.
And if perhaps superiors diligently delegate duties, let him fulfill them not displaying pomp here but eternal. Which, if they bring profit, let him repay equally praises and services to the eternal one. And although he may suffer eclipse, let him not, being without part, desert from demanding the same one, nor let him blaspheme in poverty, ignorant by what judgment he has been touched. Can unexpected changes not succeed? For if the vainglorious one will gape, grateful to be auctioned before himself, this one, crushed, will be able to be a hedgehog and to be thrust back frequently. Behold! But why do I, lavished with noise, like an emphatic herald, strive to explain the digested state of the departing age, whose diseases not even eloquent commentary can bear to cast forth? But worse, it once languished while it was sick, and pressed down by filth, redoubling lamentations, it was wasting away with salvation already exhausted. To whom also the first-called stood over to demolish with poisonous bites. But looking back meanwhile, he who had anointed the same one with his palm came to the aid of the worn one, and shook him from the mouth of the same one—whom also, while avoiding, he bit. And making firm again then the loosened and weakened world, he established it to stand.
To whose salvation equally became known what should be avoided and what should be used. Because the transgressor communicates with harmful things and refuses salutary things, from this source it is seen to relapse so many times. But the craftsman himself, physician and author of salvation, places healing for the ungrateful. Now let us pray urgently that he not yet, moved by rage—with antidotes already very often dispensed and purging potions equally strained—withdraw his hand or press surgery, but may he delegate ointments from the highest powers, may he pour in and smear with which he may also repel destructive things, so that when the satiety of surgery delayed by these things comes, he may find little that he presumes to burn or cut away, lest the bride dedicated to Christ's chamber weep for the prior shoots which the mystical inner cistern washed. No one so capable will be able to be skilled in medicine unless he intently examines the physical principle. And lest anyone understand the fashioning except as he who had fashioned it. Who therefore will be able to protect the world with salvation? I will not say a monarch or a portion in it, unless he himself is its potter of the first molding? And if he confesses it to be unfolded, whatever through human form, nevertheless the kindly Spirit remains in that which she ministers from that source—to whom, if she serve purely into the age, from that will come prosperous things, whatever may be done. From crimes from there swiftly he will also flee if she will have milked together. Which, deserted, will immediately perish then with its actions, unless it should educate itself from them and stand as before.
From which source it is fitting that whoever prepares to obtain the name of Augustus should beware this judgment—let him receive what has been brought into the common net. Thus also let him live, having obtained it, lest he profane it. In which let him conduct himself according to divine nods, and let him frustrate his own—let there be illustrious judges who still excel and who already have preceded. From this source also let him place a mirror as testament, which not even the cunning innkeeper, the fraudulent tavern-keeper mixing water with wine, may wash back—which is referred also to false teachers who, by infecting true things with false, are accustomed to serve up harmful teachings. These finally, not according to the truth of the matter or the definition of authorities, but by their own invention, seek to handle sacred Scriptures so that they may more powerfully reconcile grace for themselves and from that the favor of the people. For this reason, constantly devoted, let him implore the Lord and not add to delaying. It is also owed that the entire people should persist in fulfilling this, lest it be seduced by him—since he who foolishly neglects to demand help from his patron struggles to abolish the force of the principal testament. I am ready, indifferent. Pray now; I will now respond to you in Adam's tongue, so that it may be done. And if you prefer, take auspices for me from the highest majesty, so that he may wash away the fault from me with a placid light, whom also he may fashion for himself as pleasing, and may he accept the prayers to be poured forth—which neither may the troublesome enticer be able to make impious, nor to mutilate the polished tongue of one gaping, with that one inspiring who also may not allow my heart to slip away elsewhere, I say easily, but may I deserve to be heard by the pious author of piety.
Trusting in which help, I will then arise to pour forth suppliant words, which may your Hebrew chant confirm with one utterance, and may it be fitted with the final times of his speech.
