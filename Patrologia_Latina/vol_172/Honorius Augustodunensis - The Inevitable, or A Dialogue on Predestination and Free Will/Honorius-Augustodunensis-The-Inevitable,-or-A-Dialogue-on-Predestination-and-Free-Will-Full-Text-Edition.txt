
by Honorius Augustodunensis
Patrologia Latina Volume 


The Inevitable, or A Dialogue on Predestination and Free Will Between Master and Student

Student: The brothers who walk in unity in God's house are praying earnestly for your wellbeing. They offer prayers for your diligence, and they give thanks to Christ, the Key of David, who through your work has unlocked so many mysteries about his Mother in the Song of Songs, because of her merits. For this reason, and for clarifying many other things unknown to most in praise of her, you have made them your debtors.
So now I act as their representative, and they ask with deepest devotion that you untangle for them the inextricable knot of free will. If only the predestined are saved, no matter what they do, as some people claim, then free will seems to be completely eliminated, and those who are damned appear to be punished not only without fault, but even unjustly.
Therefore I ask you to discuss this matter more fully, and not to leave even the smallest difficulty for anyone dull or blind to understanding.
Master: Since the greatest teachers have published many treatises on this subject, why do you ask anything more from me? Compared to them, I am tongue-tied.
Student: They certainly argued brilliantly, but because they seem to hold different opinions, they left us more uncertain. You, however, to tell the truth, make tangible for us briefly what they did not make even visible in their lengthy treatises.
Master: I do not refuse what you ask, because I seek not my own interests but those of charity. I despise verbal ornaments when I am concerned with the simplicity of the brothers, and I also disregard the envious fangs of the jealous, because my help comes from the Lord.
Student: I ask that you bear with me, who am truly foolish, and correct with the word of truth the ignorance of those who inquire through me.
Master: Truth will free those who seek it from the darkness of error and illuminate them with the light of its clarity. For if the cause of charity makes you foolish, wisdom will meet you with the light of truth.
Student: Three knots intertwine with one another in an exceedingly perplexing tangle, which scarcely anyone passes through without getting stuck in them. The first asserts that only the predestined are saved. The second maintains that people attain salvation through grace alone. The third says that free will alone accomplishes salvation.
So first cut through the knot of predestination, which with a certain force binds the hinge of the entire question. For some people think that predestination pertains only to the elect, since the Apostle says in his letter to the Romans, chapter eight, verses twenty-nine and thirty: 'Those whom he predestined, he also called, justified, and glorified.'
Therefore I first beg you to say what predestination is.
Master: Predestination is nothing other than God's eternal preparation of each person, according to their merits, either for glory or for punishment.
There is a twofold predestination: one of the good to glory, the other of the wicked to punishment.
But because some people also grumble about providence and foreknowledge, as if these impose necessity on everyone, it must be understood that providence does not compel anyone to pursue the good, nor does predestination drag anyone by force to the reward of life. Likewise, foreknowledge forces no one to sin, and predestination drives no one by necessity to punishment.
Moreover, those who are foreseen as persevering in good are predestined to glory, while those who are foreknown as ending their lives in evil are certainly preordained to punishment.
Therefore those whom God foreknew would believe in his Son and would willingly keep his commandments, he predestined before the ages. He called them to faith in their own time, justified them through baptism, magnified them with virtues, and glorified them as vessels of mercy.
But those whom he foreknew would not do these things, he rejected from the kingdom of glory and predestined as vessels of wrath and disgrace, prepared for punishment.
Student: Since it is written in the Gospel of John, chapter seventeen, verse twenty-four, 'Father, you loved them before the foundation of the world,' those who were loved before the world's beginning seem to be saved by necessity.
Master: Nothing is future to God. Rather, all things are present to him, both what already exists and what will exist. Therefore all future people were always present in God's sight, and for this reason they were loved before the foundation of the world. But they were chosen for glory in their own time according to their merits.
Therefore those whom God chose by predestining them before the foundation of the world were drawn to glory not by necessity but by their own merit. To them he will say at the end of the world, as recorded in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-five, verse thirty-four: 'Come, blessed of my Father, receive the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of the world.'
And that this kingdom is given to them according to their merits is shown by the divine voice, which immediately follows by recounting their good deeds.
Student: Now that the barrier of predestination has been removed, come, enter the forest of the expected discussion. Unlock what free will is, and how predestination and foreknowledge do not contradict it, and how it harmonizes with grace. Cut down with the sword of reason whatever branches spring up from this.
Master: You tell me what free will is and what you think about it.
Student: It seems to me that free will is called the freedom to choose good or evil.
Master: Although this definition pleases most people, I fear that penetrating reason rejects it.
For choice is only spoken of regarding two or more things, where the option of choosing among many is proposed. But to the angel and to the human being, to whom alone free will is given, only justice alone was proposed, by keeping which they would enjoy eternal blessedness.
Therefore the definition does not seem fitting for free will.
Student: What if it is said that death and life were proposed to the human being, and he chose death?
Master: This is not true. He did not choose death, but being deceived, he desired higher knowledge.
Student: What if it is said that good is proposed by God, evil is urged by the devil, and it is in the human being's free will to choose what he wishes?
Master: Both of these can be said about the human being, but not about the angel, since there was no one who persuaded him of anything evil. Moreover, there is no middle ground between good and evil. But if the human being seemed to be placed in some middle position, then perhaps rightly the freedom to choose good or evil would be said to exist in him. But since he is established only in the good, and the choice of evil is nothing other than a certain falling away of the soul from the good—namely when it abandons the justice it ought to preserve and declines toward injustice, which it knows it should avoid.
Student: But I greatly await what its definition is.
Master: Free judgment is the determination of one's own will regarding what it wishes and what it does not wish. This is called free because it is not necessary, but in those who have reason, there also exists the freedom of willing and not willing.
And because free will is given solely for preserving justice, in which the salvation of the soul consists, its definition is considered fitting in this way.
The freedom of choice is the power to preserve the rightness of the will for the sake of that rightness itself. For the right will is to will what God wills. God wills that the rational creature be subject to him not by compulsion but voluntarily, so that it may be well with the creature. When the creature also wills this, then its will is right.
The will is free to preserve this rightness, constrained by no necessity. The rightness of the will is justice. Therefore if it loves God, who is justice and supreme blessedness, not for any other reason than for the sake of justice itself, it will have its reward.
Therefore the most fitting definition of free will is this: the power to preserve the rightness of the will for the sake of that rightness itself, through which one rightfully possesses eternal blessedness.
Student: But how does one now have free will for preserving justice when one does not have justice?
Master: Just as someone has sight for seeing the sun when it is absent, which he certainly can see if it is present, so the human being naturally has free will for preserving justice, even if it is absent, which he can preserve if it is present.
But this justice which he does not possess, he cannot possess by himself, because no one except God has what he has not received, as stated in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter four, verse seven. If it is given to him, it is the grace of the one who says in the Gospel of John, chapter fifteen, verse five: 'Without me you can do nothing.' Not to abandon the justice one has received is a matter of free will. To preserve this justice is merit.
Therefore, as stated in Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter nine, verse sixteen, it does not depend on the one who wills—understand, what he wills—nor on the one who runs—understand, what he runs—but on God who shows mercy. God's grace goes before him without any preceding merits, but freely, by giving him the ability to be just, and follows him so that he may run in the stadium of justice, so that he may receive or obtain the prize of life.
Just as if a poor naked person is clothed by a rich person, it is not his doing that he is clothed, but the doing of the one who gives the clothing. But it is in his own choice whether he wears the garment or takes it off. So it is with prevenient grace and accompanying free will.
Student: Since the Apostle says in his letter to the Romans, chapter nine, verses eleven through thirteen: 'Before they were born or had done anything good or evil, so that God's purpose according to election might stand, not from works but from the one who calls, it was said, The older will serve the younger, as it is written, Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated'—some people seem to be saved or damned not according to merit but according to God's purpose, or God seems to punish some people's deeds before they commit them. For on account of what merit did Jacob deserve to be loved, when he did not yet know how to do good? And for what guilt was Esau held in hatred, when he had not yet done anything evil?
Master: As was said earlier, nothing is future to God. Sacred Scripture testifies that Jacob was upright and Esau profane. And what they were going to become in their own time through free will, this they already were in the Lord's sight before they were born.
Therefore because Jacob was upright in the Lord's sight, the Lord loved him. But because Esau was profane, he held him in hatred.
He used the past tense 'I loved' because of eternity, for just as the past is always past, so everything that eternity foresees as going to happen is immutable.
Moreover, because there is no injustice with God, as Romans chapter nine, verse fourteen states, he punishes no one without preceding injustice, but mercifully forgives many sins that have been committed.
Therefore, although it is necessary that everything happen which God has purposed, foreknown, and predestined to happen, nevertheless purpose, foreknowledge, and predestination impose no force of necessity on anyone. Rather, each person does what he wills by free will.
And those who through free will turn away from evil and do good, these are his people and the sheep of his pasture, as stated in Psalm seventy-eight, verse thirteen. For these he prepared the pastures of life, and these he chose in Christ before the foundation of the world, as Ephesians chapter one, verse four declares.
See Anselm and various places in his book. But those who abandon their freedom, and with no necessity compelling them, but willingly submit themselves to slavery, having become servants of sin, as the Gospel of John, chapter eight, verse thirty-four states, continually serve the demons who preside over vices. These are the people of Pharaoh, and they are not among the sheep of the Lord, but are sheep to be placed in hell, whom death will devour, as Psalm forty-eight, verse fifteen declares. Such people, having heard so many teachings, seen so many signs, suffered so many plagues, remain incorrigible, and the Lord rejects them when he justly abandons them in their injustice.
Therefore God's Predestination and Foreknowledge remain unchangeable and fixed, because all things that God foreknows and predestines to happen will happen without deviation. But grace and free will work together in harmonious covenant for the salvation of humanity. Yet man, failing in his freedom by his will alone, inclines toward sin.
Student: Since you have elegantly untied the knot of free will through the help of grace, they ask that you explain whether the devil was created for blessedness.
Master: It is established that every rational creature was created for blessedness. But the devil is a rational creature; therefore he was created for blessedness. For it is written of him in the book of Ezekiel, chapter twenty-eight, verses twelve and thirteen: You were full of wisdom and perfect in beauty, you were in the delights of God's paradise.
Student: And what about what the Lord says of him, that he did not stand in the truth, as the Gospel of John, chapter eight, verse forty-four records?
Master: That is, he did not remain in the rightness of his will. For he willingly abandoned justice, and therefore justly lost his blessedness.
Student: What is blessedness?
Master: The sufficiency of all good things, without any need whatsoever.
Student: Why didn't God make him such that he could not lose this?
Master: If God had made him that way, he would have been subjected to necessity like a beast. But God made him just, blessed, and free. Just, so that he would want what God wanted; blessed, so that he would always enjoy God; free, so that he would voluntarily want justice, and by this merit would rightfully always be blessed.
Student: And why did the devil fall from this glory?
Master: Because he desired what he should not have, and what God did not will, and he wanted to be like the Most High, as Isaiah chapter fourteen, verse fourteen states.
Student: In what way similar?
Master: He wanted to be called God, and he wanted to be worshiped by the angels as God.
Student: Since he is not located in a place, being incorporeal as an angel, from where and how did he fall?
Master: Just as someone standing in the light of the sun, if he suddenly lost his sight, would be said to have fallen into darkness, even though he has not changed location, especially since he was not in any particular place.
Student: Since God was completely blessed in all things and always sufficient to himself, what was the reason that he wanted to create anything?
Master: Because of his goodness he created all things, so that they might exist, into which the abundance of his goodness might flow, as the book of Proverbs, chapter sixteen, verse four declares: The Lord has made all things for himself, even the wicked for the day of evil. For every creature in some way enjoys the goodness of God the Creator. Some in that they exist; some in that they live; some in that they feel; some in that they understand. Therefore each thing is good, and all things considered together are very good, as Ecclesiasticus chapter thirty-nine, verse twenty-one says: All the works of the Lord are very good.
But he created angelic and human nature to see him. And so that they might have some proper merit with him which he ought to reward, he gave them justice. And so that they might have full joy, he added free will, by which they would keep the justice given to them not by compulsion but freely, and thus would always reign well with him.
Student: And if they were created for blessedness, why do so many perish?
Master: Because through free will they fall away from the good and submit themselves to sin. They are justly deprived of the glory of blessedness and handed over to punishments.
Student: And how is it true that God wills all people to be saved, as Paul's first letter to Timothy, chapter two, verse four states?
Master: God created rational creatures for glory. But angels and humans are rational creatures; therefore all were created for glory. And that he created them for glory, he did this either willingly or unwillingly.
Student: He could not have done so unwillingly.
Master: Therefore he did it willingly.
Student: That follows.
Master: And what God wills once, he always wills. For his will is unchangeable. Therefore he always wills all to be saved.
Student: And if he wills this, why are not all saved? For who can resist his will, as Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter nine, verse nineteen asks?
Master: The perdition of those who perish does not proceed from the will of God, because he does not will the death of the sinner, as Ezekiel chapter eighteen, verse thirty-two declares, and he who did not make death does not rejoice in the perdition of the dying, as the book of Wisdom chapter one, verse thirteen says: Nor does he rejoice in the perdition of the living. Rather it comes from free will, by which they willingly abandon justice, which is the covenant of blessedness. Having abandoned this, they lose blessedness, and thus will always be wretched.
Student: How does this come from free will?
Master: The angel, with no predestination and no necessity drawing him, but by free will alone, abandoned justice when he wanted to be like God, as Isaiah chapter fourteen, verse fourteen recounts. And because by his own will he broke the bond of blessedness, which was justice, he justly fell into misery having lost blessedness, from which, as justice dictates, he never merited to be rescued.
The principle of justice demanded this. For he who fell with no one pushing him ought justly to be lifted by no one.
Student: What about man?
Master: Man similarly, forced by no power, compelled by no necessity, but by free will abandoned justice and submitted himself to injustice when he desired to be like God.
Student: Why did this fallen one merit to be raised up?
Master: His restoration was grace, but the principle of justice also demanded that this happen. For he who fell because another pushed him ought justly to be lifted by another.
Student: I think it was not fitting for God to give them free will, through which he foreknew they would sin.
Master: That's like saying: He ought not to have given them knowledge, which he foreknew they would abuse. If he had not given them knowledge, how would they differ from brute animals? If he had not given them free will, they would be subjected to necessity in all things like beasts, and would have no merit before God.
Therefore it was necessary that he give them knowledge by which they would recognize the Creator, and that free will also be added, by which they would freely keep justice, through which they would always be blessed.
Student: If God foreknows all things, then surely he foreknew that they would act wickedly and through this would suffer eternal punishment. Why then did he want to create those who were going to commit so many and such great crimes and misdeeds, and for these would bear eternal punishments?
Master: God foreknew all things, and this befits his eternity. He knows nothing as future, as if it were yet to be done, nor anything as past, as if it were finished. Rather, he beholds all things to be done and all things done as present with unchangeable vision.
But that he created the angel or man, each of whom he foreknew would act wickedly, this the principle of justice and power demanded. Power, because he foreknew that their malice could utterly harm him not at all, and how many good things he planned to make from their evils in the universe. Justice, because it was just that God should do everything that would be useful for something in the universe. And the wise do not fail to see how useful and how necessary demons or evil men are in God's commonwealth.
Therefore he gave justice to those he created, which he foreknew they would not keep at all. Indeed, he created them just, because it befits his goodness to do nothing except what is just and right. To them also he gave free will, that is, he made them masters of their own will, because it befits his perfection to accomplish nothing except what is perfect. For they would not be perfect if they were not free. And if they were not free, they would be subjected to necessity like beasts. Therefore he did everything as he willed and as he ought to have done.
Student: Why did God not prohibit them from evil when he could have? If he willed it and could not, why was he powerless? But if he could and would not, why did he will them to sin and consequently to suffer punishments? And since he permitted them to sin, he surely consented to their sins. For if any prince foreknows that some subject wants to kill him, and does not prohibit it when he can, he surely consents to the homicide when he permits it to happen. But if he permits it willingly, he shows himself powerless.
Master: That God did not prohibit them from evil, this the principle of immutability forbade. For God created them for full joy, but they would not have full joy if they were not free. Therefore, so that they might have full joy, he gave them free will. Indeed, he created them free, that is, masters of their own will, so that they might freely delight in happiness, and not enjoy happiness forced by necessity or compelled by violence.
But the freedom which he once gave them, the immutable God ought not to have taken away, nor could he have. For if he had taken it away, then he would not want them to want justice, which is impossible, since he willed them to want justice. For to sin is nothing other than to not want justice. And therefore evil or sin is nothing by substance, just like injustice. But injustice is nothing other than where justice is not, just as silence is where there is no voice, just as darkness is where there is no light. Therefore to do evil is nothing other than to fail from a just will. And God would in some way have taken away their freedom if, restrained by him, they could not sin. And if they had kept justice by compulsion, they would not have had the merit of justice.
Student: What? Can't the good angels sin?
Master: This is not from the necessity of impotence, but proceeds from the will of great power. For it is great power to always want what one ought and what befits, for example to always be healthy and wise. But it is maximum impotence, indeed madness, to want what one ought not and what does not befit, such as wanting to kill oneself. For when other angels declined toward injustice, the good angels themselves kept justice by free will, and for this merit they were immediately so confirmed in this will that they no longer want evil. And therefore it is said that they cannot.
Student: But as was said, God seems to have consented to their sin when he permitted them to sin, or rather to have created them for punishments when he did not prohibit them from sin.
Master: When truth is consulted, it shows something quite different. Although the ways of the Lord are as far removed from the ways of men as the heavens are exalted above the earth, as Isaiah chapter fifty-five, verse eight declares—For my thoughts are not your thoughts, nor are your ways my ways, says the Lord. Because as the heavens are exalted above the earth, so are my ways exalted above your ways—nevertheless this matter does not differ greatly from our thoughts, which clear reasoning thus illuminates for us.
It does not always follow that whoever permits something to happen consents to its happening, if he could prohibit it. For when someone permits thorns to grow in his field, though he could prohibit it, he nevertheless does not consent, nor does he allow them to grow for the fortification of a hedge or for fuel for fires, though they are suitable for both. So God, when he permitted them to sin, nevertheless did not consent to sin, nor did he create them for punishments. However, after their sins he handed them over to punishments. But those who by free will cling to him who is supreme blessedness, he crowned with glory and honor, as Psalm eight, verse six declares. But those who by free will depart from him who is eternal justice, he punished with fitting torments. And by their harshness, as with thorns for a hedge, he strengthens the glory of the former, and by their burning he illuminated his entire work.
Student: Why did God create those whom he foreknew would not keep justice at all and therefore would lose glory, and not only those whom he foreknew would remain in glory for keeping justice?
Master: This is like complaining why God permitted night to exist, since it is dark, as if day were useless and unnecessary, when rather night itself is the rest of laborers and the restoration of strength. So good is commended through evil. For the just compared to the unjust appear more glorious, just as day compared to night shines more graciously. Therefore the principle of good demanded that God permit evil to exist, through which, by reciprocal measures, through its contraries it might harmonize in all things. For although evil is nothing by substance, and therefore brings no dissonance to God's eternal disposition, nevertheless substance through the very fact that it exists makes all substances appear good, and it becomes known how good it is to cling to the supreme good.
Student: It seems very amazing about the goodness of God that he wanted to create something that would be subjected to eternal pains and eternal torments, when it would seem much better for something not to exist than to exist miserably, or to be an insensible stone than to endure eternal pains.
Master: It seemed better to wise God that what is both exist, than not to exist at all. And therefore he made to exist what exists. But that he did not make all things equal, but unequal, through this both the ineffable wisdom of the craftsman is known, and the inestimably adorned fullness of the work. And although God made punishments, he nevertheless created nothing for punishments. Otherwise insensible creation would be superior to sensible creation, which would feel the pain of punishment without end.
Furthermore, when rational creation willingly cast off the justice it possessed, then it lost the glory set before it, and thus fell into misery. Misery is to grieve over what was lost, and not to be able to have what one desires, and to be afflicted by the things in which one exists, and not to be able to escape these things. But to be tormented by punishments is nothing other than to suffer something contrary to oneself against one's will.
But for the beauty of the universe, God created two and two things, as Ecclesiasticus chapter thirty-four, verse fifteen says: Against evil is good, and against death is life, so also against the just person is the sinner. And thus contemplate all the works of the Most High, two and two and one against one. He created things contrary to each other, like water against fire and earth against air. And other things created in these suffer pain from contraries, just as things formed in water or earth are tormented in fire. For the salamander lives in flame and dies in water, while the fish nourished in water dies in fire.
Therefore for rational nature, to feel the bitterness of pains is to not have the sweetness of blessedness. For the principle of justice demands that those who willingly stripped themselves of the blessedness of glory should always be in the bitterness of misery. And that this should be without end, this is the reason: that they were created immortal.
Student: Since it was arranged by God from eternity that his Son would be incarnated, it seems man necessarily sinned so that God's purpose would be fulfilled. For if man had not sinned, the Son of God would not have been incarnated, and thus God's decree would be made void. Therefore both seem to depend on necessity: that man sinned and that the Son was incarnated.
Master: God foreknew from eternity man's fall, and therefore from eternity he purposed that his Son would be incarnated for his redemption. But man sinned by no necessity, but by free will alone, and therefore the Lord imputed sin to him. For if he had sinned by necessity, he would not be guilty, since he would be doing what he could not avoid.
But because the Lord forewarned him about sin and set before him the punishment of death, and he despised the divine warnings, for this reason he became guilty. But that the Son of God was incarnated, or immolated for man, as far as he himself was concerned, was will alone; as far as man was concerned, it was absolute necessity. For unless the Son became incarnate, man would never be saved. Therefore it was necessary for man that he will to become incarnate.
Student: Why was not an angel or a man sent to redeem man, but the Son of God?
Master: The principle of justice demanded this. For an angel ought not to have been sent for man, because man pertained nothing to angelic nature. But man ought not to have been sent, because every man was a sinner, and a sinner could not redeem a sinner. Therefore it was necessary that he be sent who alone without sin could reconcile man to God, as Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter three, verse ten says: Because there is no one just. And Romans chapter five, verse twelve: As through one man sin entered into this world and through sin death, and so death passed to all men in whom all sinned.
Student: Why was neither the Father nor the Holy Spirit, but the Son alone incarnated?
Master: Justice demanded this. For he is God in a second place after the Father, not in dignity but in order. For he is equal to God the Father, that is, one with him. From this he is also called the likeness of God. But the devil usurped this privilege for himself when he wanted to be like the Most High, namely to be called God and to be adored by angels as God. And because he did not obtain this from angels in heaven, in the world he taught men to worship him as God and to adore him as God. Therefore it was necessary that he to whom the injury was done should come, and show himself to be the true God and the devil to be a deceiver.
Student: And why did he come as man?
Master: So that he, an innocent man, might make satisfaction to God the Father for guilty man, as the letter to the Hebrews, chapter one, verse thirty-three states, and through man might restrain the tyrant, and rescue man who had been deceived by him.
Student: Through what did the devil lose man whom he once possessed?
Master: Through God's justice and power. For as one blind, he gravely offended the power of God, because now he not only made himself equal to God but exalted himself above God, as Isaiah chapter fourteen, verse fourteen records, when he commanded that he be worshiped by the Son of God, coequal with the Father, in the temptation. But he gravely incurred offense against God's justice in the passion, when he killed a just and innocent man.
Therefore through justice he experienced power. For when a just man is unjustly killed, the unjust tyrant is justly oppressed, and deceived man, unjustly oppressed, is redeemed through justice and established through power above the angel whom he worshiped as God. From this, favorable circumstances are still called secondary, because through justice one arrives at eternal power.
Student: Is there free will in infants?
Master: Free will is naturally present in them, but like fire hidden in flint, it accomplishes nothing in them.
Student: Why are not all children saved through redemption, in whom free will as yet accomplishes nothing?
Master: This proceeds from the principle of justice, because nothing is owed to man except punishment.
Student: And why are very many saved in whom free will equally accomplishes nothing?
Master: But this descends from grace, which also bestows its gifts on the undeserving. If the people of some city offended the king, he would justly punish some and mercifully absolve others. So when the whole world offended God, he exacted punishment through justice on some, but forgave others through mercy.
Student: From what source is it that now many who possess free will are saved, and very many are not saved?
Master: The reason is the same as was stated. Those who are saved are saved from grace and free will, and from the gift of God, and not from their own merit. For it is from God's grace that, being preceded by God, they want the good; it is from free will that they do not abandon the good. Perseverance in this good is attributed both to God's gift and to man's merit, for which he will be rewarded with the prize of life. For whoever perseveres to the end will be saved, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-four, verse thirteen declares.
Student: From what source is it that some who have grown old in evils are finally saved?
Master: From grace and free will. For from prevenient grace, good will is divinely inspired as a gift; from free will they cooperate with what is offered to them, and for this merit they are saved.
These two are together like soul and body. For through grace, free will is vivified, as body through soul. But free will cooperates with grace as body with soul. Grace without free will is powerful in itself to save; free will without grace is powerless to work good.
Therefore through grace, free will accomplishes much, and its perseverance is crowned first. It is grace that, through many years, while from childhood to decrepit old age they rage insatiably in wickedness, at the very end of life they are snatched through repentance from the very jaws of the devil and led to enjoy the pleasantness of paradise.
Student: And why are others not saved?
Master: This is known to come from justice and free will. From justice, because they are not softened by grace unto good, but are permitted to be hardened, although often the fountain of grace flows over them and grants them repentance for a time, which they immediately cast off and return like dogs to their vomit, as the book of Proverbs, chapter twenty-six, verse eleven, and Peter's second letter, chapter two, verse twenty-two record.
From free will, because they refuse to receive the justice offered to them, and choose iniquity instead. For those mentioned above, the word of God is a cause of salvation and the way to life; but for these others, it is a cause of death and testimony to destruction. The former are vessels of mercy, prepared for honor; the latter are vessels of wrath, as Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter nine, verse twenty-two states, prepared for dishonor. Of the former, no one will be able to perish; of the latter, no one will be saved. For the former, a kingdom is prepared from the foundation of the world; for the latter, a furnace of fire with the devil and his angels, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-five, verse thirty-four declares.
From this variety it happens that many pass their lives in monasteries under great strictness, and very many dwelling in the desert lead a solitary life with supreme abstinence, yet in the end descend to the depths of the abyss.
Student: This is stupefying beyond all wonder.
Master: Receive the causes of each. Those who perish in monasteries trust in their own prudence, despise the warnings of their superiors, and die in disobedience. But those who perish in the desert live without discretion, when they choose for themselves to follow the examples of the saints while scorning and rejecting their father's precepts. Of these it is written in the book of Proverbs, chapters fourteen, verse twelve, and fifteen, verse twenty-five: There are ways that seem just to men, whose end leads to destruction.
Student: Why are some saved and some reproved when they are equal in the same fault?
Master: From this the incomprehensible judgments of God are proclaimed, as Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter eleven, verse thirty-three says: O the depth of riches. He is affirmed to be terrible in his counsels over the sons of men, as Psalm sixty-five, verse five declares. He chooses through mercy whom he wills, and reproves through justice whom he wills, to whom no one can say: Why do you do this? All his ways are mercy and truth, as Psalm twenty-four, verse ten states.
Student: Is salvation attributed more to grace or to free will?
Master: The summit of salvation is attributed to God's grace, to which also merits are ascribed, because no one can come to the Son, who is eternal life, unless the Father through grace, that is the Holy Spirit, draws him, as the Gospel of John, chapter six, verse forty-four declares. For without him we can do nothing, as the Gospel of John, chapter fifteen, verse five states. For God works in us both the willing and the doing for good will, as Paul's letter to the Philippians, chapter two, verse thirteen declares.
Student: If God works, what reward is imputed to man? Master: Both God works and the elect cooperate. God works in his elect, by his grace going before them to will and following to enable. They cooperate through free will by consenting with good will.
This good will is rewarded in them, as it is written in the Gospel of John, chapter one, verse sixteen: We have received grace for grace. We receive grace when God goes before us so that we may will, and follows so that we may be able. For this grace he will give that grace when he will reward us in glory.
Student: Some are accustomed to say that the predestined are necessarily saved. If this is so, why do they labor at all? Or why is it preached to the gentiles that they should convert? Or why is the word of admonition administered daily by priests to the people so that they may advance in service to God's purpose?
Master: God's predestination violently saves or damns no one. Those once predestined to the kingdom for their merits are indeed necessarily saved when from justice the proposed glory is recompensed to their merits. But because no one presumes himself predestined—since not even Paul, the vessel of election, presumed this of himself—it is necessary to apply oneself to labor with all effort, by which they may be able to obtain predestination.
For through labor, predestination of life is obtained, as it is said in sacred authority: Through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God, as the Acts of the Apostles, chapter thirteen, verse twenty-one declares. Therefore to infants through the bitterness of death, to the young through the exercise of work, to those doing penance in their final moments, predestination is given through examination by the torments of purgatory.
But to the gentiles it is preached because the predestined who are outside, standing idle in the marketplace, are called into the Lord's vineyard for labor, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter ten, verse six indicates. To whom also a denarius is proposed as reward, because the predestined glory will be given to those laboring to the end.
Often also some of the reprobate enter the vineyard with them, but in the beginning of labor they fail from the work, and either leave the vineyard blaspheming, or remaining in the vineyard, they impede those laboring. Of these it is said in John's first letter, chapter two, verse nineteen: They went out from us, but they were not of us. And again in Psalm thirty-nine, verse six: They have multiplied beyond number—namely beyond the number of those predestined to life.
Many indeed clung through faith to Peter's net, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter thirteen, verse forty-seven describes, but because they were not foreknown in the number of the elect, they sprang back into the waves of vices when the net broke.
But the people of the faithful, wearied in the labor of pious work, are lifted up by the word of admonition lest they fail, just as a sick person going to church, but failing on the way, is sustained by someone so that he may complete the journey.
For the word of God is the true seed, as the Gospel of Luke, chapter eight, verse eleven declares; but the field is the hearts of men. When this seed is scattered by God's farmers, the seed is God's grace. But if it is received by the agriculture, it is free will. And this, received through faith, if it sends forth the sprout of good works, the harvest will be in predestined glory.
Student: Since clear reason brings forth all these things from the light of truth, they ought to please all the studious greatly in all respects. But I am much moved that angelic nature could fall if it was created for blessedness, for it seems that some force impelled it, that it fell so irrecoverably. For I am exceedingly moved about the fall of man, that he could descend from such great glory into such great misery if no necessity impelled him. Therefore I beg that you pour out your heart and bring forth the secret of truth.
Master: I will briefly lay open to you the hidden things of God, and listen carefully with the opened heart.
Before God created the world, he foreknew both the fall of angel and of man. He permitted this to happen because he judged that good should be illuminated by evil.
He also foreknew who and how many would remain with him, who and how many would depart, who and how many would return to him. For if he had not known this, he would not be foreknowing of future things. And if there were not a fixed number of the elect, then the kingdom of God would consist not by ordered disposition but by random chance, to which a crowd of uncertain number would flow together. But since with God there is a fixed number of hairs, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter ten, verse thirty, and Luke, chapter twenty-two, verse seven declare: All the hairs of your head are numbered, much more is there with God a predetermined number of the elect.
But however many are inscribed in this number by God, these were chosen for blessedness before the foundation of the world. Of these, no one will perish, but each one will hasten with all effort to predestined glory. But those who are multiplied beyond this number are not numbered among the sheep of Christ.
And because this number was diminished by the angels falling, it was restored by humans being born. And therefore just as we begin to count from one, so from one man the number was begun. And as the number grows to perfection, so human offspring succeeded until the completion of the elect.
Therefore this sacred number, known to God alone and predetermined by him with eternal certainty, is like the boundary of a certain city, within which all must be contained, whose citizens of this city have been foreknown from eternity. But the law of the city is inviolable justice instituted by the most just king, and the keepers of this are called just, and these alone are numbered among the citizens.
The privilege of this sacred city is freedom. Its kingdom is blessedness. Therefore all the inhabitants of this city are just and were free, but they did not want to keep the law of this city. And therefore they could not be numbered among its citizens. But they aspired to dominion over it, while each desired to be as God in it.
Therefore no force, no necessity expelled them from this city, but the law of justice forbade violators of the law to be citizens in it.
Student: O how high a watchtower you have led me to, in which I see very many things! But because some things are still closed, I demand that they be opened to me.
If man falls through free will, why does he not rise again through free will?
Master: Man can throw himself from a high mountain into a deep precipice by himself, but he cannot return unless helped by another. So through free will, someone can abandon justice, but not by himself, but only through grace alone can he receive it back. For by whom someone is conquered, of that one he also becomes a servant, as Peter's second letter, chapter two, verse nineteen states: By whom someone is overcome, of that one he is also a servant.
For he who willingly addicts himself to luxury or to any vice over which it presides can no longer through free will shake off the yoke of its domination, to whose dominion he freely chose to subject himself, unless God's grace goes before him so that he desires the good which he scorned, and follows so that he may prevail to fulfill it.
Student: Since man's will was made free, why is it so prone to evil and so sluggish toward good?
Master: Every rational will naturally desires the supreme good, but deceived by error it slips into the false, which is evil. For everyone wants to be happy, and therefore each person most greatly desires that in which he hopes to be happy. But because the supreme good, or blessedness, consists in God alone, whoever desires something placed below God as the supreme good errs.
But the cause of this error is this: God created man just and blessed, without any need, in the sufficiency of all good things, as Ecclesiastes chapter seven, verse thirty declares: I found that God made man upright, and he himself mixed infinite questions. And God gave free will of justice and blessedness, so that by the will of justice he would rule over the subjected body, and by the will of blessedness he would obey God. Therefore he had justice for God's honor, and blessedness for his own advantage. And if, having kept justice, he had honored God, he would have attained to the supreme blessedness of the angels.
But because he abandoned justice, he lost blessedness. Yet he retained the will for blessedness. Therefore, because he burns with desire for advantages, but cannot have the advantages befitting rational nature which he lost, he turns to false and brutish advantages and bestial appetites. And because he was made like senseless beasts, as Psalm forty-eight, verse thirteen declares: Man when he was in honor did not understand; he was compared to senseless beasts and made like them—therefore through concupiscence he mates like a beast, gives birth with pain, nurses infants with milk like a beast nurses cubs, and desires only the advantages of the body.
Student: Why are beasts not damned for these things?
Master: Appetites in brute animals are not sin but are natural, but in humans they are irrational. For other living creatures are driven by necessity; man is governed by freedom. From this it is written: Whoever does this, which man ought not to do, let him be removed from among men.
Student: What is it that some strive with immense effort to live well and accomplish nothing? Or after great progress they fall from their purpose? Does not some hidden force draw them back so that they cannot complete the work they began?
Master: No necessity draws them back, but will alone turns them away, when they want more what temptation suggests to them than what reason persuades. Some also presume more on their own will than on grace, and attribute all things to their own merits. Therefore, with grace deserting them, they justly lose their labor.
Student: Why does God make man's will so changeable?
Master: For his great advantage. For what can be turned to evil can again be turned to good. If man once fallen were not changeable, he would never be convertible to good.
Student: Why did God not make him so that he would be unchangeable in good?
Master: Then he would be equal to God; for this belongs to God alone.
Student: If each person works by free will, how does God work all things in all?
Master: All good things that happen, whether in heaven or on earth or in every creature, God alone who is good works through the elect, whether angels or men, with them cooperating by consenting through free will.
God works through the elect, as he converted the gentiles through the apostles, he himself inspiring within and giving increase. They cooperate through external ministry, planting and watering.
But if the elect have done anything contrary, as David did with Uriah in the second book of Kings, chapter eleven, verse one, God justly permits this to happen, which nevertheless he himself converts to his praise, since after the fall they became more humble and render more abundant thanks to him. For them even sins themselves cooperate unto good, as Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter eight, verse twenty-eight states: For those who love God, all things cooperate unto good. Therefore all things are from him and through him, as Romans chapter eleven, verse thirty-six declares.
Through the reprobate also God works when he exercises his judgment through them, just as through the Chaldeans he destroyed Jerusalem, as Jeremiah chapter fifty-two, verse twenty-four records. Behold, one and the same work God and the Chaldeans accomplished in different ways, and yet he is praised for it and they are condemned. And what he did through justice, they did through cruelty.
Similarly through Judas, God handed over his Son, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-six, verse forty-eight records, and redeemed the world from death. Behold, God and Judas accomplished one work with different minds. But while the whole world venerates God for this and detests Judas, it is because what God did for love of the human race, Judas did through greed.
I boldly say that God even works through the devil, in that what pertains to justice is determined to be exercised through him in the reprobate. But what God does through most just equity, the devil does through most wicked cruelty. Yet he cannot rage against them more than is permitted. From this God is praiseworthily magnified, but the devil is damnably reproved for the same thing.
But whatever is committed by the reprobate through free will against what is established is indeed permitted by God to happen and is turned to his praise, when they are justly subjected by him to punishments. Therefore God works all things, either by favoring or by permitting.
And because from him, as is read in the book of Wisdom—from God come both good and evil, as Ecclesiasticus chapter eleven, verse fourteen says: Good and evil, life and death, poverty and honor are from God—all things are good, and nothing is evil except what is bitter to those who suffer something harsh, and that is called evil. And therefore all things are to the praise and glory of God, who has mercy on whom he wills, as Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter nine, verse eighteen declares: On whom he wills he has mercy, and whom he wills he hardens, bestowing grace and hardening whom he wills by leaving them in malice.
Student: Have these things never been heard in the world?
Master: Indeed, they are heard daily in the Scriptures, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter eighteen, verse eighteen says: Whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven. And verse nineteen: Because if two of you consent on earth about any matter whatever they ask, it will be done for them by my Father who is in heaven. And chapter twenty-eight, verse nineteen: Go and teach all nations. But they are not attended to by the lazy and negligent, and therefore are not understood. But when they hear these things from the learned in disputation, they are astonished as if awakened from sleep. They doubt they are true because they think they are written nowhere. But by the studious they are read, heard, attended to, examined, and understood, and brought back to memory.
For the Bridegroom Christ, departing, left to his bride the Church the keys of knowledge, by which daily, to the sons who knock, he opens the secrets of God by unlocking understanding. But from dogs and from swine he closes the pearls lest they be defiled.
Student: All things which you propose, proving with such strong testimonies and concluding by inference, demonstrate that he errs more than a blind man who has presumed to attack these things obstinately.
But I ask, explain: Do prosperity and adversity come about through free will?
Master: Rather through God's grace and justice. God's grace indeed dispenses prosperity, but justice dispenses adversity. But the just are provoked through prosperity to eternal goods, while the unjust are rewarded through prosperity, like that rich man in the Gospel of Luke, chapter sixteen, verse twenty-five: Son, you received good things in your life and Lazarus likewise evil things; but now he is consoled here, but you are tormented. But adversity touches the elect for three reasons. First, so that some may be corrected from sins through adversity, like David; second, so that some who are tested may be crowned more, like Job; third, so that some may be drawn back from the delight of sin, like Paul.
But they strike the reprobate for two reasons. First, so that the elect may be corrected through their plagues, as is read in the destruction of Korah; second, so that they themselves may be restrained from malice lest they harm as much as they wish, as is written about Antiochus and Herod.
Student: You have dug through the wall, and behold a door appears. Since Scripture says that Pharaoh was hardened by God, as the book of Exodus, chapter nine, verse sixteen declares: For this purpose I have raised you up, that I may show my strength in you and that my name may be declared in all the earth. And Romans chapter nine, verse seventeen: Because for this very thing I raised you up, that I might show in you my power, and that my name might be declared in all the earth—indeed, he was established for this very purpose, that God's name might be declared in all the earth through him. Who does not see that, driven by necessity, he afflicted the people, since he was established for this very purpose and was submerged as if drawn by some fate?
Master: Note three things: The people afflicted, Pharaoh afflicting, God liberating.
God tests the elect, like golden vessels through the reprobate, in the furnace of tribulation by trying them. But the reprobate themselves do not intend that their vexation might profit those for salvation whom the malice of cruelty alone afflicts.
Furthermore, the devil, who freely abandoned justice, God permitted to be hardened in injustice like the anvil of hammers, and established him as a smith for purifying the vessels of mercy. This smith made all the impious his instruments, of whom Pharaoh was one. When through free will he refused to serve God, God hardened him by not freeing him from malice and hardness.
And when God's people were to be tested through affliction and proved through adversity, God for this very purpose established Pharaoh to test the people, when he offered himself through free will—as one of the devil's hammers, so to speak—to pound the vessels of God. And so Pharaoh, unknowing, served the just like a servant serves sons, while by separating them from the vessels of wrath, he instructed them with scourges.
The devil and the impious indeed want evil in themselves, but not as much as they want; rather, only as much as they are permitted can they do. And when they are permitted to prevail over God's elect, they are said to be established for this very purpose. On this matter and manner of speaking, Saint Gregory's Moral Exposition, book thirteen on Job chapter seventeen, chapter twelve, and book thirty-four on Job chapter forty-one, chapter ten, ought rightly to be consulted.
But God's name became known to all through Pharaoh, when he justly perished with all his own, and God rescued those oppressed by him, like vessels already tested by fire in the furnace, with signs and prodigies.
Student: Also about Judas it is written: That Scripture might be fulfilled, He who eats my bread has lifted his heel against me, as the Gospel of John, chapter sixteen, verse eighteen records. And again: No one perished of those whom you gave me, except the son of perdition, that Scripture might be fulfilled, as the Gospel of John, chapter seventeen, verse twelve states: Those whom you gave me I have guarded, and no one of them perished except the son of perdition, that Scripture might be fulfilled.
And since Scripture, with the Lord himself as witness, must be fulfilled, who unless blind in all things does not see that Judas, driven by some force of necessity, betrayed the Lord, especially since the Lord had come to suffer and it was necessary that he be handed over by someone?
Master: The Lord, as far as he himself was concerned, willed to suffer not by necessity but by most free will, because the divine nature was impassible. And this was very necessary to be done for us, whom the necessity of death drove to destruction.
No necessity but malicious will instigated Judas to betrayal, when he preferred to hate the Lord with the Jews rather than to love him with the apostles. Similarly the Jews, compelled by no necessity but driven by the worst will, killed him, when they had gratuitous hatred for him whose signs and virtues they regretted were manifest.
But because the Holy Spirit, to whom all future things are present, foreknew that they would want to do such things, he thus predicted through the prophets in the Scriptures, just as afterward everything happened, as Psalm one hundred eight, verse eight says: Let his days be few, and let another take his office. Nevertheless that Scripture imposed no necessity of willing or doing on them. Just as if I foreknew a war and predicted it would happen, my words would not make the war necessary.
Student: What therefore should one gather from this, except that it was necessary for Christ to be crucified by the Jews, who had come to be killed by them, which surely he would not have done if he ought not to have done it?
Master: And do you believe that the Jews alone killed Christ? All the wicked from the beginning to the end of the world consented to Christ's death, as Psalm eighty-six, verse five begins: They have multiplied beyond the hairs
of my head who have hated me without cause. All who hate justice and truth thereby persecute the just because of justice and truth, which is Christ himself. And so they are found guilty of the Lord's death.
For they would gladly destroy all the just, if they could, so that they might pursue their own desires without opposition. Therefore, even if the Jews had not done this deed, others like them would have accomplished it.
DISCIPLE: Led inward by you, I behold many splendid things. But many remain sealed, and I grieve that I cannot perceive them. Therefore I beg you, with the key of knowledge given to you, open what is closed to me. Unfold what is wrapped up, and let me see it clearly.
MASTER: To one who knocks so diligently, the Lord will answer favorably through me, and reveal to you the hidden mysteries.
DISCIPLE: Why do we say that God permits those whom he has predestined to reign with him forever to wander in error for so long?
MASTER: By tolerating some in error for a long time, like Paul, God displays the greatness of his patient endurance. And by suddenly calling them to repentance, as he did with Paul and Mary Magdalene, he reveals the riches of his mercy. He came into the world for such people. For such people he even underwent death.
And although he died for sinners, his death did not profit Annas and Caiaphas, Herod and Pilate. Rather, it harmed them greatly. This was not only because they conspired in the Lord's death, but because they hated goodness without cause and refused to repent.
But many of the elect consented to Christ's death, though ignorantly. For these he prayed even on the cross, saying, Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing. And later, by believing, they drank the blood that they had earlier shed in violence.
DISCIPLE: Since human evil deeds do not harm God, and their good deeds do not benefit him, why does he separate people after death? Why doesn't he place them all together equally in one place?
MASTER: It contradicts the principle of justice that the just should occupy the same place as the unjust, or vice versa. God distinguishes the whole picture through manifold variation.
Therefore, just as a painter varies his work with colors, so God magnificently illuminates his kingdom with distinct orders. For what beauty would a painting have if the whole ceiling were covered with one color? Indeed, it could not even be called a painting.
But now he paints different colors in different places, and thus makes his work delightful to the sight. Similarly, the jeweler sets various gems in different places, and thus makes his work more precious.
DISCIPLE: Why does a final judgment remain to come?
MASTER: The principle of justice demands that judgment be made. Those who now hide themselves in hypocrisy will appear as wicked. And those who now publicly glory in their crimes will then justly be subjected to fitting punishments.
Those who despise laws, scorn justice, oppress the poor and the just, who feel secure through their power, who flourish through robbery, rejoice in others' harm, insult the just, trample God's words, who are praised for such things and similar deeds, who are honored by people through fear:
these, I say, will then be separated from joy and united with the unjust in punishment. But the just who are now unjustly oppressed will be raised up. Those who lived and acted well will be rewarded. And those who were in disgrace for the sake of justice will be honored.
DISCIPLE: Now tell me why man was placed in paradise when he was not going to remain there.
MASTER: The first man was created for blessedness and placed in paradise, that is, in a place of delight. But because he refused to keep the Lord's command, he justly suffered exile as a rebel.
If he had tasted nothing of paradise's delights but had violated the command he received, perhaps he would never have repented and would thus have remained an exile forever. But after he recalled to memory the sweetness of paradise that he had experienced but quickly lost, he punished himself with heavy repentance. Therefore he returned, and therefore he merited return.
From his womb, as if from a certain fortress, a double army soon came forth, namely of the elect and the reprobate. And when this was done, they joined battle with implacable discord.
In this great battle, the struggle goes both ways. The victors, crowned and triumphant, seek the stars. But the vanquished, in confusion, descend to the depths of hell.
Stand here now and consider the determination of both sides.

Concerning the humble citizens of Jerusalem.

Someone walks the path of humility from childhood, and up to old age always advances toward what is better. He considers all people superior to himself, and judges himself inferior to everyone. He praises all others' actions, but condemns his own.

Concerning the chaste citizen of Jerusalem.

Another seizes the path of chastity, guarding his senses with great care. And yet he counts all others as holy and judges himself as unclean.

Concerning the patient one.

Another treads the way of patience, patiently enduring all harsh and difficult things for Christ. He considers others gentle, but thinks himself harsh.

Concerning the abstinent one.

Another walks the path of abstinence and wastes away through great mortification of the flesh. Yet in honor he considers others stricter in abstinence, and condemns himself as gluttonous.

Concerning the one who has charity.

Another, walking in the breadth of charity, loves not only friends in God but even enemies for God's sake. When he suffers insult, he not only forgives the injury when the offender seeks pardon, but he himself hastens to be reconciled.
These and such like are the paths of the citizens of Jerusalem, by which they hasten from exile to the eternal dwelling places.

Concerning the citizens of Babylon and the reprobate.

Turn now to the citizens of Babylon and see what kind they are, and by what streets they travel.

Concerning the lustful one.

Someone begins with lust from earliest youth and continues insatiably in it until decrepit old age, never considering what he has already done but only what he can still do.

Concerning the gluttonous one.

Another subjects himself to gluttony and drunkenness from boyhood and persists in these things until the very end of old age.

Concerning the rapacious and cruel.

Another hastens to plunder with all his effort. Another keeps watch for opportunities to steal. Another feeds on cruelty. Another is never satisfied with gain. This one wastes away with envy, while that one grows foul with filth. This one, puffed up with pride, despises everyone. That one, full of hatred, afflicts everyone with lies and slander.
Such are the streets of the reprobate, by which they hasten irrevocably to the depths of hell.

Concerning the different manner of fighting between the citizens of Jerusalem and the citizens of Babylon, and vice versa.

Look now at the battle lines drawn up for combat in different ways.
The reprobate abominate the words and works of the just, detest their company, and flee from all their counsel. Often they craftily entrap them. More often they fraudulently or even violently seize their goods. Most often they afflict them with beatings or tortures, or kill them by mutilating their limbs or inflicting various punishments.
On the other hand, the just endure the ways of the reprobate as a heavy burden. They groan over their miserable manner of life. They avoid their contamination as much as they can. They desire to be freed from association with them as soon as possible. They pray to God for their salvation. They provide them with necessities as they are able.

Concerning the good harmony of the citizens of Jerusalem and the evil harmony of the citizens of Babylon.

Consider yet another thing. The elect all agree in goodness, and they love in others the good that they themselves cannot accomplish.
But the reprobate are all in agreement about evil. In good things they are always discordant. And if perhaps a word of reproof or admonition is offered to any of them by any of the just, they all resist. They all contradict together.
Even those who do not themselves do evil things, because they hate in others the good things they refuse to do, take it badly. Therefore they all declare worthy of hatred even the person they do not know. They slander him with lies and provoke him with injuries.
Look carefully and you will see in this entire conflict Cain always standing ready in battle line against Abel, Ishmael armed against Isaac, Esau fighting against Jacob, Saul resisting David, Judas against the Lord, and Simon Magus rushing against Peter.
See also in this contest how often the reprobate propose to run with the elect. Sometimes they run for a long time, but then, weary on the journey, they fail and shamefully return to their vomit like dogs.

Concerning the citizens of Babylon in particular, and first concerning those who are princes and judges over others.

Come here to the brow of the mountain where you can survey all the buildings of the condemned city. Look at the princes and judges. Behold, in them is placed the throne of the beast.
At all times they are intent on evil, always occupied insatiably in the business of iniquity. They not only commit crimes themselves, but teach others to do so. They sell holy things and buy crimes. They labor with all their strength lest they come to hell alone.

Concerning the clergy and priests of Babylon.

Turn to the clergy and you will see in them the tent of the beast. They neglect the service of God. They serve earthly gain. They pollute the priesthood through filth. They seduce the people through pretense. They deny God through evil works.
They reject all the Scriptures that pertain to salvation. In every way they offer themselves as a snare and ruin to the people. Blind themselves, they lead the blind people before them to destruction.

Concerning the monks of Babylon.

Look also at the gatherings of monks, and you will see in them the tabernacles of the beast. By their false profession they mock God and provoke his anger. They trample his regular rule with their behavior and life. They deceive the world through their habit.
Deceived themselves, they deceive many others. They are entangled in worldly business, that is, they are lazy in God's service. Many of them are devoted to gluttony and pleasures. Some rot in the filth of impurity.

Concerning the nuns of Babylon.

Look also at the dwellings of nuns, and you will see in them the prepared bridal chamber of the beast. From tender youth they learn shamelessness. They gather many accomplices for the increase of their damnation.
They hasten to cover themselves with a veil so that they may more freely loosen the reins of lust with all fornicators. They are filled with the filth of impurity. They ensnare the minds of young men and rejoice if they deceive many. And she wishes to have the palm of victory who surpasses others in wickedness.

Concerning the common people of the city of Babylon.

Turn to the rest of the common people and you will find the image of the beast. They despise priests. They scorn to hear anything about God. They spend their whole lifetime in vanity and boasting, and they are reprobate for every good work.
The uneducated rabble also has the idol of the beast. They do not know the true God. They serve the god of the belly with their whole intention. They dissolve in various fleshly desires and in all things live a bestial life.

Concerning the women of Babylon.

Come here to the slopes of this valley and you will see the monstrous gatherings of women. In them the beast has placed all his pomp and monstrosities, and he has appointed them to bear his weapons.
You see how young men are ensnared through lust. This woman kills many with poison. That one destroys her husband's life for gold. This one murders her children. That one provokes quarrels. Another instigates wars.
This one alienates men's minds through sorceries. That one is satisfied by no amount of money or pleasure. One deceives many by her appearance, another seduces very many by her tears. These are the fortifications of this city, and these are the weapons of the beast.
DISCIPLE: Oh God, how many monstrosities I see!
MASTER: You have seen the walls of the city of Babylon. Now look at the destruction of each part.
Now the citizens of Babylon, though they are more numerous, always wage war against the citizens of Jerusalem who are placed among them in exile.
But afterward, God the king of heavenly Jerusalem, coming with the army of angels, will utterly overthrow this ruined city. He will free his elect from it and bring them with him into the heavenly palace, to whom he now presents this spectacle.
This Babylon that you see, that is, the glory of this world, together with its prince the devil and all the citizens of this city, namely the lovers of this world, he will suddenly plunge into the lake of fire and sulfur. And then he will change all things to a better state.
DISCIPLE: You have provided a great spectacle for all who read these things. May God grant that you be present at that spectacle. And for what reason will the just be in glory?
MASTER: Because if they were to live without end, they would always wish to be just. Justice therefore requires that those who have always embraced justice should always be blessed.
DISCIPLE: But what is the reason that the unjust will always be in punishment?
MASTER: Because they would wish to live without end so that they could do evil without end. Therefore justice demands that those who never desired justice should never be free from punishment.
DISCIPLE: Why cannot the just sin after the resurrection?
MASTER: Because their free will is so confirmed that they desire nothing evil. They will be equal to the angels, and therefore they will be blessed forever.
DISCIPLE: Why will the unjust not be able to turn to good?
MASTER: Because they always hold good in hatred, and therefore they will be miserable forever.

Conclusion or summary of this book.

Therefore, to conclude the teaching of our disputation with a brief summary:
God's purpose is to complete the number of the elect from angels and humans. Predestination is that the just always glory in blessedness, and the unjust always suffer torment in misery.
Foreknowledge or providence is God's ever-present vision of future things. Necessity is that man must die after sins.
Justice is to will what is right. Free will is the judgment of willing right justice. Freedom is the power to preserve justice, or the judgment of willing and not willing by choice.
Grace is the inspiration of good or liberation from evil. Merit is perseverance in justice. The highest reward is blessedness. Punishment is eternal misery.
And because it is inevitable that certain infants, being baptized, are not damned by God's justice alone, and that certain adults, despisers of God's justice through free will, cannot escape due punishment as justice dictates, for this reason the title is given to this little book:

The Inevitable.

These things concerning predestination, foreknowledge, and free will I offer to the children of the Church, because the children of Babylon will despise them.
But whoever despises or attacks this work will demonstrate that he is not among the number of the predestined. And whoever raises questions about free will after these matters is like a blind man who stumbles to his death in broad daylight.
DISCIPLE: Blessed be God who inspired the brothers to send me to you, so that I might deserve to receive these wonderful things from your honeyed mouth.
And truly you should know that the flocks of the reprobate will abominate you with great hatred for this venerable work, and especially because you have proven them to be sons of vices, indeed of demons, through their free will. They will detest you.
But the glorious company of those predestined to life will render you great thanks because you have brought forth such a wonderful work in their praise.
And, as I have heard you say, since it is predestined by God that whatever the elect justly ask will be granted to them, they beseech God's mercy with great prayers that your last day may find you in their fellowship. Amen.
