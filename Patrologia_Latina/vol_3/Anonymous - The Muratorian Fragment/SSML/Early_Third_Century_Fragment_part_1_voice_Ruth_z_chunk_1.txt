<speak>
  <p>
    <s>The Muratorian Fragment is one of the earliest known lists of New Testament books, discovered by Ludovico Antonio Muratori in the 18th century.</s>
    <s>Found in a manuscript at the Ambrosian Library in Milan, this ancient text likely dates from the late 2nd or early 3rd century CE.</s>
  </p>
  <p>
    <s>Muratori described the manuscript as nearly a thousand years old, written in large, square letters.</s>
    <s>Despite some errors in copying, he recognized its importance, hoping it would be well-received due to its "highly venerable antiquity."</s>
  </p>
  <p>
    <s>Scholars have debated the fragment's authorship, with some attributing it to Caius, a Roman presbyter from the late 2nd century.</s>
    <s>While this remains uncertain, most agree on its antiquity and importance in understanding the early development of the New Testament canon.</s>
  </p>
  <p>
    <s>The fragment discusses various books now in the New Testament, omits some, and includes others not in the current canon.</s>
    <s>It also mentions some non-canonical works, accepting some for private reading while rejecting others.</s>
  </p>
  <p>
    <s>Despite some textual issues and scholarly debates, the Muratorian Fragment remains a key document in the study of early Christianity and the formation of the New Testament canon.</s>
  </p>
</speak>
