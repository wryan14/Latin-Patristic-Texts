
by Taio of Zaragoza
Patrologia Latina Volume 


Book Five. Chapter One: Lovers of the World

All the wicked neglect to turn their hearts toward eternal things, and because they do not pay attention to how quickly present things pass away, they fix their minds on love of this present life. They build themselves a foundation as if for a long habitation here, because they are anchored in earthly things through their desires.
Cain is described as the first to build a city, as recorded in the book of Genesis, chapter four. This openly demonstrates that he laid his foundation on earth because he was alienated from the stability of the eternal homeland.
A person who is an exile from the heights lays his foundation in the depths when he places the intention of his heart in earthly pleasure.
In Cain's lineage, Enoch, whose name means dedication, is born first. But in the line of the elect, Enoch is recorded as the seventh. This is because the reprobate dedicate themselves by building in this present life, which comes before eternity. But the elect await the dedication of their building at the end of time, that is, in the seventh age.
Very often we see that most people think only of temporal things, seek honors, pant after worldly ambitions, and seek nothing beyond this life. What are such people doing except dedicating themselves in the first generation?
On the other hand, we see that the elect seek nothing of present glory, gladly endure poverty, and bear the evils of the world with equanimity so that they may be crowned at the end. So for the elect, Enoch is born in the seventh generation, because they seek the dedication of their joy in the glory of final reward.
All the wicked dedicate themselves at the beginning, because they plant the root of their hearts in this life which comes first, so that they may flourish here according to their wishes but completely wither away from the delights of the homeland that follows.
Lovers of this age dwell all the more in external things, the more they leave their interior life uncultivated.
As a certain wise man says in the book of Exodus, chapter twenty-three, verse fifteen: You shall not appear before the Lord empty-handed. Anyone who loves the present world appears empty before the Lord, because he brings with him nothing from the fruit of his labor.
Whoever loves earthly things and not heavenly things, one person sweats to obtain honors, another burns to multiply his wealth, another pants to earn praises. But because everyone leaves all these things behind when dying, he appears empty before the Lord, because he brought nothing with him before the Judge.
Every tree stands robust and thick near the ground, but as it grows it becomes narrower above. And the higher it rises, the more slender it becomes at the top.
To what are such trees similar except to earthly minds, thick below and narrow above? All lovers of this age are strong in earthly things but weak in heavenly things. For they desire to labor until death for temporal glory, yet for the sake of eternal hope they do not endure even a little labor.
Lovers of this age tolerate any injuries for the sake of earthly profits, yet for heavenly reward they refuse to bear even the slightest verbal insult. For the more intent they are on worldly concerns, the more insensible they become within, and the more zealous they are for external things, the more hardened they become inside.
All who conform themselves to this age through earthly thinking strive in everything they do to leave a memory of themselves to this world. Some labor intensely through the glory of wars, others through towering buildings, others through learned books of secular teaching, and they build for themselves a name in memory.
But since life itself runs more swiftly to its end, what will stand fixed in it when life itself passes by so quickly? No matter how much anyone labors to make the glory of his name, he has placed his memory like ashes, because the wind of mortality snatches it away more quickly.
And he immediately loses everything he greedily loves from this world. As the psalmist says in Psalm twenty-three, verse four: He who has not received his soul in vain. For he receives his soul in vain who, neglecting its life, prefers to it the care of the flesh; who, thinking only of present things, does not attend to what will follow him forever.

Chapter Two: The Vanity of Fine Clothing

Let no one think that there is no sin in the pursuit and preoccupation with clothing, because if this were not a fault, the Lord would in no way have praised John for the roughness of his garment.
If the cultivation of clothing were not a fault, the apostle Peter would not have restrained women from the desire for costly garments through his letter, saying in Peter's first letter, chapter three, verse three: Not in costly clothing.
We must consider most carefully regarding the cultivation of clothing what fault it is that even men desire this, from which the shepherd of the Church took care to prohibit even women.
The Truth says concerning John the Baptist in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter eleven, verse eight: What did you go out into the desert to see? A man clothed in soft garments? Behold, those who wear soft clothing are in the houses of kings.
For John is described as having been clothed in garments woven from camel's hair. And what does it mean to say: Behold, those who wear soft clothing are in the houses of kings, except to demonstrate with open statement that those fight for an earthly kingdom, not a heavenly one, who refuse to endure harsh things for God but, devoted only to external things, seek the softness and pleasure of present life?

Chapter Three: Those Bound by Earthly Desires

Those who are bound by love of earthly things take no delight in God, because they are ensnared by the pleasures of this world. The soul can never exist without delight, for it delights either in the lowest things or in the highest. And the more keenly it burns with concern for the lowest things, the more it grows cold toward the highest things in that condemnable time.
For both cannot be loved simultaneously and equally. The apostle John, knowing that the thorns of worldly loves cannot allow the harvest of heavenly charity to spring up, first eradicates the harvest of worldly loves from the hearts of his hearers with the holy hand of the word before he spreads the seeds of eternal love, saying in John's first letter, chapter two, verse fifteen: Do not love the world or the things that are in the world.
Concerning the vain love of this world, the same apostle adds, saying: If anyone loves the world, the charity of the Father is not in him. As if he were saying openly: Both loves cannot contain themselves in one heart, nor does the crop of heavenly charity sprout in that heart where the thorns of base pleasure kill it.
The same apostle John enumerates the stinging wounds born from the base pleasure of this world, saying: All that is in the world is the lust of the flesh and the lust of the eyes and the pride of life, which is not from the Father but from this world, and the world passes away and its lust.
A person laid low by love of this world cannot take delight in God, because heavenly desires do not come forth in his mind, which the thorns of earthly love surely press down. When anyone in this age finds the result of the happiness he sought, he does not seek the Author who granted this very happiness, because he is not ashamed to pursue this world with selfish love.

Chapter Four: The Wise of This World

The wise of this age must be admonished to lose the knowledge they have and to desire to know what they do not know. This first thing must be destroyed in them: that they think themselves wise. For it is written in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter three, verse nineteen: The wisdom of this world is foolishness with God.
The wise of this world must be told to become wisely foolish, to abandon foolish wisdom and learn the wise foolishness of God, as it is written: If anyone among you seems to be wise in this age, let him become a fool that he may be wise.
Very often the wise of this age are better converted by arguments of reasoning than by examples, so that they may lie defeated by their own arguments who stood rigid in their own obstinacy.
The outstanding teacher, the apostle Paul, debtor to both the wise and foolish, when admonishing some of the Hebrews who were wise and some who were even slower of understanding, speaking about the fulfillment of the Old Testament, overcame their wisdom with an argument, saying in the letter to the Hebrews, chapter eight, verse thirteen: For what is becoming obsolete and growing old is ready to vanish away. So that victorious reasoning might break them and persuade them to better things by the argument of gentle speech.

Chapter Five: Those Hardened by God's Judgment

Just as no one resists the generosity of God when He calls, so no one opposes the justice of God when He abandons. For the Lord does not harden the heart of the sinner, but He is said to harden when He does not free from hardening.
He says He hardens through justice when He does not soften the hearts of the reprobate through grace. For God to shut is not to open what is closed, but to leave sinners in the darkness of their own works.
Merciful God grants us time for repentance, but when we turn the patience of His grace into an increase of guilt, He turns that very time which He mercifully appointed for sparing into a stricter time for striking.
When anyone, even after receiving time, has refused to return from the dark paths of his error, he increases his guilt for his evils through that very means by which he could have washed them away if he had been willing to turn back.

Chapter Six: Those Who Voluntarily Undergo Tribulations for Love of the World

He who, out of love for the present age, is alienated from the sense of reason, while he considers whatever he endures for the world as light, does not perceive the bitterness of the labor he tolerates, because he is led delightfully to all things in which he is painfully exhausted.
The prophet Jeremiah, seeing the human race drunk on its own pleasures, says in the book of Lamentations, chapter three, verse sixteen: He has made me drunk with wormwood, He has filled me with bitterness. For a drunk man does not know what he suffers. But he who is made drunk with wormwood, what he has taken is bitter, and yet he does not perceive the very bitterness with which he is filled.
Just so are the lovers of this age, who become bitter through manifold hardships and tribulations of labor, and yet like drunkards are rendered insensible through the madness of desire.
The human race, by the just judgment of God left to its own pleasures and through those same pleasures handed over to voluntary tribulations, is drunk with wormwood. For what it endures for love of this age is bitter, and yet through the blindness of desire, as if through the insensibility of drunkenness, it does not perceive that very bitterness.
Whoever thirsts for the glory of the world, while he finds many tribulations for it, what he drinks is bitter. But because he has taken it too greedily, he cannot discern the evil of that bitterness because of the very drunkenness.
Perverse people love even tribulations for the sake of this world's glory. They willingly serve with all labors for it and most devotedly submit their necks to the yoke of heavy burdens.

Chapter Seven: The Prosperity of the Wicked

He who turns away from God and prospers becomes all the closer to perdition, the more he is found alienated from the zeal of discipline.
Some both perpetrate iniquities and do not cease to rejoice. These indeed rejoice when they have done evil and exult in the worst things, as the book of Proverbs, chapter two, verse fourteen says. Some of the reprobate, when they act wickedly and prosper, are not raised up by health but by insanity with which they ought to be afflicted. And from this, miserable people flow into exultation from which they are wept over by the good.
Very many of the reprobate, while they are bound by the chains of their sins, in no way return to the path of justice. They are similar to the minds of madmen, who think the insanity by which they excel is virtue, who do not know that what they can do more than healthy people comes from disease, and they think they have grown in strength while they approach the end of life through the increases of sickness.
Sometimes the reprobate, similar to madmen because they do not have the sense of reason, are wept over while they laugh. And they expand themselves in great exultation all the more because, being insensible, they do not know the evil they suffer.
In the book of Ecclesiastes, chapter ten, verse seven, it is written: I saw servants on horses and princes walking like servants on the earth. Everyone who sins is a slave of sin. For servants are on horses when sinners are lifted up by the dignities of present life.
But princes walk like servants when many who are full of the dignity of virtues are raised up by no honor, but supreme adversity presses them down as if unworthy. Through the psalmist it is said: Those who mounted horses have slept, that is, in the death of the soul they closed the eyes of the mind from the light of truth who trusted in the honor of present life.

Chapter Eight: The Agreement of the Wicked

When the wickedness of the perverse is joined in peace, strength is certainly increased to their evil deeds. For the more they agree with each other in malice, the more strongly they dash themselves against the afflictions of the good.
To blessed Job the divine voice says concerning Leviathan in the book of Job, chapter forty-one, verse fourteen: The parts of his flesh cling to each other. His body is like shields of cast metal, compacted with scales pressing on each other, one joined to another, and not even a breath passes through them.
For the followers of Satan, since they are divided by no adversity of discord among themselves, mass together all the more heavily for the destruction of the good. He who unites the wicked in peace administers strength to wickedness, because they more severely oppress the good whom they unanimously persecute.
Those whom similar guilt unites, perverse defense also binds together with concordant stubbornness, so that they protect each other regarding their crimes with mutual defense. For each one fears for himself when he sees another being admonished or corrected.
And therefore they rise up unanimously against the words of those correcting, because each protects himself in the other. While the wicked protect each other by turns with proud defense, they by no means permit the breathing passages of holy exhortation to enter themselves.
Blessed Job indicates the pestilent agreement of the wicked under the scales of Leviathan, saying: One will cling to another and holding each other they will by no means be separated. For those who could be corrected if divided, united they persist in the stubbornness of their iniquities.
And daily they become more separated from the knowledge of justice, the more they are not separated from each other by any rebuke. Just as it is usually harmful if unity is lacking to the good, so it is destructive if it is not lacking to the evil.
Sometimes unity strengthens the perverse while it harmonizes, and makes them all the more incorrigible the more unanimous it makes them. Concerning the unity of agreeing reprobates, it is said through Solomon in the book of Sirach, chapter twenty-one, verse ten: The assembly of sinners is like tow gathered together. Concerning this the prophet Nahum says in chapter one, verse ten: As thorns embrace each other, so is their banquet of those drinking together.
For the banquet of the reprobate is the delight of temporal pleasures. In this banquet indeed they drink together, because they make themselves drunk concordantly with the enticements of their pleasure.
The members of Satan, that is, all the wicked whom the word of God compares to the joining of scales, equal guilt harmonizes for their defense, just as it is said to blessed Job concerning the scales of Leviathan: One will cling to another, and holding each other they will by no means be separated.
For holding each other they cannot be separated, because they are mutually bound together for their defense, since they remember that they are in all things similar to each other.
In the book of Exodus, chapter fourteen, verse twenty-two, it is written: The water was divided, and the children of Israel went through the midst of the sea on dry ground. For the just are rescued when the unjust are divided through discord, and the desires of the elect arrive at perfection when the bands of the reprobate are confused by the bond of discord.
When the wave of the Red Sea is divided into two parts, the journey to the promised land is made by the chosen people, because while the unity of the wicked is split, good minds are led to what they desire.
If the unity of the wicked were not harmful, divine providence would never have scattered the tongues of the proud in such diversity. If the unity of the wicked were not harmful, the prophet would not say concerning the enemies of the holy Church in Psalm fifty-four, verse ten: Cast them down, O Lord, and divide their tongues.

Chapter Nine: Princes of the World

The princes of this world, the more they know themselves to be set over many throngs of peoples, should prostrate themselves with humble mind under the discipline of the holy faith, so that they strive not only to preside but rejoice to benefit many.
For he who is lifted up by the multitude of subjected peoples is deservedly punished by the ruin of his fall through the very arrogance of pride, because he strives to climb to the heights, as it is written in Psalm seventy-two, verse eighteen: You have cast them down while they were being lifted up. The psalmist does not say, You cast them down after they were lifted up, but while they were being lifted up.
Therefore let princes fear this first ruin, who do not fear to love their own private glory, because they usually fall inwardly to the extent that they rise wrongly outwardly.
All who rule in this world are pressed in their hearts by dense tumults of thoughts. And while they stir up crowds of desires within themselves, they trample down the prostrate mind with the foot of wretched busyness.
Sometimes a prince, by the very fact that he excels others, swells with pride of thought. And while all things are subject to his use, while commands are swiftly fulfilled according to his wishes, while all subjects exalt with praises whatever has been well done but contradict with no authority whatever has been done badly, while they often praise even what they should have rebuked, seduced by these things that are submitted below, his spirit is lifted up above himself. And while he is surrounded outwardly by immense favor, he is emptied inwardly of truth.
And forgetting himself, he scatters himself into the voices of others, and he believes himself to be such as he hears himself to be outwardly, not such as he should discern inwardly.
Often princes despise each of their subjects and do not acknowledge them as equals in the order of nature. And they believe they have surpassed in the merits of life those whom they have exceeded by the lot of power.
They esteem themselves wiser than all those over whom they see themselves to have more power. For they establish themselves in a certain height in their own estimation. And because they are bound by the same condition of nature as others, they disdain to regard others equally.
And so they are led to the likeness of him concerning whom it is written in the book of Job, chapter forty-one, verse twenty-five: He sees everything that is high, and he is king over all the children of pride. Whoever, endowed with the power of honor, is lifted up above his subjects with the arrogance of pride, is led to the likeness of him who, desiring singular height and despising the social life of the angels, said as recorded in the book of Isaiah, chapter fourteen, verse fourteen: I will ascend above the height of the clouds; I will be like the Most High.
Therefore by a wonderful judgment he finds within the pit of abasement while outwardly he lifts himself up in the height of power. For he becomes similar to the apostate angel when a human being disdains to be similar to another human being.
King Saul, by the merit of humility, grew into the swelling of pride at the height of power. For through humility he was set over others, but through pride he was rejected, as the Lord testified, saying in the first book of Samuel, chapter fifteen, verse seventeen: When you were little in your own eyes, did I not make you head over the tribes of Israel?
Saul had seen himself as little before power, but supported by temporal power he no longer saw himself as little. For preferring himself by comparison with others, he judged himself to be great in his own estimation. But in a wonderful way, when he was little in his own eyes, he was great before the Lord; but when he was great in his own eyes, he was little before the Lord.
Often, while the spirit of a prince is inflated by the abundance of subjects, it is corrupted into the excess of pride, with the very height of power acting as procurer. But it is one thing not to have any goods, another thing not to know how to possess goods well.
In everything that a prince does, he is surrounded by tribulation and anxiety. For his heart is confused by worry and suspicion, as it is said through blessed Job in chapter fifteen, verse twenty-four: Tribulation will terrify him, and anguish will surround him like a king who is prepared for battle.
Although a prince positioned in any place sins by thinking, speaking, or doing, yet his spirit is carried away more unbridledly through these three things when he is more frequently lifted up by the prosperity of this world.
When a prince sees that he surpasses others in power, he thinks lofty things about himself with pride. And when the authority of his voice is resisted by no one, his tongue is unbridled more licentiously into precipices. When a prince is usually allowed to do whatever he wishes, he considers that everything he wishes is justly permitted to him.
Nebuchadnezzar, king of Babylon, while turning over in his mind with pride and saying in the book of Daniel, chapter four, verse twenty-seven: Is this not Babylon which I have built? was immediately turned into an irrational animal. For he lost what he had been made, because he did not wish to humbly conceal what he had done.
And because he lifted himself above human beings by the pride of his thought, he lost the very sense of humanity that he had in common with other people.
Often princes who are in power break out indiscriminately into insults toward their subjects, and they lose through talkativeness of tongue what they serve by being vigilant in governance.
Through Solomon it is said in the book of Ecclesiastes, chapter ten, verse sixteen: Woe to you, O land, whose king is a boy and whose princes eat in the morning. The king is not inappropriately called a boy when he who presides, despite the care of governance, delights in childish acts and is by no means withdrawn from the perpetration of sin.
Whence Isaiah rightly says in chapter sixty-five, verse twenty: The boy of a hundred years will die, and the sinner of a hundred years will be accursed. That is, the life of a boy is indeed drawn out long that he may be corrected from childish deeds. But if he is not restrained from the perpetration of sin even by length of time, this very length of life, which he received through mercy, grows for him into a heap of curse.
What wise person of right understanding does not know that the kings of the earth are far from solitude to the extent that they are crowded by countless wedges of attendants? For those who are bound by such hard bonds of multiple responsibilities proceed with difficulty toward rest.
As Scripture testifies, which says in the book of Wisdom, chapter six, verse six: The harshest judgment will be made on those who preside. Whence also in the Gospel, the Truth says in the Gospel of Luke, chapter twelve, verse forty-eight: To whom much has been given, much will be required of him.
Blessed Job, having fulfilled the honor of leadership, suggests how great was his loftiness and how great his kindness, saying in the book of Job, chapter twenty-nine, verse nine: Princes stopped speaking and placed their finger on their mouth. And again in verse twenty-five: And when I sat as king with an army standing around me, I was nevertheless the consoler of those mourning.
Often those who rule by power lose the things they do rightly because they think about them with pride. And while they consider themselves useful for everything, they even condemn for themselves the merit of expended usefulness.
For in order that the deeds of a prince be more worthy, it is necessary that they always seem unworthy to him in his own estimation, lest the same good action lift up the heart of the doer, and by lifting him up cast down the author more through pride than it perhaps helps those on whom it is expended.
Power, when possessed, should usually both be thought of for the benefit of many and concealed because of swelling. So that he who uses it both knows that he is able so that he should benefit, and does not know that he is able so that he should not be lifted up.

Chapter Ten: Good Princes

Great is princely power, because it has its merit before God from good administration of governance. Princely power is good in its order, but it needs the cautious life of the one governing. He exercises it well who knows to be raised up through it over faults, and knows to be composed with others in equality through it.
The human mind is usually lifted up even when it is supported by no power. How much more does it raise itself on high when power is also joined to it? And yet it is prepared with suitable execution for correcting the vices of others. Whence it is also said through Paul in his letter to the Romans, chapter thirteen, verse four: For he is God's minister, an avenger for wrath.
When the height of princely power is undertaken, it must be watched with supreme care so that anyone knows both to take from it what helps and to fight against what tempts.
When princes are supported by the power of this world, they reduce themselves under greater discipline of mind to the degree that they know that unlawful things are persuaded to them more licentiously through the impatience of power. For they restrain the heart from considering glory, restrict the tongue from immoderate speech, and guard their work from the wandering of restlessness.
It must be known most especially that a king who is prepared for battle is so suspicious of the enemy that he also fears the very army he leads, lest it be weakened and he be exposed to the javelins of enemies by the desertion of soldiers.
Often a king who is prepared for battle is surrounded by excessive anxiety, because he fears lest he suddenly lose his soldiers and be seized by the snares of attacking enemies through some neglect.
A prince managing an earthly republic punishes differently a citizen sinning within and an enemy rebelling without. In the former case he consults his laws and condemns him under words of worthy rebuke. But against an enemy he wages war, exercises instruments of destruction, and repays torments worthy of his malice. But he does not inquire what the law has concerning his evil, for it is not necessary to be destroyed by law for one who could never be held by law.

Chapter 20: The Location and Nature of Hell

Some have thought that hell exists in a certain part of the earth, while others believe it is beneath the earth. But this question troubles the mind: if we call it hell because it lies below the earth as the earth lies below heaven, then hell must be to the earth what the earth is to heaven. This may be why Scripture says, as in the Psalms, chapter eighty-five, verse thirteen, 'You have delivered my soul from the lower hell'—suggesting that there is an upper hell, which would be the earth, and a lower hell beneath the earth.
The voice of John agrees with this estimation. When he said he saw a book sealed with seven seals, he added—as recorded in the book of Revelation, chapter five, verse four—'And I wept much, because no one was found worthy, neither in heaven, nor on earth, nor under the earth, to open the book and loose its seals.' Yet he says that afterward the book was opened by the Lion of the tribe of Judah.
What else is signified in that book but Sacred Scripture? Only our Redeemer opened it—he who became human and, by dying, rising, and ascending, revealed all the mysteries that had been sealed within it. No one in heaven was found worthy to open the book, not even an angel; no one on earth, not even a man living in the body; no one under the earth, because not even souls stripped of their bodies could open for us the secrets of sacred Scripture, except the Lord.
Since no one under the earth was found worthy to open the book, I see no obstacle to believing that hell is under the earth.

Chapter 21: Purgatorial Fire and Post-Mortem Purification

The foundation bears the whole weight of the building, because our Redeemer alone bears the conduct of all people. For it is written, in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter three, verse eleven: 'No one can lay a foundation other than that which has been laid, which is Jesus Christ.' The foundation bears the stones, and is not borne by the stones, because our Redeemer bears all that is ours, but in him there was no evil that needed to be borne.
He is the foundation of foundations, because he is the source for beginners and the strength of those who are strong. The excellent preacher says, in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter three, verse twelve: 'If anyone builds on this foundation with gold, silver, precious stones, wood, hay, or straw, each one's work will become manifest. For the Day of the Lord will declare it, because it will be revealed by fire; and the fire will test each one's work, of what sort it is. If anyone's work which he has built endures, he will receive a reward. If anyone's work is burned, he will suffer loss; but he himself will be saved, yet so as through fire.'
Although what we have said above can be understood as referring to the fire of tribulation applied to us in this life, if anyone takes it to mean the fire of future purgation, we must carefully consider that he said the person can be saved through fire—not one who builds on this foundation iron, brass, or lead, that is, greater sins that are therefore harder and already insoluble, but wood, hay, and straw, that is, the smallest and lightest sins, which fire easily consumes.
Yet it must be known that no one will obtain any purification there, even for the smallest sins, unless while still placed in this life he merits it through good deeds, so that he may obtain it there.

Chapter 22: God's Wrath and Unchanging Justice

The wrath of almighty God daily carries out the force of his strict judgment in that he plunges those living unworthily into deserved punishments. This wrath is passing even now, but it will fully pass at the end, because it is now at work, but will be consummated at the world's end. This fury, as far as the souls of the elect are concerned, passed away at the coming of our Redeemer, because he—the mediator between God and humanity—brought them back from the prisons of hell to the joys of paradise when he descended there in mercy.
We must know that the name of fury does not properly belong to the Divinity, because no disturbance confounds the simple nature of God. As the book of Wisdom, chapter twelve, verse eighteen says: 'You, Lord of power, judge with tranquility, and with great reverence you dispose of us.' The Creator of all is supremely immortal in that he is not changeable in the manner of creatures. For it is written: 'You, Lord, judge with tranquility.'
And again, from the prophet Jeremiah, chapter twenty-five, verse thirty-eight: 'The land has been made desolate before the wrath of the dove, before the fury of the Lord.' What the prophet foretold as the wrath of the dove, he then called the fury of the Lord. For the dove is a very simple creature, and because no inequality of fury creeps into God, he called the fury of the Lord the wrath of the dove. So that the prophet might demonstrate the undisturbed force of divine judgment, he spoke of both wrath and the dove—as if saying more openly: he who remains gentle exercises strict judgment, and punishes without disturbance.

Chapter 23: Divine Chastisements: Purposes and Types

There are different kinds of blows. One kind of blow is when a sinner is struck to be punished without possibility of retraction. Another is when a sinner is struck to be corrected. Another is when someone is sometimes struck, not to correct past sins, but to prevent future ones. Another is when someone is often struck, through which neither past guilt is corrected nor future sin prevented, but rather, when unexpected deliverance follows the blow, the power of the Savior becomes known and is loved more ardently; and when an innocent person is worn down by the scourge, the highest sum of merits is heaped up for him through patience.
Sometimes a sinner is struck to be punished without retraction, as is said to doomed Judea in the prophet Jeremiah, chapter thirty, verse fourteen: 'I have struck you with the blow of an enemy, with cruel chastisement.' And again, verse fifteen: 'Why do you cry out to me over your crushing? Your pain is incurable.'
Sometimes a sinner is struck to be corrected, as is said to someone in the Gospel of John, chapter five, verse fourteen: 'See, you have been made well; sin no more, lest something worse happen to you.' For the Savior's words indicate that previous sins demanded the force of suffering experienced.
Sometimes someone is struck not to wash away past guilt, but to avoid future sin, as Paul openly testifies about himself in his second letter to the Corinthians, chapter twelve, verse seven: 'Lest the greatness of the revelations should exalt me, a thorn in my flesh was given to me, an angel of Satan to buffet me.' For since he does not say 'because it exalted' but 'lest it exalt,' he openly indicates that by that blow he is restrained lest it happen, not that guilt which has happened is purged.
Sometimes someone is struck neither for past nor future iniquity, but so that the power of divine virtue alone might be shown through the removal of the blow. Thus when it was said to the Lord in the Gospel of John, chapter nine, verse two, about the man born blind: 'Did this man sin, or his parents, that he was born blind?' The Lord answered, saying in verse three: 'Neither this man sinned, nor his parents, but that the works of God might be manifested.' In this manifestation, what is accomplished except that virtue of merits is increased by the scourge, and when no past iniquity is wiped away, great strength is generated from patience?
Almighty God reserves not only future punishments for those acting wickedly, but also entangles their hearts here, even where they sin, with penalties, so that by the very act of sinning they strike themselves, always trembling, always suspicious, fearing to suffer from others the evils they remember having done to others. When someone is so struck that his strength is overcome by the blow, the Lord no longer strikes him as a son through discipline, but as an enemy through wrath. When blows exceed the strength of our patience, we must greatly fear lest, our sins demanding it, we be struck not as sons by a father, but as enemies by the Lord.

Chapter 24: The World's End and Its Sufferings

Those who love God are commanded to rejoice and be glad at the world's end or its blows, because they will soon find the one they love, while that which they did not love passes away. God forbid that any faithful person should not desire to see God, and should mourn the world's blows, knowing that the world is being ended by those very blows.
It is written in the letter of James, chapter four, verse four: 'Whoever desires to be a friend of this world is constituted an enemy of God.' Therefore, whoever does not rejoice as the world's end approaches testifies that he is its friend, and is thereby proven to be an enemy of God. God forbid that it should be far from the hearts of the faithful that they not rejoice at the world's end. God forbid that this be true of those who believe through faith that another life exists, and love it through their actions.
To mourn the world's destruction belongs to those who have planted the roots of their hearts in love of it, who do not seek the life to come, who do not even suspect that it exists. We, who have come to know the eternal joys of the heavenly homeland, ought to hasten toward them as quickly as possible. We should desire to go more swiftly and to reach that life of perpetual happiness.
By what evils is the world not pressed? What sorrow, what adversity does not afflict us? What is mortal life but a journey? And what a thing it is—consider it first—to grow weary in the labor of the journey, yet not wish for that same journey to end. But that the world should be trampled and despised, our Redeemer makes clear by a wise comparison when he says in the Gospel: 'Look at the fig tree and all the trees; when they now put forth fruit, you know that summer is near. So also, when you see these things happening, know that the kingdom of God is near.' As if saying openly: Just as the approach of summer is recognized from the fruit of trees, so from the ruin of the world the kingdom of God is known to be near.

Chapter 25: The Conversion of the Jews at the End

The holy Church, made fruitful in its beginnings by the multitude of nations, will scarcely at the world's end receive the Jews whom it finds. Gathering the last remnants, it will place them like the remnants of crops. Concerning these remnants of the Jewish people, the prophet Isaiah, chapter ten, verse twenty-two says: 'If the number of the children of Israel be as the sand of the sea, a remnant shall be saved.'
With Elijah's coming, it is promised that he will turn the hearts of the children back to their fathers—as the prophet Malachi, chapter four, verse six says—so that the teaching which has now been taken from the heart of the Jews will then return, the Lord having mercy, when the children begin to understand concerning the Lord what the fathers preached. Esau, who returns late to his father, signifies that the Jewish people returns late to pleasing God. To him this is said in the blessing in the book of Genesis, chapter twenty-seven, verse forty: 'And the time will come when you will shake off his yoke from your neck.'
The Jewish people will be freed from the servitude of sin at the world's end, as it is written in Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter eleven, verse twenty-five: 'Until the fullness of the Gentiles has come in, and so all Israel will be saved.' And as the Prophet says in Isaiah, chapter ten, verse twenty-two: 'If the number of the children of Israel be as the sand of the sea, a remnant shall be saved.' The holy Church, still placed in this life, receives double gifts for the labors it sustains, since, having received the Gentiles fully as we said, at the world's end it also converts the hearts of the Jews to itself, as it is written: 'Until the fullness of the Gentiles has come in, and so all Israel will be saved.'

Chapter 26: The Times of Antichrist

The ancient enemy approaches to tempt more terribly at the world's end, because he becomes all the more fervent toward cruelty the closer he feels himself to punishment. For he considers that it is near that he should lose the license of his most wicked freedom; and the more he is constrained by the brevity of time, the more he expands in the multiplication of cruelty. In the book of Revelation, chapter twelve, verse twelve, it says: 'Woe to the earth and the sea, because the devil has come down to you having great wrath, knowing that he has little time.'
Then he expands himself in the fervor of great wrath, lest he who could not stand in blessedness should fall into the pit of his own damnation with only a few. Whatever the devil has wickedly been able to do, he pursues more cunningly at the world's end. Then he raises the neck of pride higher, and through that damned man whom he carries, he wickedly displays all that temporarily prevails.
The same John says in Revelation, chapter twelve, verse four: 'The dragon's tail drew a third part of the stars of heaven, and cast them to the earth.' For heaven is the Church, which in this night of the present life, while it contains in itself the countless virtues of the saints, shines with stars radiating from above. The dragon's tail casts stars to the earth, because that extremity of Satan, raised up through the audacity of the man he has assumed, shows certain ones whom he finds in the Church as if they were God's elect to be reprobate by prevailing over them.
For stars to fall from heaven to earth means that some, abandoning the hope of heavenly things, under that leader's guidance gape after the pursuit of worldly glory. Therefore this dragon cast forth his tail and drew out a third part of the stars. For a part of the stars is drawn by the dragon's tail, because by the final persuasion of Antichrist, certain ones who seem to shine will be snatched away.
Some report that Antichrist will come from the tribe of Dan, as is said through the patriarch Jacob in the book of Genesis, chapter forty-nine, verse seventeen: 'Let Dan be a serpent in the way, a horned viper in the path, biting the horse's heels so that its rider falls backward.' In this place Dan is declared both serpent and biting. Hence not undeservedly, when the Israelite people received lands in the division of camps, Dan first camped toward the north—signifying him who said in his heart, as Isaiah chapter fourteen, verse thirteen records: 'I will sit on the mount of assembly, in the far reaches of the north; I will ascend above the heights of the clouds; I will make myself like the Most High.'
And concerning him the prophet Jeremiah, chapter eight, verse sixteen says: 'From Dan was heard the snorting of his horses.' Antichrist is called not only serpent but also horned viper. For in Greek, kerata means horns, and this serpent is said to be horned, through which the coming of that same Antichrist is fittingly asserted, because against the life of the faithful he is armed not only with the bite of pestilent preaching but also with the horns of power.
Who does not know that a path is narrower than a way? Therefore Dan becomes a serpent in the way, because in the breadth of the present life he provokes them to walk whom he flatters as if sparing them; but in the way he bites, because those to whom he grants liberty, he consumes with the venom of his error. He becomes a horned viper in the path, because those whom he finds faithful and constraining themselves to the narrow roads of heavenly commandment, he attacks not only with the wickedness of cunning persuasion but also oppresses with the terror of power; and in the languor of persecution, after the benefits of feigned sweetness, he exercises the horns of power.
This horse, whose hooves the horned viper is said to bite, suggests this world, which through its exaltation foams with the rushing of passing times. And because Antichrist strives to seize the world's extremities, this horned viper is said to bite the horse's hooves. To bite the horse's hooves is to strike and reach the world's end, 'so that its rider falls backward.' The faithless Jewish people, caught in the snares of their error, await Antichrist instead of Christ.
Well does Jacob, in that same place, suddenly turn to speak in the voice of the elect, saying in verse eighteen: 'I will await your salvation, Lord'—that is, not like the faithless awaiting Antichrist, but I faithfully believe that the one who is to come for our redemption is the true Christ.
Solomon says in the book of Proverbs, chapter thirty, verse thirty-two: 'He who appeared foolish after he was elevated on high—if indeed he had understood, he would have put his hand over his mouth.' For Antichrist will be elevated on high when he lies that he is God. But elevated on high he will appear foolish, because in that very exaltation he will fail at the coming of the true Judge. But if he had understood, he would have put his hand over his mouth—that is, if he had foreseen his punishment when he began to be proud, he who was once well hidden would not have been exalted in such boasting of pride.
Antichrist will walk in his deceit, but according to the brief time of the present life, that very deceit will prosper for him. Hence it is said through the prophet Daniel, chapter eight, verse twelve: 'Strength was given to him against the continual sacrifice, because of sins, and truth will be cast down to the ground, and he will act and prosper.' What Solomon says in Proverbs, chapter thirty, verse twenty-nine, 'He walks prosperously,' this Daniel says: 'He will act and prosper.'
For it is written of Antichrist's times in the book of Job, chapter eighteen, verse twenty: 'In his days the last ones will be stunned, and horror will invade the first.' For then he will be so unbridled in iniquity against the just that even the hearts of the elect will be struck with no small fear—as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-four, verse twenty-four says: 'So as to lead into error, if it were possible, even the elect.' This is said not because the elect will fall, but because they will tremble with great terrors.
Then both the last and the first of the elect are said to have the contest of justice against Antichrist, because both those who will be found elect at the world's end must be laid low in the death of the flesh, and those also who proceeded from the earlier parts of the world—namely Enoch and Elijah—will be called back to the middle, and will suffer the savagery of his cruelty still in their mortal flesh.
The ancient enemy, given over to his own powers, is loosed for a short time indeed, but with much strength against us. Because the force of savagery expands him toward cruelty, yet heavenly mercy constrains him by the brevity of days. For Truth itself says in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-four, verse twenty-one: 'Then there will be great tribulation, such as has not been from the beginning of the world until now, nor ever will be.' And again it says in verse twenty-two: 'Unless those days had been shortened, no flesh would be saved.'
Because the Lord sees us both proud and weak, he mercifully calls shortened the days he brought forth as singularly evil—certainly so that he might terrify pride by the adversity of the time, and comfort weakness by its brevity.
The prophet Daniel, speaking of Antichrist's times, says in chapter seven, verse eight: 'I was considering the ten horns, and behold, another little horn sprang up from among them, and three of the first horns were plucked up before it; and behold, eyes like the eyes of a man were in this horn, and a mouth speaking great things.' Because transgression is expressed by the number eleven, the author of transgression himself is indicated by the eleventh horn of this beast.
It rises small because a mere man is generated, but it grows monstrously, because it advances even to the angelic strength joined to it. That damned man at the world's end will pluck up three horns that are before his face, because he will subjugate to his dominion three kingdoms that are near to him.
His eyes are like the eyes of a man, but his mouth speaks great things, because in him human form is indeed seen, but by his words he is elevated beyond men. What is said through Paul the Apostle in his second letter to the Thessalonians, chapter two, verse four—'Exalting himself above all that is called God or that is worshiped'—this the prophet Daniel testifies, saying: 'A mouth speaking great things.'
That Daniel reports he speaks great things, and Paul testifies he is exalted above the worship of deity—this is what blessed Job compares to a cedar in divine discourse, chapter forty, verse twelve. For in the manner of a cedar he strives toward the heights, while he prospers in all the pomp of deceit, both in the strength of power and in the exaltation of his summit.

Chapter 27: Antichrist and His Members

The strength of Antichrist is not strengthened by his own powers, because he is raised up to the glory of perdition by Satan's power, as it is written in the prophet Daniel, chapter eight, verse twenty-three: 'A king shall arise, shameless in face and understanding dark schemes, and his strength shall be made strong, but not by his own powers.'
The prophet Daniel says of the same Antichrist in verses ten through twelve: 'He cast down some of the host and some of the stars, and trampled them, and he magnified himself even up to the Prince of the host, and from him he took away the continual sacrifice and cast down the place of his sanctuary. And strength was given to him against the continual sacrifice because of sins, and truth will be cast down to the ground.'
That principally damned man is the one called the whirring of wings, as Isaiah chapter eighteen, verse one says, because those who fly through pride into the height of thought sound forth by preaching that same perverse man. This land is rightly said to be beyond the rivers of Ethiopia. For Ethiopia sends forth a black people, and this world brings forth every man as a sinner, just as Ethiopia produces a black people.
But the land to which woe is said is reported to be beyond the rivers of Ethiopia, because that damned man is so immensely wicked that he transcends the sins of all who sin. That perverse man sends envoys on the sea, because he scatters his preachers throughout the world. Concerning them it is rightly added how they are sent, when it says in Isaiah chapter eighteen, verse one: 'In vessels of papyrus on the waters.' For from papyrus comes parchment. What therefore is designated by papyrus but secular learning? Therefore vessels of papyrus are the hearts of secular teachers.
In the book of Revelation, John says in chapter thirteen, verse eleven: 'I saw another beast ascending from the earth.' For he had narrated the first beast—that is, Antichrist—in the earlier description. After him this other beast is said to have ascended, because after him the multitude of his preachers is made thick from earthly power. For to ascend from the earth is to be proud of earthly glory.
This beast has two horns like a lamb, because through the hypocrisy of holiness it falsely claims to possess in itself the unique wisdom and life that the Lord truly had. But because under the appearance of a lamb, serpentine poison is poured into reprobate hearers, it is rightly added there in verse eleven: 'And it spoke like a dragon.' Therefore this beast—that is, the multitude of preachers—if it spoke openly like a dragon, would not appear like a lamb; but it assumes the appearance of a lamb in order to carry out the operation of a dragon.
The bitterness of perverse minds does not in this time break out externally into the voice of depraved license. The time will certainly come when the perverse and carnal will preach with open voice what they now contrive in hidden thought. The time will come when they will oppress the Catholic Church not only with unjust voices but also with cruel blows.
In the Gospel, Truth says in Matthew chapter twenty-four, verse twenty-four: 'False christs and false prophets will arise, and will give great signs and wonders, so as to lead into error, if it were possible, even the elect.' Now our faithful ones work wonders when they suffer perverse things; but then the satellites of Antichrist will work wonders even as they inflict perverse things.
Let us consider what will be that temptation of the human mind in the times of Antichrist, when the devout martyr both subjects his body to torments, and yet before his eyes the torturer works miracles. Whose virtue will not then be shaken from the very depth of thoughts, when he who tortures with scourges flashes with signs? Because certainly he will then be exalted by veneration of the prodigy, and harsh by the cruelty of the torment. For he will not then be raised up by power alone, but will also be supported by the display of signs.
He says: 'False christs and false prophets will arise and will give great signs and wonders, so as to lead into error, if it were possible, even the elect.' In this matter it must be greatly asked how those who are elect can be led into error, or why 'if it were possible' is added as if from doubt, when the Lord who foreknows all things awaits what is to be done.
The heart of the elect in the times of Antichrist will be shaken by trembling thought, yet their constancy will not be moved. With this one sentence the Lord embraced both, saying: 'So as to lead into error, if it were possible, even the elect.' For to waver in thought is as if already to err; but immediately 'if it were possible' is added, because without doubt it cannot happen that the elect be fully caught in error.

Chapter 28: The Second Coming of Christ

The Mediator between God and humanity, the man Christ Jesus, just as he came humbly to his passion, so will he appear terrible in the final judgment in the vengeance upon the reprobate. The Lord himself says in the Gospel of Luke, chapter twenty-one, verses twenty-six and twenty-seven: 'The powers of heaven will be shaken, and then they will see the Son of Man coming in clouds with great power and majesty.'
For what does the Lord call the powers of heaven but angels, archangels, thrones, dominations, principalities, and powers? This multitude of angels will appear visibly to our eyes at the coming of the strict Judge, so that they may then strictly demand from us what the invisible Creator now bears with us equably. They will see Christ in power and majesty who refused to hear him when he was placed in humility, so that they may then feel his power all the more strictly as they now do not incline the neck of their heart to his patience.
The prophet Isaiah says in chapter three, verse fourteen: 'The Lord will come to judgment with the elders of his people and its princes.' Christ will come to judgment with those of whom he himself says in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter nineteen, verse twenty-eight: 'You who have followed me, in the regeneration, when the Son of Man sits on the throne of his majesty, you also will sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel.'
When Solomon saw this judgment approaching for the reward of the holy Church, he said in the book of Proverbs, chapter thirty-one, verse twenty-three: 'Her husband is noble in the gates, when he sits with the senators of the land.' As if saying openly: Because he pronounces the sentence of the final judgment with the holy preachers of that same Church.

Chapter 29: The Resurrection of the Dead

There are some who are uncertain about the resurrection of the flesh, and when they see flesh reduced to rot and bones to dust through the graves, they doubt that flesh and bones can be restored from dust. And so, reasoning among themselves, they say: When is man brought back from dust? When does it happen that ash is animated?
It is far less for God to restore what has been than to create what has not been. Or what wonder is it if he raises man from dust, who created all things at once from nothing? It is more wonderful for God to have established heaven and earth from things that did not exist than to restore man himself from earth.
The ash of a dead man is observed in the grave, and it is despaired that it can return to flesh, and the power of divine operation is sought to be comprehended as if by reason. What wonder is it if God restores bones, sinews, flesh, and hair from dust, who daily restores wood, fruits, and leaves in the great mass of a tree from a small seed?
When therefore the doubting mind seeks a rational explanation for the power of resurrection, questions must be brought to it about those very things that are done continually yet cannot at all be comprehended by reason—so that while it cannot penetrate by seeing what it beholds, it may believe from the promise of divine power what it hears.
David the psalmist says in Psalm one hundred thirty-one, verse eight: 'Arise, Lord, into your rest, you and the ark of your sanctification.' For the Lord arose into his rest when he raised his flesh from the tomb. After him the ark also arises, because the Church rises.
Through the prophet Ezekiel, chapter seventeen, verses four and following, it says: 'Dry bones, hear the word of the Lord. Thus says the Lord God to these bones: Behold, I will cause spirit to enter into you, and you shall live.' Hence it is that another prophet saw the human race raised up at the end through resurrection and says in the prophet Hosea, chapter six, verse three: 'He will revive us after two days; on the third day he will raise us up, and we shall live in his sight.'
When the Lord spoke about himself, he said in the Gospel of John, chapter five, verses twenty-eight and twenty-nine: 'Do not marvel at this, for the hour is coming in which all who are in the tombs will hear his voice. And they will come forth—those who have done good, to the resurrection of life; but those who have done evil, to the resurrection of judgment.'
Hence Paul says in his letter to the Philippians, chapter three, verse twenty: 'From which also we await the Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ, who will transform the body of our humility, conformed to the body of his glory.' Hence again he says in his first letter to the Thessalonians, chapter four, verse fourteen: 'For if we believe that Jesus died and rose again, even so God will bring with him those who sleep through Jesus.' Who again says in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter fifteen, verse twenty: 'Christ the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep.' For if we do not rise from the sleep of death, how do we have the resurrection of the Lord as firstfruits?
Behold, the old and new Fathers agree with one spirit about the resurrection of the flesh. Behold, Truth itself first taught by voice what it afterward demonstrated by deed concerning the resurrection of the flesh; and yet still the weakness of some has no faith, though standing in the house of faith.
The very elements themselves, the very appearances of things, proclaim to us the image of resurrection. For the sun daily dies to our eyes and daily rises. The stars set for us in the morning hours and rise in the evening. We see trees full of leaves and fruits in summer, which in winter remain bare of leaves, flowers, and fruits, and as if dried up; but when the spring sun returns, when moisture rises from the root, they are clothed again in their beauty.
Why then is it doubted concerning men what is seen to happen in trees? Often faithless men, when they look at the dust of rotting flesh, say: From where will flesh and hair be able to be restored in the resurrection? Therefore, seeking these things, let them see the small seeds of huge trees, and if they can, let them say: Where in them is hidden such a mass of strength, such a diversity of branches, such a multitude and greenness of leaves, such an appearance of flowers, such an abundance, taste, and smell of fruits?
Do the seeds of trees have the smell or taste that the trees themselves afterward put forth in their fruits? If therefore it can be produced from a tree's seed, which cannot be seen, why is it doubted concerning the dust of human flesh that from it can be restored the form which is not seen?
If you cannot comprehend the effect of resurrection by reason, consider how many things there are that you do not understand how they are, and yet you do not doubt that they are. Tell me, I ask, if you know the circuit of heaven, the hinges of the earth, the abysses of the waters, where they end, where they are suspended? But we know that what was made from nothing hangs in nothing. Why do you doubt the glory of resurrection by disputing and examining it, you who have believed so many mysteries without examination?
Yet if you do not believe in the resurrection of the flesh, you have believed everything for nothing, because in this spectacle of angels you seem indeed to run swiftly, but after you have completed the course, you oppose receiving the prize. Through Paul the apostle it says in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter nine, verse twenty-four: 'Run in such a way that you may obtain it.' Who again says in chapter fifteen, verse nineteen: 'If in this life only we have hope in Christ, we are of all men most miserable.'
But the mysteries of divine power, which cannot be comprehended, are not to be examined by understanding but venerated by faith. We must know that whatever can be comprehended by human reason can no longer be wonderful, but the only reason in miracles is the power of the one who does them.
Just as a sleeping man, roused from sleep, awakens more quickly, so he rises swiftly at the Creator's nod from the death of the body. The excellent preacher calls the death of the Lord death, but the death of the Lord's servants he calls not death but sleep, because looking to the weak hearts of his hearers, he composes the medicine of his preaching with wonderful art; and he does not hesitate to suggest to them as dead him whom they knew had already risen.
But those who had not yet risen, to suggest the hope of resurrection, he calls not dead but sleeping. The Apostle was not afraid to say Christ was dead, whom his hearers knew had already risen, and he was afraid to say dead those whom they scarcely believed would rise.
In that glory of resurrection our body will be subtle indeed through the effect of spiritual power, but palpable through the truth of nature. Blessed Job, speaking of the true resurrection of the body, says in chapter nineteen, verse twenty-six: 'Again I shall be clothed with my skin, and in my flesh I shall see God, whom I myself shall behold, and my eyes shall look upon, and not another.'
Behold the true resurrection: behold he confesses with open words skin, behold flesh. What then remains by which our mind can doubt? But we, following the faith of blessed Job, and truly believing that our Redeemer's body after resurrection was palpable, confess that our flesh after resurrection will be both the same and different: the same by nature, different by power.
After resurrection our flesh will be subtle, it will be palpable, because it will not lose the essence of true nature. In the earth the dead descend, not in spirit but in body. Therefore they proceed in the sight of the Lord who descend into the earth, because they come to judgment by rising, who now rot in dust, as it is written in the Psalms, chapter twenty-one, verse twenty-nine: 'In his sight shall proceed all who descend into the earth.'
The same psalmist again says in Psalm sixty-two, verse two: 'My soul has thirsted for you, how manifold also has my flesh.' The soul thirsts to see God. The flesh, what does it thirst for but to rise again? Hence again he says in Psalm one hundred three, verse twenty-nine: 'Take away their spirit, and they will fail, and will return to their dust.' And immediately he adds about the resurrection of the flesh in verse thirty: 'Send forth your Spirit, God, and they shall be created, and you shall renew the face of the earth.'

Chapter 30: The Dreadful Judgment of the Eternal King

God the Almighty Son, when he comes as Judge of the dead and the living, will appear both gentle to the just and terrible to the unjust. Coming to judgment, he caresses the former through the gentleness of mercy, but terrifies the latter through the strictness of justice.
When a storm arises, first gentle waves, and afterward greater swellings are stirred up; finally the waves raise themselves to the heights, and overturn all navigators by their very height. So, so indeed that final tempest hastens, which will overthrow the entire world, a tempest of souls. The tempest of the coming judgment now shows us its beginnings with wars and calamities, like certain waves; and the closer we become daily to the end, the more we see heavier volumes of tribulations rushing in.
But finally, when all elements are shaken, the Judge from above coming brings the end of all, because then indeed the tempest lifts the waves to heaven; whence it is also said in the prophet Haggai, chapter two, verse seven: 'Yet once more, and I will shake not only the earth but also heaven.' This tempest, because holy men watchfully behold it, they tremble daily as if swelling waves were upon them, and from the tribulations that strike the world they foresee what will follow.
What will the dreadful Judge do when he comes in himself and his wrath burns for the vengeance of sins, if now it cannot be endured when he strikes us through the thinnest cloud? In the presence of his wrath what flesh will stand, if he moved the wind and overthrew the earth, stirred the air and laid low so many buildings? Paul, considering this strictness of the coming Judge, says in his letter to the Hebrews, chapter ten, verse three: 'It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of the living God.'
The psalmist expresses this, saying in Psalm forty-nine, verse three: 'God shall come manifestly, our God, and shall not be silent. Fire shall burn before him, and round about him a mighty tempest.' For the tempest and fire accompany the strictness of such justice, because the tempest examines those whom the fire will consume.
Through the prophet Zephaniah, chapter one, verses fourteen and following, it says: 'Near is the great day of the Lord, and exceedingly swift. The voice of the day of the Lord is bitter; the strong man shall be troubled there. That day is a day of wrath, a day of tribulation and distress, a day of calamity and misery, a day of darkness and obscurity, a day of clouds and whirlwind, a day of trumpet and alarm over all fortified cities and over all lofty corners.'
The day of the Lord full of vengeance and punishment comes upon fortified cities and upon lofty corners, because the wrath of the final judgment destroys human hearts both closed by defenses against truth and dissolves them wrapped in duplicities. Then fortified cities fall, because minds impenetrable to God will be condemned. Then lofty corners collapse, because hearts that raise themselves through the prudence of impurity are cast down by the sentence of justice.
Concerning this day of judgment the Lord again says through the prophet Haggai, chapter two, verse twenty-two: 'Yet once more, and I will shake not only the earth but also heaven.' If when he moved the air, the earth does not stand, who will endure when he moves heaven? The terrors we now see are heralds of the coming wrath.
Hence it is necessary to consider that these tribulations are as different from that final tribulation as the person of the herald is distant from the power of the judge. Therefore let us ponder that terrible day with all intentness, let us correct our life and conduct, let us conquer tempting evils by resisting, and let us punish those perpetrated with tears. For we will someday see the coming of the eternal Judge all the more securely as we now anticipate his strictness by fearing it.
In the examination of the dreadful Judge, all the elect, separated from the reprobate, will rejoice, because when final vengeance strikes all the wicked, they themselves rejoice in the glory of worthy retribution. The just, established in eternal life, do not then sympathize from humanity with the damned, because cleaving to divine justice through vision, they are made solid by the unshaken vigor of inner strictness.
It is hardly sufficient for the Redeemer of the human race for our edification that he declared he would say in the final judgment, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-five, verse forty records: 'As long as you did it to one of these my least brothers, you did it to me'—unless he also showed in himself before judgment what he had said, so that he might demonstrate that whoever now performs good works for the needy, renders them especially to him, for love of whom he has performed them.

Chapter 31: Fruitless Repentance of the Reprobate

When the eternal Judge comes, each of the reprobate is brought back to his heart, so that when he can no longer do holy things, he may feel it, and may know from the punishment of his perversity the right way he should have followed. Through the prophet Jeremiah, chapter twelve, verse three, it says: 'Gather them like a flock for sacrifice, and sanctify them in the day of slaughter.' For on the day of slaughter the reprobate are sanctified, because then they feel the holy things they should have done, when they can no longer avoid the punishments worthy of their wickedness.
All the reprobate then fear to be struck when they are struck by the final vengeance through the sentence of the Judge, and his terror then awakens them from the sleep of their torpor when vengeance disturbs them. Through the prophet Isaiah, chapter twenty-eight, verse nineteen, it says: 'And vexation alone will give understanding to the hearing.' For when the wicked begin to be scourged through vengeance for the commandments of God that were despised and rejected, then they understand what they heard.
And the psalmist says in Psalm seventy-seven, verse thirty-four: 'When he slew them, then they sought him.' All the wicked on the day of final judgment, having known their damnation, will say, as the book of Wisdom, chapter five, verse six records: 'We have erred from the way of truth, and the light of justice has not shone for us, and the sun has not risen upon us.' As if saying openly: The ray of inner light has not shone back to us.
The wicked man does not know the evils he has done unless he has already begun to be punished for those same evils. For then he understands what he heard, when he has already grieved to be vexed for his contempt, as it is written: 'And vexation alone will give understanding to the hearing.' The reprobate do not understand eternal things unless they are already punished with temporal things without amendment.
For then the mind burns, and inflames itself with the fires of fruitless repentance; it fears to be led to punishment, it holds onto the present life out of desire; but it is dissolved from its place, because abandoning the delights of the flesh, its hardness is melted through punishment.

Chapter 32: Damnation of the Devil and Demons

When the eternal Judge appears terribly, with legions of angels standing by, with all the ministry of heavenly powers assisting, and with all the elect brought to this spectacle, the devil—cruel and strong beast—is led captive into the midst, and with his body, that is, with all the reprobate, is consigned to the eternal fires of hell, when it is said in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-five, verse forty-one: 'Depart from me, you cursed, into the eternal fire that has been prepared for the devil and his angels.'
Oh what a spectacle it will be when this most monstrous beast is shown to the eyes of the elect, which in this time of war could have terrified them greatly if it had been seen! But it is done by the hidden and wonderful counsel of God that now through his grace it is conquered by the fighters without being seen, and then is seen as already captive by the joyful victors.
Then the just more fully recognize how much they are debtors to divine help, when they will have seen so strong a beast, which they have now conquered while weak. And in the enormity of their enemy they see how much they owe to the grace of their defender.
The ancient enemy, bound by the chains of his wickedness, suffers one thing now and another at the end, because since he fell from the order of intimate light, he now confounds himself inwardly with the darkness of error. The ancient enemy at the time of his damnation is wrapped in bitterness, because from the merit of spontaneous darkness he is tortured by the eternal torment of hell.
Rightly blessed Job says of Antichrist in chapter three, verse five: 'Let darkness seize upon him, let him be wrapped in bitterness.' For everything that is wrapped indicates its end nowhere, as it were, because just as it does not show where it begins, so it does not reveal where it ends. Therefore the ancient enemy is said to be wrapped in bitterness, because the punishments of his pride are prepared not only in every way but also infinitely.
The punishment of Antichrist then receives its beginning when the Judge comes strictly to final judgment; whence it is also well added in verse six: 'Let a dark whirlwind possess that night.' Therefore the dark whirlwind possesses this night, because that fearful tempest snatches the apostate spirit from the sight of the strict Judge to eternal punishments. Therefore this night is possessed by the whirlwind, because its proud blindness is struck by strict punishment.

Chapter 33: The Eternal Punishments of the Reprobate

The justice of almighty God, foreknowing the future, created the fire of hell from the very origin of the world—fire that would once begin to exist in the punishment of the reprobate, but would never end its burning, even without wood. It must be known that all the reprobate who sinned with both soul and flesh will there be tortured in eternal punishment equally in soul and flesh.
Through David the prophet it says in Psalm twenty, verse ten: 'You will make them as an oven of fire in the time of your countenance.' For an oven burns inwardly. But one who is devoured by fire begins to be consumed from the outer part. Therefore, so that the sacred words might demonstrate that the reprobate burn both outwardly and inwardly, they testify that they are both devoured by fire and placed like an oven, so that they are tortured by fire in the body and burn through pain in the mind.
