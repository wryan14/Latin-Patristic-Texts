
by Taio of Zaragoza
Patrologia Latina Volume 


Book Three. Chapter One: God's Will and Permission.

The just and merciful God, ordering the actions of mortals, grants some things in kindness and permits others in wrath. Yet even what he permits, he tolerates in such a way that he turns it to serve his purpose.
In a marvelous way, what happens without God's will does not contradict his will—because when evil deeds are turned to good use, even things that resist his plan end up serving his plan.
As Scripture says: The works of the Lord are great, sought out in all his purposes—from the Psalms, chapter one hundred ten, verse two.
His works are great in precisely this way: that through everything people do, his will is accomplished. Often what seemed like it would thwart his purpose actually brings it to fulfillment.
Again Scripture says: Whatever the Lord willed, he accomplished in heaven and on earth—from the Psalms, chapter one hundred thirty-four, verse six.
From this Solomon declares: There is no wisdom, no prudence, no counsel against the Lord—from Proverbs, chapter twenty-one, verse thirty-nine.
Therefore, in everything we do, we should search out the force of God's heavenly will. Once we recognize it, our actions should serve it devotedly and follow it as the guide of our journey—lest by proudly turning away from it, we end up serving it unwillingly anyway.
The power of God's plan cannot be avoided. But the person who restrains himself beneath God's direction controls that power with great virtue. And whoever willingly bears God's plan on the humble shoulder of his heart lightens its weight for himself.

Chapter Two: God's Dispensation.

Great is the dispensation of almighty God. It often happens that to those he gives greater gifts, he does not grant certain smaller ones—so that their soul always has something for which to reproach itself.
Often the chosen desire to be perfect, but cannot. They work hard with what they have received from the Lord, yet even working hard, they do not prevail. But in what they have received, they do not exalt themselves—and they learn that they do not possess greater goods from themselves, since they cannot conquer even small faults by themselves.
When the Lord brought his people to the promised land and destroyed all their strong and mighty enemies, he nevertheless reserved the Philistines and Canaanites for a longer time—so that, as Scripture says in the book of Judges, chapter three, verse four, Israel might be tested through them.
Sometimes, as we have said, the Lord leaves certain small faults even in those to whom he grants great gifts—so they always have something to fight against, and after conquering great enemies, they do not become proud when even small adversaries still harass them.
In a marvelous way, one and the same mind both flourishes with virtue and grows weary with weakness—so that it is partly built up and partly sees itself torn down. Through the good it seeks but cannot possess, it humbly preserves what it does have.
Why is it surprising that we say this about human beings, when even that heavenly realm suffered partial losses among its citizens and partly stood firm? The chosen angelic spirits, seeing others fall through pride, stood all the more strongly precisely because they stood more humbly.
So it is also with each individual soul: sometimes in the safeguard of humility, it is preserved for the greatest gains through the smallest loss.
Often those who already shine with great virtues still endure something resisting them from the darkness of fault—so that even while they shine with great clarity of life, they still unwillingly drag along remnants of night.
It is accomplished by the great dispensation of almighty God that a mind advancing toward the virtue of righteousness is better strengthened by its own weakness, and shines more truly in goodness precisely because small faults humbly obscure it even against its will.
When the land of promise was divided among the Israelite people, the Canaanite gentile population was not killed but made tributary to the tribe of Ephraim, as it is written in the book of Joshua, chapter sixteen, verse ten: The Canaanite dwelt in the midst of Ephraim as a tributary.
What does the Canaanite—that is, a gentile people—signify if not vice? And often we enter the land of promise through great virtues, because we are strengthened by inner hope concerning eternity.
But when amid sublime deeds we retain certain small vices, we allow the Canaanite, so to speak, to live in our land. The Canaanite therefore becomes tributary, because the very vice we cannot conquer, we humbly twist to our own advantage—so that precisely because the mind cannot conquer even the small things it desires by its own strength, it feels itself worthless even in the highest things.

Chapter Three: How Sacred Scripture Pronounces the Sentence of Curse.

Since we know with certainty that sacred Scripture forbids cursing, why do we say that sometimes what we know should be avoided according to that same sacred text is rightly done?
But we must understand that sacred Scripture mentions cursing in two ways: one that it approves, another that it condemns. A curse is pronounced one way by the judgment of justice, another way by the spite of vengeance.
A curse by the judgment of justice was pronounced when the first human being sinned, when he heard: Cursed is the earth in your works—from Genesis, chapter three, verse seventeen.
A curse is pronounced by the judgment of justice when it is said to Abraham: I will curse those who curse you—from Genesis, chapter twelve, verse three.
Again, because a curse is uttered not by the judgment of justice but by the spite of revenge, we are warned by the voice of Paul the preacher, who says in his letter to the Romans, chapter twelve, verse fourteen: Bless, and do not curse. And again in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter six, verse ten: Neither will cursers possess the kingdom of God.
Almighty God is said to curse, and yet a human being is forbidden to curse—because what a human being does through the malice of revenge, God does not do except through the examination and power of justice.
When holy men pronounce a sentence of curse, they do not break forth into it from a vow of vengeance, but from the justice of examination. For inwardly they see God's subtle judgment, and they recognize that evils arising outwardly deserve to be struck with a curse. And they do not sin in cursing precisely because they do not disagree with the internal judgment.
The apostle Peter hurled a sentence of curse against Simon who offered him money, saying in the book of Acts, chapter eight, verse twenty: May your money go with you to destruction.
For the one who did not say 'is' but 'may it be' indicated that he spoke these words not in the indicative mood but in the optative.
Likewise Elijah said to two captains of fifty coming to him, as recorded in the fourth book of Kings, chapter one, verse ten: If I am a man of God, let fire descend from heaven and consume you.
The outcome of the case showed with what rational truth the sentence of both prevailed. For Simon perished in eternal destruction, and the flame coming from above consumed the two captains of fifty.
The power that follows testifies with what mind the sentence of curse is uttered. For when both the innocence of the one cursing remains, and yet the curse absorbs unto destruction the one who is cursed, it is gathered from the outcome of both parties that the sentence is hurled against the guilty party taken from the one intimate judge.
If we carefully weigh the words of each blessed man regarding how he pronounces the sentence of curse in sacred Scripture, his curse does not arise from the malice of an offender but from the righteousness of a judge—not the anger of one disturbed but the teaching of one tranquil.
For the one who pronounced such a righteous curse did not succumb to the vice of disturbance but exercised the office of teaching.

Chapter Four: The Five Ages of the World and of Human Beings.

It is very important to know that just as there are stages of growth in the body, so also are there stages of growth in the mind. The first age of a human being is infancy, when even if one lives innocently, one does not yet know how to speak about the innocence one has.
And then follows childhood, in which one is already able to say what one wants. This is succeeded by adolescence, which is indeed the first age in action. Then follows youth, that is, the age suited to strength. And afterward old age, now suitable in time to maturity.
We know adolescence to be the first age suited to good deeds. And just men, when they advance in great maturity of mind, sometimes bring back to memory the beginning of their actions. And they reproach themselves about their beginnings precisely to the degree that they have advanced higher in weightiness of mind—because they find themselves to have been indiscrete precisely to the degree that they afterward more fully attain the summit of discretion.
Morning is, so to speak, the childhood of our understanding. The third hour can be understood as adolescence, because the sun is already advancing on high, as it were, while the warmth of age increases.
The sixth hour is youth, because the sun is fixed as if in the center while in it the fullness of strength is solidified. The ninth hour is understood as old age, in which the sun descends as if from its high axis, because age fails from the heat of youth.
The eleventh hour is that age which is called decrepit or very old. Hence the Greeks call the very elderly not gerontas but presbyteros, to suggest that those they call more advanced are more than merely old.
As infancy passes to childhood, childhood to adolescence, adolescence to youth, youth to old age, and old age passes to death, in the course of present life the human being is driven toward losses by his very growth. And he always declines precisely from the source where he believes himself to be advancing in the span of life.
We cannot have a fixed state in this world, where we have come in order to pass through. And this very living of ours is a daily passing away from life.
For the mind is always driven by the weight of its changeability to something other than what it is. And unless it is held in its state by the strict discipline of custody, it always slips into worse things.
For what always abandoned its standing has lost the stability it could have had. When one person is led to the good life in childhood, another in adolescence, another in youth, another in old age, and yet another in decrepit age, they are called as workers to the vineyard, as it were, at different hours.
From righteous Abel to the last chosen one who will be born at the end of the world, the holy Church has sent forth as many branches, so to speak, as it has brought forth saints.
The gospel's master of the household hires workers to cultivate his vineyard at morning, at the third hour, at the sixth, at the ninth, and at the eleventh hour—because from the beginning of this world until its end, he does not cease to gather preachers to instruct the people of the faithful.
Morning of the world was with the following hour from Adam to Noah. The third hour was from Noah to Abraham. The sixth from Abraham to Moses. The ninth from Moses to the coming of the Lord. And the eleventh from the coming of the Lord to the end of the world.
In this hour, the holy apostles were sent as preachers, who received full wages though coming late. Therefore the Lord, to instruct his people—as if to cultivate his vineyard—never ceased at any time to send workers. For both earlier through the Fathers, and afterward through teachers of the law and prophets, and finally through the apostles, he cultivated the customs of his people and labored greatly, as through workers, in the cultivation of his vineyard.

Chapter Five: The Brevity and Misery of Present Life.

What are the nations in the world but certain flowers in a field? Let us direct the eyes of our heart toward this breadth of the present world, and behold, it is full of as many flowers, so to speak, as there are human beings.
Life in the flesh is therefore a flower in grass. Hence it is well said by the Psalmist in the Psalms, chapter one hundred two, verse fifteen: A person's days are like grass, and he will flower like the flower of the field. Isaiah also says in chapter forty, verse six: All flesh is grass, and all its glory is like the flower of the field.
For a human being proceeds from the hidden like a flower and suddenly appears in public—but is immediately withdrawn from public through death back to the hidden. The greenness of flesh displays us, but the dryness of dust withdraws us from sight.
We appeared like a flower when we were not, we wither like a flower when we appeared temporarily. And because moment by moment a human being is daily driven toward death, it is rightly said in the book of blessed Job, chapter fourteen, verse two: And he flees like a shadow, and never remains in the same state.
A human being here has received strength for living only briefly, in order to pass into perpetuity where no limit closes off his life. But in this brevity where he is strengthened, he gathers what he will find in perpetuity—either to rejoice forever or to not escape the punishments he has received.
Everything that passes is brief, even if it seems to end more slowly. We walk on the path of death through which we do not return—not that we will not be brought back to life in the flesh by rising again, but that we will not come again to the labors of this mortal life or to acquire the rewards of labor.
The person who considers what he will be like in death always becomes timid in action—and precisely because in his own eyes he already lives as if he were not living, in the eyes of his Creator he truly lives.
He desires nothing that passes away, he contradicts all desires of present life. And he considers himself almost dead, because he by no means forgets that he is going to die. Perfect life is the imitation of death, which when the just carefully perform, they escape the snares of faults.
Hence it is written in the book of Sirach, chapter seven, verse forty: In all your works remember your last things, and you will never sin.
However long the time of present life may be, it is brief precisely because it is not lasting. For it is not worthy to be judged long-lasting when it is limited by an end.
Blessed Job, considering the misery of the human condition, says in chapter fourteen, verse one: A human being born of woman, living a brief time, is filled with many miseries.
Behold, by the voices of holy men the suffering of human beings is briefly expressed: because it is narrowed toward life and expanded toward misery. For if we consider carefully, everything that is done here is suffering and misery.
For to serve the very corruption of the flesh for necessities and what is allowed is misery: that against cold garments must be sought, against hunger food, against heat coolness.
The health of the body is guarded with much caution, but often even when guarded it is lost; when lost it is restored with heavy labor; and yet when restored it always remains in doubt. What is this other than the misery of mortal life?
We love friends who can be offended by suspicion. We fear enemies, and we are certainly not secure about those we fear.
Often we speak confidently to enemies as if to friends. And sometimes we receive the pure words of neighbors, and perhaps those who love us much, as if they were the words of enemies. And we who never want to be deceived or to deceive, through our caution we err more gravely.
The misery of human life is this: that having lost the heavenly homeland, the rejected human being delights in exile, is weighed down by cares, and yet pretends not to think how heavy it is because many things are being thought. That he is deprived of internal light, and yet wants to endure his blindness for a long time in this life—what is this other than misery born from our punishment?
But although he desires to remain here for a long time, he is nevertheless driven by the very course of mortal life to depart.
All the goods of present life are few, however many they may seem. But then the faithful servant is set over many things, when, having conquered all the troubles of corruption, he glories in eternal joys in that heavenly dwelling.
Then the servant is perfectly brought into his Lord's joy when, taken up into that eternal homeland and mingled with the companies of angels, he rejoices so inwardly from the gift that there is no longer anything outwardly to grieve him about corruption.
The glory of the wicked, when it often extends over a multitude of years, is estimated by weak minds to be long and almost stable. But when sudden death interrupts it, it is proven without doubt to have been brief—because when it ends it becomes known that what could pass away was small.

Chapter Six: Youth and Old Age.

In the youth of a human being the body flourishes, the chest remains strong and uninjured, the neck is muscular, the arms are full. But in the elderly the empty frame is bent, the dried neck droops, the chest is pressed with frequent sighs, strength fails, breathing interrupts the words of the speaker. For if illness is absent, often for the elderly their very health is sickness.
The world in earlier years flourished as if in youth, was robust for propagating the offspring of the human race, green with the health of bodies, fat with the abundance of things. But now it is pressed down by its own old age, and as if toward approaching death it is pressed by growing troubles.
Do not love the aging world, which you see cannot stand for long. Place the apostolic precepts in your mind, by which he warns us, saying in the first letter of John, chapter two, verse fifteen: Do not love the world, nor the things that are in the world—for if anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him.

Chapter Seven: The Married.

In the practice of mortality, some things are harmful in themselves, but others are harmful from the things that revolve around them. Some things are harmful in themselves, such as sins and crimes. But some things sometimes harm us from what surrounds them, such as temporal power or the marriage bond.
Marriage is good, but the things that grow around it from the care of this world are bad. Hence the apostle Paul says in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter seven, verse thirty-three: But the one who is with a wife thinks about the things of the world, how to please his wife.
Persuading certain ones to better things, the same apostle Paul calls them back from marriage, saying in verse thirty-five: But I say this not to cast a snare upon you, but for what is honorable and what provides the ability to beseech the Lord without impediment.
Therefore while what does not harm is held, from things placed nearby what does harm is often committed. Often we proceed on a straight and clean path, yet we are held back by our garments on thorns that have grown beside the road. We do not stumble on the clean road itself, but from the side there arises what pricks us.
Those who cling to carnal union are, as it were, stationed on a mountain, but who apart from the due mingling for receiving offspring are not released by any pleasure of the flesh.
To stand on a mountain is not to seek the fruit of offspring in the flesh. To stand on a mountain is not to adhere to flesh carnally. Many are those who abandon the crimes of flesh, and yet being placed in marriage, they preserve only the rights of what is owed.
Lot indeed went out from Sodom, but nevertheless did not immediately arrive at the mountains—because now the damnable life is abandoned, but the height of conjugal continence is not yet subtly maintained.
There is in the middle the city of Segor, which saves the weak one fleeing—because indeed when spouses mingle with each other through incontinence, they both flee the falls of crimes and yet are saved by pardon.
They find, as it were, a small city in which they are defended from fires—because this conjugal life is not indeed marvelous in virtues, but nevertheless secure from punishments.
Lot, prefiguring the continence of conjugal life, as he fled from Sodom, said to the angel as recorded in Genesis, chapter nineteen, verse twenty: There is the city of Segor, to which I can flee; it is small, and I will be saved in it. Is it not modest, and will not my soul live in it?
It is therefore called small, and yet it is attested as secure for salvation, because conjugal life is neither far divided from the world nor yet alien from the joy of salvation.
Then in the action of carnal union, spouses guard their life as in the small city of Segor when they intercede for themselves with constant prayers.
Rightly it is said by the angel to the same Lot in verse twenty-one: Behold, even in this I have received your prayers, that I will not overthrow the city about which you have spoken—because indeed when prayer is poured out to God, such a life of marriage is by no means condemned.
Concerning this prayer Paul warns, saying in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter seven, verse five: Do not defraud one another, except perhaps by consent for a time, that you may be free for prayer.
The apostle Paul, when he learned that some were living incontinently, admonished them saying in verse two: But because of fornication, let each man have his own wife, and let each woman have her own husband.
Since a wife should not be had except for the sake of begetting children, lest anyone slide into the fault of fornication, he granted to spouses something from which they could still rise to better things.
The same Paul, outstanding preacher, says about spouses in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter seven, verse twenty-eight: Such will have tribulation in the flesh.
But those who live spiritually can also suffer tribulation of the flesh here. Why then is tribulation of the flesh said to be in spouses almost specially, when it is not far even from the life of spiritual people, except that those who delight in pleasures of the flesh more frequently receive greater tribulations from the flesh?
We who carry the care of pastoral office, when we see someone wandering and slippery, he should be admonished to strive to bridle his iniquity by marriage, so that through what is permitted he may learn to overcome what is not permitted.
But when we see one who is married, he should be admonished to exercise care for the world in such a way that he does not postpone the love of God—to please the pleasure of his spouse in such a way that he does not displease his Creator.

Chapter Eight: Virgins and the Continent.

Those ignorant of the sins of the flesh should fear headlong ruin all the more carefully the higher they stand. Let them know therefore that the more they stand in a prominent place, the more they are assailed by the more frequent arrows of the insidiator, who is usually aroused all the more ardently the more robustly he sees himself conquered; and he disdains to be conquered all the more intolerably because he sees the intact camps of weak flesh fighting against him.
Those preserving chastity should be admonished to look unceasingly upon the rewards, and gladly without doubt to trample the labors of the temptations they endure. For if the happiness that is attained without passing away is considered, what is labored in passing becomes light.
Let those ignorant of the sins of the flesh hear what is said through the prophet in Isaiah, chapter fifty-six, verses four and five: Thus says the Lord to eunuchs: Who keep my sabbaths, and choose what I will, and hold my covenant, I will give them in my house and within my walls a place and a name better than sons and daughters.
For eunuchs are those who, having compressed the movements of the flesh, cut off in themselves the affection for evil work. What place they hold with the Father is shown, because in the Father's house, that is, in the eternal dwelling, they are even preferred to sons.
Through John in the book of Revelation, chapter fourteen, verse four, it is said: These are they who have not been defiled with women, for they are virgins, who follow the Lamb wherever he goes—and that they sing a song that no one can sing except those one hundred forty-four thousand.
For to sing a song singularly to the Lamb is to rejoice with him forever before all the faithful even in the incorruption of the flesh. Yet the other elect can hear this song, though they cannot sing it—because through charity they indeed rejoice in their height, although they do not rise to their rewards.
Let those ignorant of the sins of the flesh hear what Truth himself says about this integrity in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter nineteen, verse eleven: Not all receive this word.
This was made known to be supreme precisely by denying it to all; and while he predicts that it is received with difficulty, he hints to hearers with what caution what has been begun should be held.
Those ignorant of the sins of the flesh should be admonished both to know that virginity is preeminent over marriage, and yet not to exalt themselves above married people—so that while they prefer virginity and postpone themselves, they both do not abandon what they judge to be better, and they guard themselves so that they do not vainly exalt themselves.
They should be admonished to consider that often the life of the continent is put to shame by the action of secular people, when both those assume works beyond their habit, and these do not stir up their hearts according to their proper order.
Hence it is well said through the prophet in Isaiah, chapter twenty-three, verse four: Be ashamed, Sidon, says the sea.
For Sidon is brought to shame, as it were, by the voice of the sea, when by comparison with the life of secular people and those fluctuating in this world, the life of one who is fortified and seems almost stable is reproved.
Often some returning to the Lord after sins of the flesh show themselves all the more ardently in good works the more condemnable they see themselves in evil things. And often certain ones persevering in integrity of flesh, when they see themselves having less to weep over, think their life's innocence fully suffices for them, and they inflame themselves with no goads of ardor toward fervor of spirit.
Often a life burning with love after fault becomes more pleasing to God than an innocence torpid with security.
Hence also by the voice of the Judge it is said in the Gospel of Luke, chapter seven, verse forty-seven: Her many sins are forgiven because she loved much. And in chapter fifteen, verse ten: There will be joy in heaven over one sinner doing penance more than over ninety-nine just persons who need no penance.
We gather this more quickly from the thing itself, if we weigh the judgments of our mind. For we love more the land that, plowed after thorns, produces abundant fruits, than that which had no thorns but nevertheless when cultivated produces a sterile harvest.
Those ignorant of the sins of the flesh should be admonished not to prefer themselves to others by the height of superior order, since they do not know how much better they are conducted by those who are inferior.
For in the examination of the righteous Judge, the quality of actions changes the merits of orders. Who, considering the very images of things, does not know that in the nature of gems the carbuncle is preferred to the hyacinth?
But nevertheless the hyacinth of blue color is preferred to the pale carbuncle, because both what the order of nature withdraws, the appearance of beauty adds to that one, and this one, whom the natural order had preferred, the quality of color defiles.
So therefore in the human race some are worse in a better order, some are better in a worse order—because both these transcend the lot of the lowest habit by living well, and those diminish the merit of a higher place by not following through in morals.
The white life of the flesh is like a lily giving off sweet fragrance from the incorruption of virginity, like the flower of a lily from the fruit of true honesty.
There are many continent persons who guard themselves from external appetite, and are seized by hope toward interior things, they macerate the flesh and pant with whole desire toward the heavenly homeland, they seek eternal rewards, they do not wish to receive human praises for their labors.
These assuredly do not place their glory in the honor of people, but they conceal it within their conscience.
The purity of chastity must be preserved through the custody of humility. For if the spirit is piously pressed under God, the flesh is not unlawfully raised above the spirit.
For the spirit has dominion over the flesh committed to it, if indeed under the Lord it recognizes the rights of legitimate servitude.

Chapter Nine: Those Recently Converted from the World.

There are some who flee the actions of the world but are exercised in no virtues. These indeed sleep in torpor, not in zeal, and therefore they do not perceive internal things because they have placed their head not on a stone but on the earth.
To whom it often happens that the more securely they cease from external actions, the more widely they heap up in themselves the noise of unclean thought through idleness.
In Deuteronomy, chapter fifteen, verse nineteen, it is written: You shall not plow with the firstborn of the ox, and you shall not shear the firstborn of the sheep.
What did Moses prohibit by the example of this prohibition, except that the life of those beginning well should be exercised in human occupations? For to plow with the firstborn of the ox is to show the beginnings of good conversion in the exercise of public action.
To shear the firstborn of the sheep is to denude our beginning goods from the covering of their concealment to human eyes. We are forbidden to work with the firstborn of the ox, and we are restrained from shearing the firstborn of the sheep—because even if we begin something robust, we should not exercise it openly too quickly. And when our life begins something simple and innocent, it is fitting that it not abandon the veils of its secrecy, lest it show this naked to human eyes as if the fleece were removed.

Chapter fourteen: Holy Men Devoted to Active and Contemplative Life

The more just men desire to cling to heavenly things through the ray of contemplation, the more they refuse to build on earth, where they know themselves to be pilgrims and strangers. Paul attests to this when he says in his letter to the Philippians, chapter three, verse twenty: Our citizenship is in heaven. And again, in his second letter to the Corinthians, chapter five, verse one: You are being built into a house not made with hands, eternal in the heavens.
When holy men desire to rejoice in what is truly their own, they refuse to be happy in what belongs to another. But the unjust, the further they are divided from the inheritance of their eternal homeland, the deeper they plant the foundations of their thoughts in earthly soil.
The people of the elect rise through some into contemplation, but through others grow strong only in the works of the active life. Therefore it is rightly said through Isaac in the book of Genesis, chapter twenty-seven, verse twenty-eight: May the Lord give you of the dew of heaven and of the fatness of the earth. For dew falls subtly from above, and we receive from the dew of heaven as often as we see something thinly of the things above through the infusion of intimate contemplation.
But when we do good works even through the body, we are enriched by the fatness of the earth. The patriarch Jacob, sleeping on his journey, saw a ladder extended from earth to heaven, and angels ascending and descending on it, and the Lord leaning upon the ladder, as recorded in Genesis chapter twenty-eight, verse eleven.
To sleep on a journey is to rest from the love of temporal things in this passage of present life, to close the eyes of the mind from desire for visible things in the course of fleeting days. To see angels ascending and descending is to contemplate the citizens of the heavenly homeland, both with what love they cling to their creator above themselves, and with what compassion of charity they condescend to our weaknesses.
It should be especially noted that he who places his head on a stone while sleeping beholds angels, because he who ceases from external works penetrates inward things when, with focused mind, which is the principal part of man, he observes the imitation of his Redeemer. To place one's head on the stone is to cling to Christ with the mind.
For those who are removed from the action of present life but are drawn to heavenly things by no love can sleep, but cannot see angels, because they scorn to hold their head upon the stone.
Holy men do not cease to mortify themselves with the sword of the sacred word from the importunity of temporal desires, from the tumult of useless cares, from the clamor of resounding disturbances, and they hide themselves within, before the face of God, in the bosom of the mind. Therefore it is rightly said through the Psalmist in Psalm thirty, verse twenty-one: You will hide them in the secret of your face from the disturbance of men.
Though this is done perfectly later, even now it is accomplished in great part when they are caught up by delight from the tumults of temporal desires into interior things, so that while their mind is entirely directed toward the love of God, it is torn by no useless disturbance.
The apostle Paul through contemplation of mind had seen disciples dead and hidden as if in a tomb, to whom he said in his letter to the Colossians, chapter three, verses three and four: For you are dead, and your life is hidden with Christ in God. For he who desires to mortify himself rejoices greatly at the rest of contemplation he has found, so that, dead to the world, he may lie hidden and hide himself from all disturbances of external things within the bosom of intimate love.
Holy David, contemplating the eternal homeland and weighing these evils in which he was, and considering those goods in which he was not yet, said in Psalm thirty, verse twenty-three: I said in my fear, I am cast away from the sight of your eyes. Lifted up in ecstasy, what our translators did not properly call fear, he saw himself cast away from the sight of God's eyes, because every elect person, after the vision of interior light which flashed in his soul through the grace of contemplation with a ray of brightness, returns to himself and, with knowledge received, discovers what goods he lacked there or what evils he had here.
This compunction usually affects the perfect soul more intimately, because it shakes off all bodily imaginations insolently opposing it, and strives to fix the eye of the heart on the very ray of unbounded light.
For it has drawn these forms of bodily figures inward to itself from the infirmity of the body, but when perfectly contrite, it watches most carefully lest, when it seeks truth, imagination of limited vision deceive it.
Sometimes the mind of the just is admitted to a certain unusual sweetness of interior savor, and suddenly in some way, inspired by an ardent spirit, it is renewed. And it loves all the more, the more it tastes what it loves. And it desires within itself what it has inwardly felt to taste sweet, because by love of this sweetness it has become worthless to itself before itself. And after it has been able to perceive this somehow, it discovers what it would have been long ago without it.
Every holy man already strives to cling to unbounded truth, but he is still repelled from its strength by his own weakness. And because he cannot be conformed to its purity, he finds it sweet to weep and to lay down the tears of his weakness for himself as he falls back to himself.
For he cannot fix the eye of his mind on what he glimpsed suddenly within himself, because by the very habit of his old nature he is compelled to go downward. Amid these things he gasps, he burns, he strives to rise above himself, but he slides back to familiar darkness, overcome by weariness.
Well is it said through the Psalmist in Psalm thirty, verse twenty-three: I said in my fear, I am cast away from the sight of your eyes. For having contemplated the interior joys of the vision of God and the social company of the abiding angels, he brought his eyes back to the lowest things. He saw where he lay, because he had been created for this, that he might stand in heavenly things. And he groaned at what he was.
And he grieved that he was cast away from the sight of God's eyes, because by comparison with the lowest things to the intimate light, he felt more heavily the darkness of his exile which he endured.

Chapter fifteen: The Elect Living Well Amid the Tumults of the Reprobate

Many of us complain why not all who live with us are good. We do not want to bear the evils of our neighbors, we decree that all should already be saints, while we do not want to be what we should bear from our neighbors.
But in this matter it is clearer than light how much we ourselves still lack of goodness. For no one is perfectly good unless he has been good even among the wicked.
Blessed Job asserts of himself, saying in the book of Job, chapter thirty, verse twenty-nine: I was a brother of dragons and a companion of ostriches. Hence Paul the apostle says to his disciples in his letter to the Philippians, chapter two, verse fifteen: In the midst of a crooked and perverse generation, among whom you shine as lights in the world.
Hence Peter, shepherd of the Lord's flock, says in his second letter, chapter two, verse seven: He delivered righteous Lot, oppressed by the injury of the wicked in their conduct. For by what he saw and heard, that righteous man dwelling among them was tormented in his righteous soul day by day by their lawless deeds.
When we often complain about the lives of our neighbors, we desire to change location, we strive to choose the secrecy of a more remote life, clearly ignorant that if the spirit is lacking, place does not help. For Lot was indeed holy in Sodom, but sinned on the mountain, as recorded in Genesis chapter nineteen, verse two and following.
But that places do not fortify the mind, the first parent of the human race himself testifies, who even in paradise fell gravely, as Genesis chapter three, verse seven records. But all that we speak from earth is less. For if place could have saved, Satan would not have fallen from heaven.
The Psalmist David, seeing that temptations are everywhere in this world, sought a place to which he might flee, but without God he could not find a fortified one. For which reason he asked that God himself become a place for him, on account of which place he sought, saying in Psalm thirty, verse three: Be to me a God who protects and a fortified place, that you may make me safe.
In the Song of Songs chapter two, verse two, it is written: As a lily among thorns, so is my beloved among daughters. As if openly saying: These are truly good who can persist in goodness even among the wicked.
It is not greatly praiseworthy to be good with the good, but to be good with the wicked. Just as it is a graver fault not to be good among the good, so it is of immense praise to have been good even among the wicked.

Chapter sixteen: The Pressures of the Elect

For one living well, the strict standard is not a wide road but a narrow path, in which each elect person is carefully constrained, because he is anxiously narrowed under the custody of precepts.
It is like a certain narrowness of journey to live indeed in this world but to have nothing of this world's desire, not to seek what belongs to others, not to hold what is one's own, to despise the praises of the world and to love reproaches for God's sake, to flee glory and seek contempt, to despise flatterers, to honor those who despise, to dismiss from the heart the evils of those who harm, and to retain toward them an immovable grace of love in the heart.
All these are indeed narrow paths, but great ones, because the more narrow they are in present life for the very custody of living, the more widely they expand in eternal reward.
The wickedness of the reprobate, in the manner of threshing, presses and purifies the life of the elect, as if separating grain from chaff. For the wicked free the good more from the desires of this world while afflicting them, because while they inflict many violent things on them here, they compel them to hasten to heavenly things.
It was well prefigured by the Israelite people, with Moses calling and Pharaoh the king raging, as recorded in Exodus chapter three, verse seven. For then Moses was sent to call that same people when Pharaoh had already been aroused to oppress with harsh works, so that the minds of the Israelites, clinging deformedly to Egypt, one might draw as he called, the other might push as he raged. And the people, fixed shamefully in servitude, would be moved either provoked by goods or impelled by evils.
This is done daily in this world, while the reprobate are permitted to rage against the elect when heavenly rewards are preached, so that if we, called to the land of promise, neglect to depart, we may at least be impelled by raging pressures. And this Egypt, namely present life, which oppressed us by flattering, helps by pressing. And what, while it cherishes, crushes with the yoke of servitude, while it torments, reveals the way of liberty.
Nor is it without cause that the just are permitted to be afflicted by the unjust, so that while they hear of future goods which they may desire, they may also suffer present evils which they may dread, and toward an easier departure, while love provokes, torment may impel.
The Psalmist David says in Psalm thirty-one, verse seven: You are my refuge from the pressure that surrounded me, my exultation, redeem me from those surrounding me. Behold, he names pressure as if in night, and yet calls his deliverer exultation amid distresses. Outside indeed was night in the encirclement of pressure, but within songs resounded from the consolation of joy.
Every elect person, while he narrates that he is surrounded by pressures and yet names God to be his exultation, without doubt sings as if a song in the night, that he may arrive happy at the day of the life to come.

Chapter seventeen: The Miracles of the Elect

The holy Church daily performs miracles spiritually which in ancient times it performed bodily through the holy apostles, as it is written in the Gospel of Mark, chapter sixteen, verse seventeen: These signs will follow those who believe: in my name they will cast out demons, they will speak in new tongues, they will take up serpents; and if they drink anything deadly, it will not harm them; they will lay hands on the sick, and they will recover.
When the priests of the Church impose hands on believers through the grace of exorcism and forbid evil spirits to dwell in their minds, what else do they do but cast out demons?
All the faithful who now abandon the worldly words of the old life and resound holy mysteries, narrating the praises and power of their Creator as much as they prevail, what else do they do but speak in new tongues?
Therefore the elect, when they remove malice from the hearts of others through their good exhortations, take up serpents. Many of the faithful, when they hear pestilent persuasions but are not at all drawn to evil action, drink what is deadly, but it will not harm them.
As often as the faithful of the holy Church see their neighbors weakening in good work and strengthen their life by the example of their own action, which was faltering in its own action, what else do they do but lay hands on the sick so they may recover?
Therefore the miracles which the elect work are all the greater because they are spiritual; they are all the more wonderful because through these not bodies but souls are raised up.
By God's authorship we see signs frequently occurring in the Church, yet from those exterior signs a holy life cannot be obtained by the one working them. For those bodily miracles sometimes show holiness, they do not make it. Spiritual miracles, which are done in the mind, do not show the virtue of life, but make it.
Even the wicked can have those, but these can be enjoyed only by the good. The exterior miracles which were done through the apostles were necessary in the beginning of the Church. For so that faith might grow, it needed to be nourished by miracles, because even we, when we plant shrubs, pour water on them so long, until we see that they have already grown strong in the earth.
But if they have once fixed their root, watering ceases. Hence it is that Paul says in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter fourteen, verse twenty-two: Tongues are a sign not for believers but for unbelievers. And when they see new powers in them, they later marvel at the miracles of those whose life they previously despised.
All unbelievers, seeing the miracles of the apostles, soon returning fearfully to their hearts, feared the holiness in miracles which they had despised in precepts. Therefore through weak things the strong were confounded. Because while the life of the humble rises in veneration, the pride of the arrogant fell.
In everything the elect do, they therefore always return to the praise of the Creator, so that they may persist with true stability in that virtue which they receive. For if they attributed anything to themselves, they could not stand in what they had received.
Well is it said by a certain wise man in Ecclesiastes, chapter one, verse seven: To the place from which rivers go out, they return, that they may flow again. For whether in virtues or in teaching, what else are holy men but rivers which water the dry earth of the carnal heart?
But whether in the work they do or in the words of teaching, they would more quickly dry up unless through the intention of heart they always carefully returned to the place from which they go out. Many of the elect who shine with miracles, if they do not return inward to the heart and bind themselves in the love of the Creator with the chains of desires, both the hand fails from what it was doing and the tongue dries up from what it was speaking.
The elect always return to the depths of their heart through love of the Creator, and what they pour out working and speaking in public, they draw in their secret place from the fountain of love. For by loving they learn what they bring forth by teaching. As if to the place from which rivers go out, they return that they may flow again, because they always draw the water of wisdom from where it springs, lest when it has run, it be dried up.

Chapter eighteen: The Miracles of the Reprobate

Not only the elect but also the reprobate can perform exterior signs or miracles. Therefore the Truth says to some in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter seven, verses twenty-two and twenty-three: Many will say to me on that day, Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name, and do many mighty works in your name? And then I will declare to them, I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of iniquity.
Any perverse person, even if he seems to hold right faith in the bosom of the universal Church and to shine with miracles, right indeed are the things he understands about the Creator through faith, but nevertheless because he scorns to hold the works of faith, he is rightly convicted of unbelief, because he is rejected by living from what he shows himself to believe.
Often heretics do wonderful works of signs, cast out spirits from possessed bodies, and by the gift of prophecy anticipate whatever is to come by knowing, but nevertheless they are divided from the giver of so many gifts by the intention of knowledge, because through his gifts they seek not his glory but their own favors.
When any reprobate elevate themselves in their own praise through goods received, they fight with those very gifts against the giver. For they grow proud against the giver from that by which they should have been more humble to him. But a stricter sentence later strikes them by as much as heavenly goodness now more abundantly pours even on the ungrateful.
Often for the reprobate the abundance of the gift becomes an increase of damnation, because though watered they have not made fruit, but under the color of greenness grow empty on high.
Sometimes heretics perform signs and miracles, but so that here they may receive rewards for their affliction and abstinence, namely they seek human praises. But because they are rejected by the voice of the Lord saying in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter seven, verse twenty-three: Depart from me, you workers of iniquity, by this sentence it is indeed given to be understood that in men the humility of charity, not the signs of powers, should be venerated.
The holy Church, even if certain miracles of heretics occur, despises them, because it knows these are not the appearance of holiness. For the proof of holiness is not to do signs, but for each person to love himself, to think truly about God, and to think better things about one's neighbor than about oneself.
Signs or miracles are not to be loved which can be held in common with the reprobate, but the miracles of charity and piety, which are all the more secure as they are hidden, and about which there may be all the greater recompense before God, as the glory is less before men.

Chapter nineteen: Divine Gifts

Almighty God, by the secret governance of his internal judgment, gives to one the word of wisdom, to another full faith, to another the grace of healings, to another the working of miracles, to another prophecy, to another discernment of spirits, to another kinds of tongues, to another interpretation of words, as recorded in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter twelve, verses eight through ten. In such a way that in one and the same spirit this one excels in the word of wisdom, yet is not supported by the word of knowledge, that is of teaching, because it is enough to sense and find even what he does not grasp through the study of learning.
Each one anticipated by the gift of grace shines in the word of knowledge, yet does not grow strong in the word of wisdom, because it is enough to fulfill what he has learned, and yet he does not rise from himself to sense anything subtle.
Each elect person commands the elements through faith, yet does not cure infirmities of bodies through the grace of healings. But that one takes away diseases by the help of prayer, yet does not restore rains by word to the parched earth.
Often a holy man by the working of miracles recalls even the dead to present life, and yet not having the grace of prophecy, he is ignorant of what is to come. That one observes all things to come as if present, and yet does not exert himself in any working of signs.
Sometimes each elect person through discernment of spirits sees minds subtly in deeds, but nevertheless does not know tongues of diverse kinds. That one examines tongues of diverse kinds, but nevertheless in similar matters does not weigh dissimilar hearts.
But another prudently judges the weights of words by interpreting in one tongue which he knows, and yet patiently lacks the remaining goods which he does not have.
Our Creator and disposer so moderates all things that he who could be exalted by the gift he has is humbled by the virtue he does not have. And so it happens that when he lifts up each one through bestowed grace, he also subjects one to another through a different gift, and each one observes him who is subjected to himself as better by another gift.
And though he feels himself to surpass by some things, he nevertheless places himself after the same one whom he surpasses in other things. Almighty God so moderates all things that while individual things belong to all, by a certain intervening necessity of charity, all things become the property of individuals. And each one so possesses in another what he did not receive, that he himself may humbly bestow on another for possessing what he received.
There are some gifts which fortify us, others which adorn us. For prophecy, teaching, kinds of tongues, the power of healings are like certain walls of the mind. Even if anyone does not have these, he can stand fortified through faith and justice.
The holy Church is the body of its heavenly head, in which one is an eye by seeing high things, another a hand by doing right things, another a foot by running to assigned tasks, another an ear by understanding the voice of precepts, another a nose by discerning the stench of evils and the fragrance of goods.
For in the manner of bodily members, while they mutually render to each other received offices, they make one body from all themselves. And when they perform diverse things in charity, they prevent being diverse where they are contained.
But if all were doing one thing, they would not be a body which is held together from many, because obviously it would not exist compactly joined together if this harmonious diversity of members did not hold it together.
When the Lord divides gifts of virtues to the holy members of the Church, he sets as it were measures to the earth, as the apostle Paul says in his letter to the Romans, chapter twelve, verse five: To each as God has divided a measure of faith. And again in his letter to the Ephesians, chapter four, verse sixteen: From whom the whole body, joined and held together by every supporting ligament, according to the working in measure of each individual member, makes the growth of the body for building itself up in love.
By wonderful counsel our author and disposer bestows on this one what he denies to another, denies to another what he bestows on this one. Therefore whoever strives to be able to do more than he has received tries to go beyond the measures set for himself.
Each one of the faithful to whom perhaps it has been given only to expound the hidden things of precepts, if he attempts also to shine with miracles, or whom the gift of heavenly power strengthens only for miracles, if he also strives to unfold the hidden things of divine law, stretches out his foot into a precipice, he who does not heed the limit of his measures.
And often he loses what he could do, because he boldly hastens to seize those things to which he cannot attain. We use the ministries of our members well when we distinctly preserve their proper offices for them. For we discern light with eyes, but we hear voice with ears.
But if anyone, with the order changed, applies eyes to voice and ears to light, both lie open to him in vain. If anyone wishes to discern odors with the mouth, to taste flavors with the nose, he destroys for himself the ministry of both senses because he perverts it. For when these are not fitted to their proper uses, they both abandon their own offices and do not rise to foreign ones.
The outstanding preacher says in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter twelve, verse twenty-seven: You are the body of Christ and members of member. For it is one thing to be a member of the body, another to be a member of a member. A member of the body is a part to the whole, but a member of a member is a particle to the part.
For a member of a member is a finger to the hand, a hand to the arm. But a member of the body is all this together to the whole body, just as also in the spiritual body of the Lord we call members of member those who in his Church are governed by others.

Chapter twenty: The Four Virtues, That Is, Prudence, Temperance, Fortitude and Justice

The solid edifice of our mind is sustained by prudence, temperance, fortitude and justice, because in these four virtues the entire structure of good work rises up. Therefore the four rivers of paradise water the earth, because when the heart is infused with these four virtues, it is tempered from the heat of all carnal desires.
Sometimes when laziness creeps into the mind, prudence grows cold, for when weary it grows torpid, it does not foresee what is coming. Sometimes when some delight creeps into the mind, our temperance withers. For the more we are led to delight in present things, the less we restrain ourselves from illicit things.
Sometimes fear insinuates itself into the heart and disturbs the strength of our fortitude. And we become all the less strong against adversities, the more we fear to lose certain things loved too immoderately. Sometimes, however, self-love thrusts itself into the mind and turns it by hidden movement from the rectitude of justice. And the more it neglects to render itself wholly to its author, the more it contradicts within itself the right of justice.
Those whom the sevenfold spirit of grace has filled, he perfects, and to them he provides not only knowledge of the Trinity but also the operation of the four virtues, that is, prudence, temperance, fortitude and justice. Through knowledge of the Trinity the action of the four virtues is received, and through the action of the four virtues one arrives even to the manifest vision of the Trinity.
We can understand by the four principal parts of the world the four virtues from which the remaining virtues arise: namely, prudence, fortitude, justice and temperance. These virtues we truly receive when we preserve their order.
First of the four virtues is prudence, second is fortitude, third is justice, fourth is temperance. For what can prudence profit if fortitude is lacking? For anyone to know what he cannot do is more punishment than virtue.
But he who prudently understands what he does and fortly does what he has understood is without doubt already just. But temperance must follow his justice, because often justice, if it does not have moderation, falls into cruelty.
Justice itself is truly justice which moderates itself with the bridle of temperance, so that in the zeal in which anyone burns he may also be temperate, lest if he burns too much he lose justice, whose measures he does not know how to preserve.

Chapter twenty-one: Active and Contemplative Life

There are two lives of holy men, namely active and contemplative. But active is prior in time to contemplative, because from good work one tends toward contemplation. Contemplative, however, is greater in merit than active, because this one labors in the use of present work, but that one already tastes the coming rest with interior savor.
The prophet Ezekiel says in chapter one, verse eight: And the hands of a man were under their wings in four directions. What therefore is signified by hands except active life, and what by wings except contemplative life? Therefore the hands of a man are under their wings, that is, the virtue of work under the flight of contemplation.
Two women in the Gospel, namely Martha and Mary, designate active and contemplative life. For Martha was busy about much serving, but Mary sat at the Lord's feet and heard his words. Therefore one was intent on work, the other on contemplation. One served the active through exterior ministry, the other the contemplative through suspension of heart in the word.
Although the active life is good, the contemplative is better, because that one ceases with mortal life, but this one grows more fully in immortal life. Therefore it is said in the Gospel of Luke, chapter ten, verse forty-two: Mary has chosen the better part, which will not be taken away from her.
If through the active life we do something good, yet we fly to heavenly desire through the contemplative. Therefore in Moses the active is called servitude, but the contemplative liberty. And though both lives are from the gift of grace, as long as we live among neighbors, one is for us in necessity, the other in will.
No mortal knowing God enters into his kingdom unless he first works well. Therefore without the contemplative life they can enter into the heavenly homeland who do not neglect to work the good things they can. But without the active they cannot enter if they neglect to work the good things they can.
That one therefore is in necessity, this one in will. That one in servitude, this one in liberty. The people of the elect rise through some into contemplation, but through others grow strong only in the works of the active life, as it is written in Genesis chapter twenty-seven, verse twenty-eight: May the Lord give you of the dew of heaven and of the fatness of the earth.
For dew falls subtly from above, and we receive from the dew of heaven as often as we see something thinly of the things above through the infusion of intimate contemplation. But when we do good works even through the body, we are enriched by the fatness of the earth.
The two wives of Jacob, namely Leah and Rachel, signify active and contemplative life. For Leah is interpreted as laborious, but Rachel as sheep or vision of the beginning. The active life is laborious because it sweats in work. But the contemplative, simple, longs only to see the beginning, namely he himself who says in the Gospel of John, chapter eight, verse twenty-five: I am the beginning, therefore I speak to you.
Blessed Jacob indeed desired Rachel, but received Leah in the night, as Genesis chapter twenty-nine, verse sixteen records, because clearly everyone who is converted to God desires the contemplative life, seeks the rest of the eternal homeland. But first it is necessary that in the night of present life he work the good things he can, sweat in labor, that is, receive Leah, so that afterward he may rest in the embraces of Rachel to see the beginning.
Rachel was seeing but sterile, Leah however had weak eyes but was fruitful. Rachel beautiful and unfruitful, because certainly the mind that seeks the leisure of contemplating sees more, but generates fewer sons to God. But when it directs itself to the labor of preaching, it sees less but gives birth more.
The contemplative life is very beautiful in the soul, but while it seeks to rest in silence, it does not generate sons from preaching. It sees and does not give birth, because intent on the pursuit of its own quiet, it is less inflamed in the gathering of others.
And as much as it sees inwardly, it is not sufficient to open to others by preaching. Leah, Jacob's wife, has weak eyes and is fruitful, because the active life, while occupied in work, sees less. But while sometimes by word, sometimes by example, it inflames neighbors to its own imitation, it generates many sons in good work. And if it cannot direct the mind into contemplation, nevertheless from what it does outwardly, it can beget followers.
After the embraces of Leah, Jacob comes to his wife Rachel, because every perfect person is first joined to the fruitfulness of the active life, and afterward united to the rest of the contemplative.
It is written in Exodus chapter twenty-one, verse two: If you buy a Hebrew slave, he shall serve you six years. For Hebrew is interpreted as passing over. And a Hebrew slave is bought and commanded to serve six years when each one who already passes in mind from this world is subjected to the service of the almighty Lord.
For he truly desires to serve God who has learned to pass in mind from this world. For a Hebrew slave is bought and commanded to serve six years, so that in the seventh he may go out free without payment. For what is designated by the number six except the perfection of the active life? What is expressed by the number seven except the contemplative?
Therefore he serves six years and goes out free in the seventh, who through the active life, which he has perfectly shown, passes to the liberty of the contemplative life. A Hebrew slave bought goes out free without payment after six years, because those who after they have done all things say they are unprofitable servants, to them without doubt, just as the active itself was from gift, so also the contemplative will be from grace.
The same slave goes out with such garment as he entered with, because it is altogether necessary that each one of us persevere in what he begins, and endure to the end of the work in that intention which he began. For he passes well to the contemplative who has not changed the garment of his intention to worse things in the active life.
It is written in the book of Revelation, chapter eight, verse one: There was silence in heaven for about half an hour. For heaven is the soul of the just, as the Lord says through the prophet in Isaiah chapter sixty-six, verse one: Heaven is my throne. And in Psalm eighteen, verse one: The heavens declare the glory of God.
Therefore when the rest of the contemplative life is enacted in the mind, silence is made in heaven, because the noise of earthly actions rests from thought, so that the soul may place its ear to the intimate secret.
Since rest of mind cannot be perfect in this life, silence in heaven is by no means said to be made for a whole hour, but as if for half. So that not even that half is fully felt, since as if is placed before it, because as soon as the soul begins to lift itself up and to be flooded with the light of intimate quiet, with the noise of thoughts returning more quickly, it is confused from itself, and confused, it is blinded.
Great is the striving of the mind in the contemplative life when it raises itself to heavenly things, when it directs the soul in spiritual matters, when it strives to pass beyond everything that is seen bodily, when it narrows itself that it may be widened.
And sometimes indeed it conquers and overcomes the resisting darkness of its blindness, and touches something subtly by stealth from the unbounded light. But immediately it is struck back to itself, and from that light to which it passes by breathing, it returns sighing to the darkness of its blindness.
In the book of Genesis, chapter twenty-three, verse nineteen, it is written: Abraham buried his wife in a double tomb. The active life is like a tomb because it covers the dead from evil works. But the contemplative buries more perfectly, because it utterly divides from all actions of the world.

Chapter twenty-two: Prayer

When God is not sought in prayer, the soul more quickly grows weary in prayer. Because when anyone asks for those things which perhaps according to hidden judgment God refuses to grant, he also comes into disgust who does not want to give what is asked.
The Lord Creator of the universe wants himself to be loved more than what he created, and eternal things to be sought rather than earthly ones, since the same Truth says in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter six, verse thirty-three: Seek first the kingdom of God, and all these things will be added to you.
For he who does not say they will be given but they will be added, certainly indicates that one thing is what is principally given, another what is added over. Because for us eternity should be in intention, but temporality in use, and that is given, and this is certainly added over from abundance.
Often people, while asking for temporal goods in prayer but not seeking eternal rewards, seek what is added and do not desire that to which it is added. Nor do they count it as gain of their petition if they are here temporally poor and there rich in blessedness, living forever. But intent only, as was said, on visible things, they refuse to trade labor of petition for invisible things.
If they sought heavenly things, they would already be showing labor with fruit. When the mind of each elect person longs in prayers for the vision of its Author, inflamed with divine desires, it is joined to heavenly things, separated from lower things, opens itself by the love of its fervor that it may receive, and receiving, inflames. And to love higher things is already to go upward. And while with great desire it opens wide to heavenly things, in a wonderful way it tastes that very thing which it seeks to receive.
The sacrifice of offering is more willingly received which is seasoned in the sight of the merciful Judge with love of neighbor. This anyone truly heaps up if he bestows it even for adversaries.
The prophet Isaiah says in chapter twenty-six, verse twenty: Enter into your chamber and close your door. And the Truth says in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter six, verse six: Pray to your Father in secret, and your Father who sees in secret will reward you.
For with door closed he asks in the chamber who, with mouth silent, pours out the affection of mind in the sight of heavenly mercy. And the voice is heard in secret when one cries silently through holy desires.
Therefore it is also rightly said through the Psalmist in Psalm nineteen, verse seventeen: The Lord has heard the desire of the poor; your ear has heard the desires of their heart. Likewise the Truth says in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter six, verse eight: Your Father knows what you need before you ask him.
He wants to be asked for what he foreknows that we will ask and he will grant. Therefore he urges us to pray importunately, and inspires that it be asked. But this he requires, that he may stir the heart of hearers to prayer.
When the Truth instructed disciples in the pursuit of prayer, he said to the Father in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter six, verse ten: Your will be done on earth as it is in heaven. Through heaven those angelic spirits who were created in heavenly things can be designated, so that clearly the will of God, as it is done by the higher creature, may thus also be kept in all things even by human weakness.
Give us today our daily bread, as Matthew chapter six, verse eleven records. Behold, we both call it ours, and yet we entreat the Lord that it be given to us. For it becomes ours when it is received, which nevertheless is God's because it is given by him. And therefore it is God's from gift, and it truly becomes ours through reception.
Forgive us our debts as we also forgive our debtors, as Matthew chapter six, verse twelve states. So that clearly the good which we, compunct, ask from God, this we first do having turned to our neighbor.
Then truly we raise our face without blemish in prayer when we neither commit forbidden evils, nor retain from our own zeal those things which have been committed against us. Our mind is weighed down with grave confusion at the time of its prayer if either its own action still stains it or preserved pain from another's wickedness accuses it. When anyone has wiped away these two things, he immediately rises freely to those things which are supported beneath.
And lead us not into temptation, as Matthew chapter six, verse thirteen says. For the Truth does not lead into temptation, who always mercifully protects his subjects from temptation. But nevertheless to lead into temptation, as it were, of his doing, is not to fortify us from the allurement of temptation. For then almighty God does not lead us into the snare of temptation when he does not permit us to be tempted beyond what we can bear.
While we suffer crowds of phantasms in prayer, we feel Jesus passing by to some extent. But when we press upon prayer vehemently, Jesus stands to restore light, because God is fixed in the heart, and the lost light is restored.
For both these things necessarily agree with each other before the Lord, so that both action be supported by prayer and prayer by action. The prophet Jeremiah says in Lamentations chapter three, verse forty: Let us search and examine our ways, and return to the Lord. Let us lift up our hearts with our hands to God in heaven.
For to search our ways is to examine the interior of thoughts. But he lifts hearts with hands who strengthens his prayer with works. For whoever prays but dissimulates working lifts heart and does not lift hands. But whoever works and does not pray lifts hands and does not lift heart.
The apostle John says in his first letter, chapter three, verse twenty-one: If our heart does not condemn us, we have confidence before God. Therefore the heart then receives confidence in prayer when no depravity of life contradicts it.
For the heart reproves us in petition when it remembers resisting the precepts of him whom it petitions. And prayer becomes execrable when it is turned away from the judgment of the law, because it is clearly fitting that anyone be a stranger from his benefits whose commands he clearly does not want to be subject to.
In prayer this is the wholesome remedy, that when the mind reproves itself from memory of fault, it first bewail what it erred, so that while it is wiped from the stain of error by weeping, the face of the heart in its petition may be seen clean by the author.

Chapter twenty-three: The Distribution of the Sevenfold Spirit

In the mind of the faithful, the first step of ascent is fear of the Lord, the second is piety, the third is knowledge, the fourth is fortitude, the fifth is counsel, the sixth is understanding, the seventh is wisdom.
The prophet Isaiah, enumerating in Christ the steps of sevenfold grace, says in chapter eleven, verses two and three: The Spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him, the Spirit of wisdom and understanding, the Spirit of counsel and might, the Spirit of knowledge and piety, and the Spirit of the fear of the Lord shall fill him.
Which steps, speaking about heavenly things, he numbered descending rather than ascending, namely wisdom, understanding, counsel, fortitude, knowledge, piety, fear. And since it is written in Proverbs chapter nine, verse ten: The beginning of wisdom is fear of the Lord, it is clear without doubt that one ascends from fear to wisdom, but does not return from wisdom to fear. For wisdom without doubt has perfect charity, as it is written in the Gospel of John, chapter four, verse eighteen: Perfect love casts out fear.
Therefore Isaiah, because he was speaking from heavenly things to the lowest, began rather from wisdom and descended to fear. But we, because we tend from earthly things to heavenly, let us number the same steps ascending, so that we may be able to arrive from fear to wisdom.
There is fear of the Lord in the mind of the faithful, but what kind of fear is this if piety is not with it? For he who does not know how to have mercy on his neighbor, who dissembles compassion for his tribulation, his fear is nothing before the eyes of almighty God, because it is not lifted up to piety. But often piety is accustomed to err through disordered mercy, if perhaps it has spared what should not be spared.
For sins which can be struck by the fires of gehenna must be corrected by the lash of discipline. But disordered piety, when it spares temporally, drags to eternal punishment. That piety may be true and ordered, it must be lifted to another step, that is to knowledge, so that it may know either what to punish from mercy or what to forgive from mercy.
But what if anyone knows what he should do but does not have the power of doing? Therefore let our knowledge grow to fortitude, so that when it sees what should be done, it may be able to do this through fortitude of mind, lest it tremble with fear and, collapsed with dread, not be able to defend the good things it perceives.
Often fortitude, if it is improvident and less circumspect against vices, falls into ruin by its own presumption. Therefore let it ascend to counsel so that by foreseeing it may fortify in advance everything it can do bravely.
Counsel cannot exist if understanding is lacking, because he who does not understand the evil which weighs down the doer, how can he solidify the good which helps? Therefore from counsel let us ascend to understanding. But what if understanding keeps watch with great keenness and does not know how to moderate itself through maturity?
From understanding therefore let us ascend to wisdom, so that what understanding keenly finds, it may arrange with the maturity of wisdom. Because therefore through fear we rise to piety, through piety we are led to knowledge, through knowledge we are strengthened to fortitude, through fortitude we tend to counsel, through counsel we advance to understanding, through understanding we come to the maturity of wisdom. By seven steps we ascend to the gate through which entrance of spiritual life is opened to us.

Chapter twenty-four: Not Returning Evil for Evil

In both Testaments it is written, as in Tobit chapter four, verse sixteen and the Gospel of Matthew chapter seven, verse twelve: What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to another. By which commands of both Testaments, through one malice is restrained, through the other kindness is extended, so that anyone, by not doing the evil he does not want to suffer, may cease from the work of harming. And again, by bestowing the good which he desires to be done to himself, he may exercise himself from kindness toward the benefit of his neighbor.
But these two things, when thought about with careful intention, direct the heart to innumerable ministries of virtues, lest either the restless mind burn with desires to inflict what it should not, or grow torpid, relaxed by idleness, toward showing what it should.
When anyone guards against doing to another what he by no means wants to endure from another, he looks around himself with careful intention lest pride lift him up and exalt his spirit by casting it down even to contempt of neighbor, lest ambition tear his thought apart and, while it widens this to seeking what belongs to others, narrow it.
It must be most carefully guarded lest lust pollute anyone's heart and corrupt it, subjected to desires through illicit things. Lest anger exasperate and inflame even to bringing forth insult. Lest envy bite and, envious of others' happiness, consume itself with its own torch.
Let anyone guard himself with careful watchfulness lest talkativeness drag the tongue immoderately and extend it to the wantonness of detraction. Lest hatred stir up malice and provoke the mouth even to the spear of cursing.
When anyone thinks to do to another those things which he himself expects to be done to himself from another, he weighs indeed that he respond to evils with goods and to goods with better things, that toward the insolent he show the gentleness of long-suffering, and to those languishing with the plague of malice he display the grace of kindness.
It is fitting for each of the faithful to unite the discordant with peace and gird the harmonious for desire of true peace, to grant necessities to the needy and show the way of rectitude to those erring. To soothe the afflicted with word and compassion, to restrain by rebuke those inflamed with the desires of this world.
Let each of the faithful so endure those things which he passes over in silence, yet not hide the poison of grief in the soul. So let him show the gift of kindness to the malevolent, yet not depart through favor from the right of rectitude. That he bestow on neighbors all things which he prevails to do, but in bestowing these may he not swell up.
And so in the goods which he shows, let him fear the precipice of swelling, yet not grow torpid from the exercise of good.

Chapter 25: On Peace and Concord

Those who are peaceful must be warned not to love the peace they possess more than necessary, lest they fail to desire the eternal peace to come. For often the tranquility of circumstances attacks the mind's focus more seriously, so that the things they hold, being free from trouble, make what calls to them less lovable; and the more present things delight them, the less they seek eternal things.
Truth speaking for itself, when distinguishing earthly peace from heavenly and calling disciples from the present to what is to come, says in the Gospel of John, chapter fourteen, verse twenty-seven: I leave you my peace, I give you my peace. I leave, that is, the transitory peace; I give the enduring peace.
If therefore the heart is fixed on the peace that is left behind, it will never reach the peace that is to be given. Present peace should be held in such a way that it must both be loved and despised, lest if it is loved immoderately, the soul of the lover be captured in sin.
The peaceful must be warned lest, while they desire human peace too much, they utterly fail to rebuke the wicked conduct of people, and by consenting to the perverse, they separate themselves from peace with their Creator; lest while they fear human quarrels outwardly, they be struck by the breaking of their internal covenant.
What is transitory peace except a certain trace of eternal peace? What therefore can be more insane than to love footprints pressed in the dust, but not to love the One from whom they were impressed?
We must know with absolute certainty that no matter how many virtues people possess, they can in no way become spiritual if they neglect to be united in harmony with their neighbors. For it is written in Paul's letter to the Galatians, chapter five, verse twenty-two: The fruit of the Spirit is charity, joy, and peace. Therefore whoever does not care to preserve peace refuses to bear the fruit of the Spirit.
From this the same Paul says in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter three, verse three: When there is among you envy and contention, are you not carnal? And again he says in the letter to the Hebrews, chapter twelve, verse eleven: Follow after peace with all, and holiness, without which no one will see God.
The apostle Paul, giving warning, says in his letter to the Ephesians, chapter four, verses three and four: Be eager to preserve the unity of the spirit in the bond of peace. One body and one Spirit, just as you are called in one hope of your calling. Therefore we in no way reach the one hope of our calling if we do not run toward it with a mind united with our neighbors.
Often some people, while receiving certain special gifts, lose through pride the gift of harmony, which is greater; so that if someone perhaps tames the flesh by restraining gluttony more than others, he disdains to be in harmony with those whom he surpasses in abstinence.
In the Gospel of Mark, chapter nine, verse forty-nine, Truth says: Have salt in yourselves, and have peace among yourselves. Indeed, salt without peace is not the gift of virtue, but a sign of condemnation.
Often birds of one and the same kind do not abandon each other in social flight, and brute animals graze together. If we observe this carefully, irrational nature by its harmony shows how great an evil rational nature commits through discord, when rational nature has lost by its failure to follow reason what irrational nature preserves by natural instinct.
The prophet David, while binding himself completely to the bonds of internal peace, testifies that he did not keep harmony with evil people, saying in Psalm one hundred thirty-eight, verses twenty-one and twenty-two: Have I not hated those who hate you, O God, and wasted away over your enemies? With perfect hatred I hated them; they have become enemies to me.
To hate the enemies of God with perfect hatred is to love what they were made to be, and to rebuke what they do.
The peaceful must be warned lest, if they leap forward to words of correction, they fear to disturb their temporal peace. Again they must be warned to hold that same peace inwardly with complete love, which they outwardly disturb for themselves by verbal rebuke.
The prophet David wisely declares that he observes both when he says in Psalm one hundred nineteen, verse seven: With those who hated peace I was peaceful; when I spoke to them, they attacked me without cause. Behold, even while speaking and being attacked, he was peaceful, because he did not cease to rebuke the insane, nor did he neglect to love those he rebuked.
Paul, commending the harmony of peace, says in his letter to the Romans, chapter twelve, verse eighteen: If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, be at peace with all people. For being about to urge disciples to have peace with people, he said first: If it is possible, and added: As far as it depends on you. For it was difficult that if they corrected wrongdoings, they could have peace with everyone.
When temporal peace is shattered in the hearts of the wicked by our rebuke, it must be preserved inviolate in our own heart. Therefore the Apostle rightly says: As far as it depends on you; as if to say: Because peace depends on the consent of two parties, so that if it is expelled by those who are corrected, it may nevertheless be held whole in the mind of you who correct.
The same Paul again warns disciples, saying in his second letter to the Thessalonians, chapter three, verse fourteen: If anyone does not obey our word, mark him through the letter, and do not associate with him, so that he may be ashamed. And immediately he adds, verse fifteen: And do not regard him as an enemy, but correct him as a brother.
As if he were saying: Break exterior peace with him, but guard interior peace concerning him to the core, so that your discord may strike the sinner's mind in such a way that peace may not depart denied from your hearts.
In the Gospel of Matthew, chapter five, verse nine, the Lord says: Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called sons of God. From this people should gather the opposite: because if those who make peace are called sons of God, without doubt those who sow conflict are sons of Satan.
Therefore those who are occupied with efforts to make peace must be warned that they ought first to pour into wicked minds love of internal peace, so that afterward external peace may be able to benefit them.

Chapter 26: On Patience

How great the excellence of the virtue of patience is, Solomon indicates, saying in Proverbs, chapter sixteen, verse thirty-two: Better is the patient man than the strong, and he who rules his spirit than the conqueror of cities.
To conquer cities is a lesser victory, because what is conquered is external. But what is conquered through patience is greater, because the soul itself is overcome and subjects itself to itself, when patience lays it low in the humility of endurance.
Better, he says, is the patient man than the strong, and he who rules his spirit than the conqueror of cities. Rightly is the patient man preferred to the conqueror of cities, because in that action of victory, man is victor over men, but in this gentleness of patience, the soul is victor over itself.
Good soil yields fruit through patience, because there are no good things that we do if we do not also bear our neighbors' evils with equanimity. It is written in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter thirteen, verse four: Charity is patient. Therefore when it is not patient, it is not charity.
Through this also, in impatience the very nurse of virtues, teaching itself, is destroyed. Solomon says in Proverbs, chapter nineteen, verse eleven: A man's learning is known by patience. Therefore one is shown to be less learned to the degree that he is proven less patient.
For he cannot truly impart good things by teaching if in living he does not know how to bear others' evils with equanimity. It is written in Ecclesiastes, chapter seven, verse nine: Better is the patient than the arrogant; because the patient person chooses to endure any evils rather than have his hidden good deeds be known through the vice of ostentation.
When patience is abandoned, even the other good things that have already been done are destroyed. Hence it is rightly said through Ezekiel, chapter forty-three, verse thirteen, that there is a hollow in the altar of God, so that the burnt offerings placed upon it may be preserved. For if there were no hollow in the altar, any sacrifice found on it would be scattered by the coming breeze.
The Lord showed patience to be the guardian of our condition, who taught us to possess ourselves in it, saying in the Gospel of Luke, chapter twenty-one, verse nineteen: In your patience you shall possess your souls.
The patient must be warned not to grieve inwardly over what they bear outwardly through patience, lest they corrupt inwardly by the plague of malice the sacrifice of so great a virtue, which they offer whole outwardly.
It must be said to the patient that they should strive to love what they must tolerate, lest if love does not follow patience, the virtue displayed be turned into the worse fault of hatred.
When the apostle Paul said in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter thirteen, verse four: Charity is patient, he immediately added: It is kind, clearly showing that those whom it tolerates through patience, it also does not cease to love through kindness.
The same excellent teacher, when urging patience on disciples, saying in his letter to the Ephesians, chapter four, verse thirty-one: Let all bitterness and anger and indignation and clamor and blasphemy be taken away from you; as if with all exterior things now well arranged, he returns to interior things when he adds: With all malice. Because indignation, clamor, and blasphemy are removed from exterior things in vain if in interior things malice, the mother of vices, reigns.
And wickedness is cut from the branches outwardly in vain if it is preserved inwardly in the root to rise up more abundantly. It usually happens to patient people that at the very time when they suffer adversities or hear insults, they are not struck by any pain, and they exhibit patience so as not to neglect to guard even the innocence of the heart.
But when a little later they recall to memory these very things they endured, they inflame themselves with the fire of pain, they seek grounds for revenge, and the gentleness they had while enduring, they turn to malice in reviewing.
The patient must be warned to fortify their heart after victory, to watch for the enemy conquered in open battle to ambush the walls of the mind, to fear the affliction creeping back more, lest the cunning enemy rejoice with all the greater exultation in deception afterward, that he tramples those stiff necks of the victors once unyielding against him.
In love of neighbor we must preserve patience and kindness, because concerning that same love it is now said in Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter fifteen, verse four: Charity is patient, it is kind. Patient, that is, so that it may bear evils inflicted by neighbors with equanimity; kind, however, so that it may bestow its own goods on neighbors with desire.
Patience truly is to endure the evils of others with equanimity, and to be bitten by no pain even against him who inflicts the evils. For he who bears his neighbor's evils in such a way that he nevertheless grieves silently and seeks a time for worthy recompense, does not practice patience but only displays it.
Every soldier of God caught in the battle of adversity must both hold forth the shield of patience lest he perish, and must hurl the spears of love's proclamation ready to conquer.
It is written in Psalm nine, verse nineteen: Will there be forgetfulness of the poor unto the end? The patience of the poor will not perish unto the end. However patient we may be in adversities or humble in prosperity, let us in no way seek present goods to be repaid to us in this life. For in return for the labor of patience, the good things of the next life must be hoped for, so that the reward of our labor may begin then when all labor finally ceases.
The patience of the poor seems to have perished, as it were, when nothing is recompensed to the humble for it in this life. But the patience of the poor will not perish unto the end, because its glory is perceived then when all laborious things are finished together.

Chapter 27: On Humility

For this purpose the only-begotten Son of God took on the form of our weakness, for this purpose he appeared invisible or despised, for this purpose he endured the mockeries of insults, the reproaches of derision, the torments of suffering, that God in humility might teach man not to be proud.
How great is the virtue of humility, how sublime the height, for the sake of truly teaching which alone he who is great beyond measure became small even unto suffering?
Because the pride of the devil provided the origin of our perdition, the humility of God was found to be the instrument of our redemption. Our enemy, created among all things, wished to be seen exalted above all things. But our Redeemer, great and remaining above all things, deigned to become small among all.
Humility is the teacher and mother of all virtues, as Truth says in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter eleven, verse twenty-nine: Learn from me because I am meek and humble of heart, and you will find rest for your souls; because while the humble abase themselves, they ascend to the likeness of God.
What therefore is loftier than humility, which while it presses itself down to the lowest, joins itself to its Creator who remains above the highest?
The testimonies of true humility are both that each person recognize his own iniquity and that he reveal the recognized iniquity by the voice of confession. It must be made known to the humble how true is the excellence they hold by hoping, how eternal are the things they desire, or how transitory are the things they despise.

Chapter 28: On Simplicity

The simple must be warned to strive never to speak falsehoods, but also to know never to keep silent about truths. For just as falsehood has often harmed the speaker, so sometimes heard truths have harmed certain people.
The simple must be warned that just as they usefully always avoid deceit, so they always usefully speak the truth. They must be warned to join prudence to the good of simplicity, so that they possess security from simplicity in such a way that they do not lose the circumspection of prudence.
For through the teacher of the nations it is said in Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter sixteen, verse nineteen: I wish you to be wise in good, and simple in evil. Truth itself warns its elect, saying in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter ten, verse sixteen: Be prudent as serpents and simple as doves; because in the hearts of the elect the cunning of the serpent must sharpen the simplicity of the dove, and the simplicity of the dove must temper the cunning of the serpent, so that neither deceived do they become crafty through prudence, nor from the pursuit of understanding do they grow sluggish from simplicity.
Nothing is more blessed than a simple heart, because to the degree that it shows innocence toward others, there is nothing it need fear to suffer from others. For it has its simplicity as a kind of citadel of strength, nor is it expected to suffer what it does not remember having done.
Well it is said through Solomon in Proverbs, chapter fourteen, verse twenty-six: In the fear of the Lord is confidence of strength. And he says again in Proverbs, chapter fifteen, verse fifteen: A secure mind is like a continual feast. For the tranquility of security is like a continuation of refreshment.
He who walks in simplicity walks confidently, as Proverbs, chapter ten, verse nine says. Indeed, simplicity of action is confidence of great security, because the Holy Spirit of discipline will flee deceit, and will not dwell in a body subject to sins, as the Book of Wisdom, chapter one, verse five states.

Chapter 29: On Intention of Heart and Body

The lamp of the body is the eye, because through the ray of good intention the merits of action are illuminated, as Truth says in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter six, verse twenty-two: If your eye is sound, your whole body will be full of light.
By the eye is expressed the heart's intention preceding its work; which before it exercises itself in action already contemplates what it desires. Finally, what is designated by the term body except each action, which follows its intention like a watching eye?
If there is right intention through simplicity of thought, good work is produced, even if it seems less good. For if the eye is evil, the whole body will be dark, because when anything even right is done with perverse intention, even if it is seen to shine before men, nevertheless before the examination of the internal judge it is darkened.
If, he says, the light that is in you is darkness, how great will that darkness be, verse twenty-three? Because if what we believe we do well we darken by evil intention, how great are those very evils which we know to be evil even when we do them?
If therefore we discern nothing there where we hold, as it were, the light of discretion, with what blindness do we stumble in those things which we perpetrate without discretion? With watchful care our intention must be weighed by us through all works, so that it may seek nothing temporal in the things it does; let it fix itself wholly in the solidity of eternity, lest if the structure of our action is placed outside the foundation, it be dissolved when the earth gapes.

Chapter 30: On Gentleness

Often the gentle suffer a neighboring and closely situated torpor of laziness, and through excessive looseness of mildness, soften the vigor of strictness beyond what is necessary. And often the gentle grow numb with the tedium of dissolution, because vice is secretly added to their virtue.
But they must be warned to flee what is beside them and to embrace solicitude, which sharpens the carelessness of much kindness. Therefore the Holy Spirit is shown to us both in the dove and in fire, because he makes all whom he fills both gentle with the simplicity of the dove and burning with the fire of zeal.
He is in no way full of the Holy Spirit who either abandons the fervor of zeal in the tranquility of gentleness, or on the other hand loses the virtue of gentleness in the ardor of zeal.
Paul the apostle, the vessel of election, bestows on two disciples different aids of preaching, though not from diverse charity. For warning Timothy he says in his second letter to Timothy, chapter four, verse two: Argue, entreat, rebuke with all patience and teaching. He also warns Titus saying in his letter to Titus, chapter two, verse fifteen: Speak these things, and exhort, and rebuke with all authority.
What does it mean that the excellent teacher dispenses his teaching with such art, that in giving instruction he proposes authority to one and patience to the other, except that he saw Titus to be of a gentler spirit and Timothy of a somewhat more fervent one?
He inflames the one through the zeal of emulation; he tempers the other through the mildness of patience. To the one he adds what is lacking; from the other he subtracts what is excessive. He strives to drive the one with the spur; he restrains the other with the bridle.
Every great farmer of the Church he has undertaken waters some branches so they may grow as they should; others, when he sees them growing more than is right, he cuts back, lest either by not growing they bear no fruit, or by growing immoderately they lose what they have brought forth.

Chapter 31: On Obedience

We must know especially that obedience sometimes, if it has something of its own, is nothing; if it does not have something of its own, it is minimal.
When the success of this world is commanded, when a higher position is ordered, he who obeys in order to receive these things empties for himself the virtue of obedience, if he also gasps for these things from his own desire.
For he does not direct himself under obedience who serves the lust of his own ambition in receiving the prosperous things of this life. When contempt of this world is commanded, when reproaches and insults are ordered to be received, unless the soul also desires these things from itself, it diminishes for itself the merit of obedience, because it descends unwillingly and reluctantly to those things which are despised in this life.
Sometimes obedience is led to detriment, when the mind is in no part accompanied even by its own wishes to receive the reproaches of this world.
Obedience ought both in adversities to have something of its own, and on the other hand in prosperity to have nothing at all of its own, so that both in adversities it may be the more glorious to the degree that it is joined also by desire to the divine order, and in prosperity it may be the more true to the degree that it is utterly separated in mind from the present glory itself which it receives from God.
By the almighty Lord, obedience is commanded to be kept even unto death, as Truth says in the Gospel of John, chapter five, verse thirty: I cannot of myself do anything, but as I hear I judge. If he judges as he hears, then he also obeys the Father even when he comes as Judge of this world.
Lest obedience appear laborious to us up to the end of the present life, our Redeemer shows, who keeps it even when he comes as Judge. What wonder is it therefore if sinful man subjects himself to obedience in the brevity of the present life, when the Mediator of God and men, even while he rewards the obedient, does not abandon it.
We must know that evil is never done through obedience, but sometimes good that is being done should be omitted through obedience. For there was not an evil tree in paradise which God forbade man to touch.
But that man, well created, might better grow through the merit of obedience, it was fitting that God should forbid him even from good, so that what he did would be all the more truly virtue, the more humbly he showed himself subject to his Creator by ceasing even from good.
We must especially note that it was said by the Lord to the first humans in the book of Genesis, chapter two, verse sixteen: From every tree of paradise you may eat, but from the tree of knowledge of good and evil do not touch. For he who forbids subjects from any one good must grant many things, lest the mind of the obedient utterly perish if, completely repelled from all goods, it starves.
It is written in the first book of Samuel, chapter fifteen, verse twenty-two: Obedience is better than sacrifices. Indeed, obedience is rightly preferred to sacrifices, because through sacrifices the flesh of another is slain, but through obedience one's own will is slain.
Therefore one placates God all the sooner the more he sacrifices himself before his eyes by the sword of precept, with the pride of his own judgment repressed. Disobedience is called the sin of divination, so that how great is the virtue of obedience may be demonstrated.
From the opposite therefore it is better shown what is felt about its praise. For if to resist is like the sin of divination, and to refuse to comply is like the crime of idolatry, obedience alone possesses the merit of faith, without which anyone is proven unfaithful, even if he seems to be faithful.
Through Solomon in the showing of obedience it is said in Proverbs, chapter twenty-one, verse twenty-eight: The obedient man speaks of victories. For the obedient man speaks of victories, because while we humbly submit to another's voice, we overcome ourselves in our heart.
Truth commending the virtue of obedience says in the Gospel of John, chapter six, verse thirty-seven: Him who comes to me I will not cast out, because I came down from heaven, not to do my own will, but the will of him who sent me. For what? If he did his own will, would he have repelled those coming to him? Who does not know that the will of the Son does not differ from the Father's will?
But because the first man, because he wished to do his own will, went out from the joy of paradise according to the book of Genesis, chapter three, verse twenty-four, the second, coming for the redemption of men, while showing that he does the Father's will and not his own, taught us to remain within through the merit of obedience.
When Christ the Lord does not his own will but the Father's, because while by his example he subjects us to obedience, he does not cast out those who come to him, and he closes to us the way of departure.

Chapter 32: On Modesty

Sometimes gentle and modest exhortation arranges modest minds toward a better life, because often for their conversion it is enough that the teacher brings their evils gently to memory.
Often greater progress is given to the modest if what is reproved in them is touched on as it were from the side. For the Lord restores the modest people through the prophet, saying in Isaiah, chapter fifty-four, verse four: You will forget the shame of your youth, and will not remember the reproach of your widowhood, because he who made you will rule over you.
The apostle Paul, an excellent preacher, reproves the faults of the modest as if sympathizing, saying in his letter to the Philippians, chapter four, verse ten: I rejoiced in the Lord greatly, that now at last you have revived your concern for me, as indeed you were concerned but were hindered.
The excellent teacher thus acted to heal the faults of the modest with medicinal kindness, while a gentler speech veiled their negligences by tempering them.
Just as modesty is praiseworthy in evil, so it is blameworthy in good. For to be ashamed of evil is wisdom; but to be ashamed of good is foolishness.
From which it is written in the Gospel of Luke, chapter nine, verse twenty-six: Whoever is ashamed of me and my words, of him the Son of Man will be ashamed when he comes in his majesty. A certain wise man says in the book of Sirach, chapter four, verse twenty-five: There is a shame that brings glory. For he who in modesty is ashamed, repenting the evils he has done, arrives at the freedom of life. But he who is ashamed to do good falls from the state of righteousness and tends toward condemnation.

Chapter 33: On Mercy and Piety

He acts well in what is pious who knows first to preserve what is just, so that the stream of mercy poured out on neighbors may be drawn from the fountain of justice.
Many bestow on neighbors as it were works of mercy, but do not abandon deeds of injustice. If they truly strove to show mercy to neighbors, they ought first to show mercy to themselves by living justly.
From which it is written in the book of Sirach, chapter thirty, verse twenty-four: Have mercy on your own soul, pleasing God. Whoever wishes to show mercy to his neighbor must draw the origin of mercy from himself. For it is written in the Gospel of Matthew, chapter nineteen, verse nineteen: You shall love your neighbor as yourself.
How therefore is he pious in showing mercy to another, who still by living unjustly becomes impious to himself? For showing mercy, so that it may be fully bestowed outwardly on the needy, two necessary things come together, that is, the man who gives and the thing that is given. But man is far and incomparably better than the thing.
From which a certain wise man says in the book of Sirach, chapter fourteen, verse five: He who is wicked to himself, to whom will he be good? In the very work of mercy, the disposition is usually weighed more by the internal Judge than the deed.
And it should be noted that blessed Job asserts he was the eye of the blind, the foot of the lame, according to the book of Job, chapter twenty-nine, verse fifteen. For saying these things, he clearly indicates that he offered his hand to the one through himself, and sustained the other by carrying him. From which it is gathered how much the heart of his mercy poured out upon the needy and weak.
The same blessed Job says in the book of Job, chapter twenty-nine, verse sixteen: I was a father to the poor. He therefore testifies that he was not their patron, or neighbor, or helper of the poor, but their father. Because by the great duty of charity he turned the zeal of mercy into the effect of nature, so that he viewed them as children through love, over whom he presided as a father through protection.
Because the force of mercy of blessed Job had imitated nature, he recalls that he was a father to the poor. Where he also added in the book of Job, chapter twenty-nine, verse sixteen: And the cause that I did not know I investigated most diligently.
In which words all things should be weighed, how distinctly they are narrated, that no reward is overlooked by him. For he is just in his actions, pious in the weaknesses of neighbors, vigorous in the affairs of the poor.
Whoever thinks of the good things of eternal retribution must mercifully extend himself to every cause of reward to follow. For through Solomon it is said in Ecclesiastes, chapter seven, verse nineteen: He who fears God neglects nothing. From this Paul says in his second letter to Timothy, chapter two, verse twenty-one: Prepared for every good work.

Chapter 34: On Almsgiving

He who provides exterior substance to a needy neighbor, but does not guard his own life from wickedness, offers his possessions to God and himself to sin. What is less he has offered to the Creator, and what is greater he has preserved for iniquity.
Sometimes proud rich men oppress their inferiors and seize what belongs to others, and yet as it were bestow certain things on others; and while they oppress many, they sometimes bring the help of defense to some, and they seem to pay a price for the iniquities which they never abandon.
Whenever we give alms after sin, we pay a price as it were for wicked deeds. From which also it is said through the prophet concerning him who does not do these things in Psalm forty-eight, verses eight and nine: He will not give God his propitiation, nor the price of redemption for his soul.
Then the price of almsgiving frees us from sins when we lament and renounce what we have done. For he who both always wishes to sin and as it were always to give alms, pays a price in vain, because he does not redeem a soul which he does not restrain from vices.
The expenditure of almsgiving cannot redeem a proud rich man, whom the plunder of the poor simultaneously perpetrated does not permit to ascend before God's eyes. From which it is said through Eliphaz in the book of Job, chapter fifteen, verse thirty-one: Let him not believe, deceived by vain error, that he is to be redeemed by any price.
Often proud rich men, when they give alms, bestow it not for the desire of eternal life but for extending temporal life. They believe they can put off death by gifts, but they can in no way obtain from the gift expended that they may escape the due end.
In the Gospel of Matthew, chapter six, verse three, Truth says: Let not your left hand know what your right hand does; that is, let the glory of present life in no way mingle with pious dispensation; but let the desire for favor not know the work of righteousness.
Often men bestow many things on the poor, not because they love those same poor, but because if they give least, they fear the wrath of the supreme Judge; who, if they did not fear God, would not have wished to give what they have.
Those who bestow their possessions mercifully must be warned not to exalt themselves with proud thought above those to whom they give earthly things, and not to consider themselves better because they see others supported by themselves.
Those who mercifully bestow their possessions should recognize that they are placed by the heavenly Lord as stewards of temporal subsidies; and let them give all the more humbly, as they understand that what they dispense belongs to another.
Lest givers rejoice excessively in good deeds, let them hear what is written in the Gospel of Luke, chapter seventeen, verse ten: When you have done all that is commanded you, say: We are unprofitable servants; what we ought to have done, we have done.
And lest sadness corrupt generosity, let them hear what is written in Paul's second letter to the Corinthians, chapter nine, verse seven: God loves a cheerful giver. Lest givers of alms seek reciprocation of grace expended, let them hear what is written in the Gospel of Luke, chapter fourteen, verse twenty-two and following: When you make a dinner or supper, do not call your friends, nor your brothers, nor your relatives, nor rich neighbors, lest perhaps they also invite you, and recompense be made to you; but when you make a feast, call the poor, the weak, the lame, the blind; and you will be blessed, because they do not have the means to repay you.
Lest givers provide too late what must be provided, let them hear what is written in Proverbs, chapter three, verse twenty-eight: Do not say to your friend: Go, and return, and tomorrow I will give to you, when you can give immediately.
Lest distributors under the pretext of generosity scatter uselessly what they possess, let them hear what is written: Let alms sweat in your hand. And lest, when many things are necessary, they give few, let them hear what is written in Paul's second letter to the Corinthians, chapter nine, verse six: He who sows sparingly will also reap sparingly.
Lest, when few things are fitting, givers provide very many, and they themselves afterward barely tolerating need break out into impatience, let them hear what is written in Paul's second letter to the Corinthians, chapter eight, verses thirteen and fourteen: Not that others may have relief and you tribulation, but by equality let your abundance supply their need, and let their abundance be a supplement to your need.
When therefore the mind of the giver does not know how to bear need, if he takes away much from himself, he seeks an occasion of impatience against himself. First the mind must be prepared for patience, and then either many or all things are to be given, lest while the irrupting need is borne less equally, both the reward of previous generosity perish, and still worse, subsequent murmuring destroy the mind.
Lest distributors provide nothing at all to those to whom they ought to give something small, let them hear what is written in the Gospel of Luke, chapter six, verse thirty: Give to everyone who asks you. And lest they provide even something to those to whom they ought to give nothing at all, let them hear what is written in the book of Sirach, chapter twelve, verse four: Give to the good, and do not receive the sinner; do good to the humble, and do not give to the impious.
It is said by a certain wise man in the book of Tobit, chapter four, verse seventeen: Set your bread and wine upon the burial of a just man, and do not eat and drink of it with sinners. For he gives his bread and wine to sinners who bestows subsidies on the wicked because they are wicked.
Some rich men of this world, while Christ's poor are crucified by hunger, nourish actors with lavish generosity. But he who gives his bread even to a needy sinner, not because he is a sinner but because he is a man, clearly nourishes not the sinner but the just, because in him he loves not the fault but the nature.
Those who mercifully bestow their possessions should carefully strive to guard, lest when they redeem committed sins with alms, they still commit sins to be redeemed; lest they consider God's justice for sale, if when they care to pay money for sins, they think they can sin with impunity.
The soul is more than food, and the body, that is, more than clothing, as the Gospel of Matthew, chapter six, verse twenty-five says. Therefore he who gives food and clothing to the poor, but nevertheless is polluted by the iniquity of soul or body, has offered to justice what is less, and has given to fault what is greater. For he has given his possessions to God, and given himself to the devil.
Let us carefully strive to consider, because both those whom we now see as needy, we will sometime see as abundant; and we who are seen as abundant, if we neglect to give, will sometime be needy.
He who now gives temporal subsidy to the poor, to receive from him afterward perpetual things, so to speak, cultivates the land as it were for fruit, which returns more abundantly what it has received. It remains therefore that pride never arise from almsgiving, when the rich man, from what he gives to the poor, acts so that he may not be poor forever.
Honor those whom you see as poor, and those whom you see outwardly as despised by the world, inwardly consider friends of God. Share with them what you have, so that sometime they may deign to share with you what they have.
Let each elect person bestow what he possesses in such a way that he may pay attention to how great is the generosity of the one rewarding. Lest when he bestows earthly things, he think of his own need more than necessary, and sadness obscure the light of cheerfulness in the offering of the gift.

Chapter 35: On the Passion of the Elect and Compassion for Neighbors

Holy men caught in the battle of tribulations, at one and the same time strike some while persuading others. Against some they oppose the shield of patience; against others they hurl the spears of teaching.
For both modes of fighting, elect men raise themselves with wondrous art of virtue, so that both inwardly they may wisely teach, and outwardly they may bravely despise adversities; correcting these by teaching, pressing down those by tolerating.
Elect men despise rising enemies by enduring them, but bring back failing citizens to salvation by compassion. They resist some, lest they also withdraw others; they fear for others, lest they utterly lose the life of righteousness.
Let us see Paul the apostle, soldier of God's camp, fighting against both. For he says in his second letter to the Corinthians, chapter seven, verse five: Outward battles, inward fears. He enumerates the battles which he endures outwardly, saying in his second letter to the Corinthians, chapter eleven, verse twenty-six: In dangers of rivers, in dangers of robbers, in dangers from my own people, in dangers from the Gentiles, in dangers in the city, in dangers in the wilderness, in dangers at sea, in dangers among false brethren.
In this spiritual battle what spears Paul hurls against the adversary, he adds in the same passage: In labor and hardship, in many sleepless nights; in hunger and thirst, in many fastings, in cold and nakedness.
Caught among manifold contests, let the Vessel of Election tell with what fortification of vigils he also guards the camp. For he says in the same passage: Besides those things that are outward, my daily pressing concern, the care of all the churches.
Behold, he both undertakes battles bravely and spends himself mercifully in protecting neighbors through compassion. He narrates the evils he suffers; he adds the good things he imparts. Let it be considered what labor it is for the apostle Paul at one and the same time both to tolerate adversities outwardly and to protect the weak inwardly.
Outwardly he suffers battles, because he is torn by blows, bound with chains; inwardly he tolerates fear, because he fears that his suffering may harm not himself but his disciples. The Apostle feared the fall of others in his own suffering, lest while disciples recognized him himself afflicted by blows for the faith, they might refuse to profess themselves faithful.
From which he writes to them, saying in his first letter to the Thessalonians, chapter three, verse three: Let no one be moved by these tribulations. For you yourselves know that we are appointed to this. O immense heart of charity of the apostle Paul! He despises the sufferings which he himself endures, and cares that disciples may not suffer anything of wicked persuasion in their heart.
In himself he despises wounds of the body, and in others he heals wounds of the heart. The just have this as their own, that placed in the pain of their tribulation, they do not abandon care for others' usefulness; and while suffering adversities about themselves they grieve, they foresee by teaching what is necessary for others, and as it were certain great physicians grow sick when struck.
They themselves tolerate the wounds of injury, and to others they offer medicines of health. But it is of far less labor either to teach when you tolerate nothing, or to tolerate when you teach nothing. Holy men carefully extend themselves to both battles of sufferings or compassions; and when perhaps they are struck by tribulations, they undertake exterior battles in such a way that they carefully think lest the interior things of neighbors be torn.
While brave men stand in battle line, there they strike opposing breasts with spears; here with shield they protect the weak placed behind. And thus on both sides they watch with speed of circumspection, so that both before themselves they pierce the bold, and behind themselves they defend the trembling from wounds.

Chapter 36: What Jubilation Is

Jubilation is spoken of when ineffable joy is conceived in the mind, which can neither be hidden nor opened in words, and nevertheless is betrayed by certain movements, although it is expressed by no specific terms.
The prophet David, seeing the souls of the elect conceive as much joy in mind as they cannot reveal in speech, says in Psalm eighty-eight, verse sixteen: Blessed is the people who know jubilation. For he does not say who speak it, but who know it, because jubilation can indeed be known by understanding, but cannot be expressed by speaking.
Through jubilation is felt what is beyond feeling; and when the consciousness of the one feeling scarcely suffices for contemplating, how does the tongue of the one speaking suffice for expressing?

Chapter 37: On Desire for the Heavenly Kingdom

The great cry of the saints is great desire, for one cries out less to the degree that he desires less; and he expresses a greater voice into the ears of the unlimited spirit to the degree that he pours himself out more fully in desire for him.
Since the mind that seeks is usually moved one way, and the mind that is sought another way, and the souls of the saints cling to God in the bosom of the internal secret in such a way that by clinging they rest, how are they said to seek when it is clear they in no way differ from his internal movement?
How are they said to ask when it is certain they know both the will of God and the things that are to be? But placed in him they are said to ask something from him, not that they desire anything that differs from the will of him whom they see, but the more ardently they cling in mind, the more also they receive from him, so that they ask from him what they know he wishes to do.
Let no one who is heard more slowly believe that he is neglected by heavenly care. For often our desires are heard because they are not done quickly; and what we ask to be fulfilled rapidly is made more prosperous by that very delay.
Often our voice is perfected by being deferred; and when the petition is neglected on the surface, our vows are fulfilled more deeply in the root of thoughts; just as seeds of harvests are solidified by the pressing of frost, and the more slowly they come to the surface, the more abundantly they rise to fruit.
Our desires are extended by delay so that they may profit, they profit so that they may grow strong for what they are about to receive, and they are exercised in struggle, so that they may be heaped with greater rewards in recompense. The labor of the fight is prolonged, so that the crown of victory may grow.
Therefore when the Lord does not hear his own quickly, by which he is believed to repel them, he continually draws them. The internal physician cuts away the contagions of sins in us, which he deeply disapproves to be within, and cuts off the poison of corruption with the blade of tribulation; and the more he pretends not to hear the voice of the sick, the more he procures the end of the sickness.
The prophet David says in Psalm twenty-one, verse five: I will cry by day, and you will not hear; in the night, and not to foolishness for me. As if he were saying: It profits me in no way toward foolishness that you do not continuously hear me crying day and night, because from where you as it were abandon me in temporal tribulation and do not hear, from there you more fully train me toward eternal wisdom.
Again the Psalmist says in Psalm nine, verse ten: A helper in opportunities in tribulation. Indeed, being about to speak of tribulation, he put opportunities first, because often we are worn down by tribulation, and yet it is not yet opportune that we be helped toward the desire of deliverance.

Chapter 38: On the Concord of the Good

Then charity makes us free from the yoke of fault, when in turn it subjects us to our own service through love, when we both believe the goods of others to be ours, and exhibit our own to others offering them as their own.
Let people know with absolute certainty that however many virtues they possess, they can in no way become spiritual if they neglect to be united in harmony with neighbors. It is written in Paul's letter to the Galatians, chapter five, verse twenty-two: For the fruit of the Spirit is charity, joy, and peace. Therefore whoever does not care to preserve peace refuses to bear the fruit of the Spirit. From this the same Paul says in his first letter to the Corinthians, chapter three, verse three: When there is among you envy and contention, are you not carnal? From this again he says: Follow after peace with all, and holiness, without which no one will see God.

Chapter 39: On Preserving Friendship

A friend is called as it were the guardian of the soul. From which not undeservedly whoever strives to guard the will of God in his precepts is called his friend, as Truth says to disciples in the Gospel of John, chapter fifteen, verses fourteen and fifteen: Now I will not call you servants, but friends. And again: You are my friends, if you do what I command you.
When someone placed in prosperity is loved as a friend, it is very uncertain whether the prosperity or the person is loved. But the loss of happiness tests the strength of love. From which a certain wise man well says in the book of Sirach, chapter twelve, verse eight: The friend is not recognized in good things, and the enemy is not hidden in evil things.
Neither prosperity shows a friend, nor adversity hides an enemy. Because both the one is often covered by reverence for our prosperity, and the other is revealed by the confidence of adversity.
How friendship is to be held our Redeemer makes known, who in very many opinions of his Scripture commands both that friends be loved in him and enemies be loved for his sake. For he truly has charity who both loves a friend in God and loves an enemy for God's sake.

Chapter 40: On Diligence in Reading

Sacred Scripture calls the soul of its reader to the heavenly homeland, and changes the heart of the reader from earthly desires to embracing heavenly things; and it exercises the strong with more obscure sayings, and flatters the little ones with humble speech.
Sacred Scripture is loved all the more, the more it is meditated on, and by the practice of reading it removes tedium; and it helps the soul of the reader with humble words, lifts it with sublime ones.
Sacred Scripture somehow grows with its readers; it is recognized as it were by unskilled readers, and yet is always found new by the learned.
To the prophet Ezekiel it is said in the book of Ezekiel, chapter three, verse one: Son of man, whatever you find, eat. For whatever is found in Sacred Scripture is to be eaten, because both its small things compose a simple life, and its great things build up subtle understanding.
By reading we come alive again through the precepts of Sacred Scripture, we who lay dead in guilt. From which it is said to the almighty Lord through the Psalmist in Psalm one hundred eighteen, verse ninety-three: I will never forget your words, because by them you have given me life.
Sacred Scripture has been made for us a light of the journey in the darkness of the present life. For from this Peter says in his second letter, chapter one, verse nineteen: To which you do well to attend as to a lamp shining in a dark place.
When to anyone reading, the word of Sacred Scripture seems lukewarm, the sense of divine speech does not stir his mind, and sends forth no light of understanding in his thought. But truly if he seeks the order of living well, and finds through the step of the heart how to place the foot of good work, he finds as much progress in sacred speech as he himself has made progress before it.
It often happens that anyone feels the words of Sacred Scripture to be mystical if, kindled through the grace of contemplation, he suspends himself toward heavenly things. For the wondrous and ineffable power of sacred speech is recognized when the mind of the reader is penetrated by heavenly love.
When we attend to sacred speeches, we often bear more heavily the snares of evil spirits, because they sprinkle the dust of earthly thoughts on our mind, so that they may obscure the eyes of our intention from the light of intimate vision.
The patriarch Isaac well represents this, when the wells which he had dug to draw water, the foreigners filled with piles of earth, according to the book of Genesis, chapter twenty-six, verse fifteen. We certainly dig wells as it were when in the hidden senses of Scripture we penetrate deep things. Which nevertheless the foreigners secretly fill, when unclean spirits thrust earthly thoughts on us tending toward high things, and as it were take away the water of divine knowledge that was found.
In examining the commandments of God the Psalmist had borne the snares of evil spirits, when he said in Psalm one hundred eighteen, verse one hundred fifteen: Depart from me, you evil ones, and I will scrutinize the commandments of my God. Clearly making known that he could not scrutinize the commandments of God when he was tolerating the snares of evil spirits in his mind.
Readers of sacred speech, while they are uncertain outwardly about what they arrange in mind, always as it were return to the tabernacle, and as if before the ark of the covenant they consult the Lord, if they seek within themselves inwardly the pages of sacred speech concerning those things about which they doubt.

Chapter 41: On Spiritual Delights

Spiritual delights increase desire in the mind while they satisfy, because the more their taste is perceived, the more it is known that they should be loved more eagerly. And therefore those not possessed cannot be loved, because their taste is unknown.
For who can love what he does not know? The Psalmist warns us saying in Psalm thirty-three, verse nine: Taste and see how sweet the Lord is; as if he openly says: You do not know his sweetness if you do not taste it at all. But touch the food of life with the palate of the heart, so that by proving his sweetness you may be able to love.
Man lost spiritual delights when he sinned in paradise. For drawn out, he closed his mouth from the food of eternal sweetness. From which we also, born in the misery of this pilgrimage, who have now come fastidious, do not know what we ought to desire.
And the disease of our fastidiousness is exaggerated all the more, the more the soul distances itself from eating that sweetness. And now it does not desire internal delights to the degree that it has long and far ceased to eat them. We waste away with the fastidiousness of our misery, and are wearied by the long plague of starvation.
And because we do not wish to taste inwardly the sweetness prepared, we wretched people love outwardly our hunger. Heavenly piety does not abandon us even when we abandon it. For it recalls those despised delights to the eyes of our memory, and proposes them to us, shakes off torpor in the promise, and that we should repel our fastidiousness, invites us and says: Taste and see how sweet the Lord is.

Chapter 42: On Discretion

Great is the teaching of discipline, the subtlety of discretion, so that each ruler may know how to spare the faults of offenders with discretion and to cut them off piously. But those who so forgive sins that they do not correct, or so strike as if correcting that they do not forgive, do not have the spirit of discretion.
It is written in the book of Genesis, chapter four, verse seven according to the Septuagint: If you offer rightly, and do not divide rightly, you have sinned. It is offered rightly when something is done with right intention. But it is not divided rightly if what is piously done is not also subtly discerned.
To divide rightly what is offered is to weigh any of our good efforts by discerning. Which indeed whoever pretends to do sins even while offering rightly. Often what we do with good zeal, while we neglect to discern cautiously, we do not know by what end it will be judged; and sometimes that becomes the guilt of crime which is thought to be the cause of virtue.
We therefore offer rightly when we do good work with good zeal; but we do not divide rightly if we postpone having discretion in good work. For he enters the narrow way who in all things which he does is carefully restricted by the subtlety of discretion.
For he who with secure mind expands himself through his own wills condemns for himself the entrance of the narrow gate. The Holy Church, through discretion foresees from what causes individual temptations proceed, and from on high perceives the coming battles of vices.

Chapter 43: On Silence

The tongue should be restrained with discretion, not bound up irrevocably. For Scripture says: A wise person will be silent until the right time—from Ecclesiasticus, chapter twenty, verse seven. The goal is that when someone judges the moment opportune, they set aside the constraint of silence and, by speaking what is appropriate, devote themselves to usefulness.
About the discipline of silence and speech, Solomon says: There is a time for speaking and a time for being silent—from Ecclesiastes, chapter three, verse seven. We must weigh carefully the changing circumstances, lest either our tongue flow uselessly in words when it ought to be restrained, or when it could speak usefully, it sluggishly restricts itself.
The Psalmist, considering how great is the benefit of restrained silence, says: Lord, set a guard over my mouth, and a door of enclosure around my lips—from Psalm one hundred forty-nine, verse three. For he does not ask that a wall be placed over his mouth, but a door—something that obviously opens and closes.
And so we ourselves must speak cautiously, so that discretion opens the mouth with voice at the right time, and silence closes it again at the right time. The overly silent should strive carefully not only to show what they are like outwardly, but also to exhibit what they ought to be like inwardly, so that they fear God's hidden judgment on their thoughts more than their neighbors' criticism of their words.
The excessively silent should be instructed that while they carelessly flee certain vices, they secretly become entangled in worse ones. For often, because they restrain the tongue too immoderately, they endure more serious chattering in the heart. The result is that thoughts burn more intensely in the mind precisely because the violent custody of indiscriminate silence constricts them.
Usually the excessively silent flow all the more widely in their thoughts because they consider themselves more secure, since outwardly they are not seen by critics. Sometimes the silent person's mind is lifted up in pride, and when he hears others speaking, he despises them as though they were weak. And while he closes the mouth of his body, he does not recognize how much he opens himself to vices through pride.
It often happens that the mind of the overly silent person presses down the tongue but lifts up the mind. And when he scarcely considers his own wickedness, he accuses everyone else all the more freely in his own thoughts precisely because he does so secretly. Often the excessively silent, when they suffer certain injustices, advance into sharper pain precisely because they do not speak about what they endure.
For if the tongue calmly spoke about the troubles inflicted, the pain would flow away from the conscience. Closed wounds torment more severely. For when the infection that festers inside is expelled, the pain is opened toward healing. Those who keep silent more than is expedient should know that among the troubles they endure, by holding their tongue they intensify the force of pain.
They should be admonished that if they love their neighbors as themselves, they should by no means keep silent when they justly reprove them. Through the medicine of voice, the salvation of both parties is served: the wrongdoer is restrained from the evil action, and the one who suffers has the fever of pain tempered when the wound is opened.
Those who observe their neighbors' evils and yet press down their tongue in silence are like those who, having seen wounds, withhold the use of medicine. They become authors of death precisely because they refused to cure the poison they were capable of curing.

Chapter 44: On Avoiding Detraction

Those who feed on slandering others' lives are undoubtedly filled with alien flesh, as Scripture says: Do not eat with those who bring meat to the feast—from Proverbs, chapter twenty-three, verse twenty. To bring meat to the feast is to mutually disparage the faults of neighbors in conversations of detraction.
Solomon rightly says concerning slanderers: Because those who linger over drinks and contribute their share will be consumed, and sleep will be clothed with rags—from the same place. They linger over drinks who intoxicate themselves on the reproach of others' lives. To contribute one's share is like each person bringing their portion of food to eat, so in the conversation of detraction they contribute words.
Those who linger over drinks and contribute their share, he says, will be consumed, because as Scripture says: Every slanderer will be uprooted. Sleep will be clothed with rags because death finds despised and destitute of all good works the one whom here the sickness of his own detraction occupied in seeking out crimes in others' lives.

Chapter 45: On Compunction

Outward discipline washes away faults, and the compunction of repentance pierces through the extended mind with vengeance. But these two differ from each other in this: the blows of beatings cause pain, while the laments of compunction bring savor. The former torment while afflicting, but the latter refresh even while afflicting. Through beatings there is sorrow in affliction; through compunction there is joy in sorrow.
Every sin is a thorn, because while it draws toward pleasure, it tears the mind as though by pricking. Hence in the voice of the just person who repents it is said: I am turned into misery while the thorn is being broken—from Psalm thirty-one, verse four. For the mind is turned to lamentation so that the pricking of sin may be broken through repentance.
You have broken my bonds, he says, and I will sacrifice to you a sacrifice of praise—from Psalm one hundred fifteen, verse sixteen. The Lord then breaks our bonds when he both shows us the evils we have done and helps us to weep for these same things we recognize. The almighty Lord exposes our guilty works, and with the merciful hand of grace he loosens the bonds of the heart, so that our mind may rise up to the leisure of repentance, and freed from the fetters of flesh, may direct its step of love freely toward its maker.
When the vehement spirit of repentance occupies the mind, it disturbs every examination of blameworthy joy within it, so that nothing pleases the mind except to weep, and it pays attention to nothing that could terrify it. Each elect person's mind places before its eyes on one side the strictness of justice, on the other side the merit of guilt: it perceives what punishment it would deserve if the mercy of the one who spares were absent—that mercy which is accustomed to rescue from eternal punishment through present laments.
When the strong force of compunction wholesomely overwhelms our minds dedicated to this world as to a sea, it is as though in a mighty spirit God shatters the ships of Tarshish. The almighty Lord, through the coming of the Holy Spirit, afflicts in the sorrow of repentance the minds of carnal people that previously wandered in the waves of this world, so that crushed from pride they may lie wholesomely in humility—they whom the wave of their prosperity previously lifted up to the heights of vanity in this sea of the world.
Usually the minds of carnal people converted to Christ through repentance—for whom it seemed a punishment to be despised by men—afterward find it burdensome to be honored by men. And those who previously were accustomed to perpetrate evils and rejoiced in them, afterward do not fear even to be afflicted for good things.
There are four qualities by which the soul of a just person is vehemently affected in compunction: either when he remembers his evils, considering where he was; or when fearing the sentence of God's judgments and inquiring within himself, he thinks where he will be; or when carefully attending to the evils of present life, he mourns and considers where he is; or when he contemplates the goods of the heavenly homeland which he has not yet attained, he grieves and sees where he is not.
Paul remembered his evils and afflicted himself for those things in which he had been, when he said: I am not worthy to be called an apostle, because I persecuted the Church of God—from Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter fifteen, verse nine. The apostle Paul, carefully weighing divine judgment, feared being in a bad state in the future, saying: I discipline my body and bring it into subjection, lest perhaps when preaching to others I myself should become disqualified—from Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter nine, verse twenty-seven.
The same Paul weighed the evils of present life when he said: While we are in the body, we are away from the Lord—from Paul's second letter to the Corinthians, chapter five, verse six. And: I see another law in my members warring against the law of my mind, and leading me captive in the law of sin which is in my members. Wretched man that I am, who will deliver me from this body of death—from Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter seven, verse twenty-two?
Again the same Vessel of Election considered the goods of the eternal homeland, saying: We now see through a glass dimly, but then face to face. Now I know in part, but then I shall know just as I am known—from Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter thirteen, verse twelve. And again: We know that if our earthly house of this dwelling is dissolved, we have a building from God, a house not made with hands, eternal in the heavens—from Paul's second letter to the Corinthians, chapter five, verse one.
Blessed Job, considering the evils of present life, said: Human life on earth is a trial—from Job, chapter seven, verse one. Hence David also said: Every living man is altogether vanity—from Psalm thirty-eight, verse six. And although man walks in the image of God, yet he will be troubled in vain. The Psalmist, contemplating the eternal homeland and weighing these evils in which he was, and considering those goods in which he was not yet, said: Woe is me that my sojourning is prolonged—from Psalm one hundred nineteen, verse five!
And: I said in my alarm, I am cut off from before your eyes—from Psalm thirty, verse twenty-three. There are some who have already received in gift the ability to speak freely for justice, to protect the oppressed, to distribute their possessions to the needy, to have the ardor of faith—but they do not yet have the grace of tears. These certainly have the southern land that is dry, but they still need irrigation, because being established in good works in which they are great and fervent, it is extremely necessary that either by fear of punishment or by love of the kingdom of heaven, they should also weep for the evils they previously committed.
Although compunction is divided into many forms when each individual fault is mourned by penitents—hence Jeremiah also says in the voice of penitents: My eye has sent down divisions of waters—from Lamentations, chapter three, verse forty-eight—nevertheless there are principally two kinds of compunction, because the soul thirsting for God is first pricked by fear, then by love.
For first it affects itself in tears because, while recalling its evils, it fears suffering eternal punishments for them. When fear has been consumed by long anxiety of sorrow, a certain security is born from the presumption of pardon, and the soul is inflamed with love of heavenly joys. And the one who previously wept lest he be led to punishment, afterward begins to weep most bitterly because he is delayed from the kingdom.
Often the mind contemplates what those angelic choirs are, what that very society of holy spirits is, what the majesty of the inner vision of God is, and it mourns more greatly because it is absent from perennial goods than it wept before when it feared eternal evils. And so it happens that perfect compunction of fear delivers the soul over to compunction of love.
In sacred and true history, a figurative narrative describes how Achsah the daughter of Caleb, sitting on a donkey, sighed—from the book of Joshua, chapter fifteen, verse eighteen and following. Her father said to her: What do you have? And she responded: Give me a blessing; you have given me the southern and dry land, add also the irrigated land. Then her father gave her the upper springs and the lower springs.
Achsah sits on a donkey when the soul presides over the irrational movements of its flesh; sighing, she seeks irrigated land from her father, because from our Creator the tears of compunction must be sought with great groaning. Because therefore, as I said, there are two kinds of compunction, her father gave her the upper springs and the lower springs.
The soul receives the upper springs when it afflicts itself in tears through desire for the heavenly kingdom; it receives the lower springs when it fears the punishments of hell with weeping.

Chapter 46: On the Hope and Fear of the Elect

Often the mind of a just person now deplores what it remembers doing perversely, now not only abandons wrongly done deeds but also punishes them with most bitter laments—yet nevertheless, while remembering what it has done, it is terrified with grave fear concerning judgment. Each elect person now converts himself perfectly, but does not yet raise himself up perfectly in security, because while he weighs how great is the strictness of the final examination, he trembles anxiously between hope and fear, because he does not know what the just Judge coming will consider concerning deeds committed, what he will forgive.
Often the penitent mind remembers what wrongs it committed, but does not know whether it has wept for these committed wrongs worthily, and fears lest the enormity of guilt exceed the measure of repentance. Often truth now forgives the guilt, but the afflicted mind still trembles concerning pardon, being very anxious about itself. Each holy person receives mercy even here, but does not know that he has received it, because man now abandons his sin by correcting and repenting, but nevertheless still fears the strict Judge concerning its retribution.
There every just person freely sings the mercies of the Lord in eternity without any fear—from Psalm eighty-eight, verse one—where there will no longer be doubt about the forgiveness of sin, where the memory of his guilt no longer indicts the secure mind, where the soul does not tremble under guilt but rejoices freely in his pardon.
When the prophet David petitioned saying: Turn away your eyes from my sins—from Psalm fifty, verse eleven—a little earlier he added: My offense is before me always—from Psalm fifty, verse five. As if to say: I ask that you not look at my sin, because I myself do not cease looking at it. Hence also through another prophet the Lord says: And I will not remember your sins, but you remember them—from Isaiah, chapter forty-three, verse twenty-five.
It must be provided for those who weep for their sins that they consider each individual committed deed, and while they weep through each one for the defilement of their error, they simultaneously cleanse themselves entirely with tears. Through Jeremiah it is said, when the individual sins of Judea were weighed: My eye has sent down divisions of waters—from Lamentations, chapter three, verse forty-eight. We send down divided waters from our eyes when we give distributed tears to individual sins. For the mind does not grieve equally for all things at one and the same time, but while now the memory of this fault, now of that fault touches it more sharply, moved in individual cases it is purged of all simultaneously.
Those oppressed by the terror of fear should be admonished to presume upon the mercy they ask for, lest they perish by the force of immoderate affliction. For the merciful Lord would not place sins to be wept for before the eyes of offenders if he had wanted to strike them strictly by himself. For it is established that he wanted to hide from his own judgment those whom, mercifully preventing them, he made judges of themselves.
Hence it is written: Let us come before his face in confession—from Psalm ninety-four, verse two. Hence Paul says: If we judged ourselves, we would certainly not be judged—from Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter eleven, verse thirty-one. Holy men, in that they remember having sinned because they fear and weep, what else do they do but cover the body? They hide carnal deeds from strict examination through good works superimposed.
It is written: Blessed are those whose iniquities are forgiven and whose sins are covered—from Psalm thirty-one, verse one. For we cover sins when we superimpose good deeds upon evil acts. For everything that is covered is placed below, and that with which it is covered is drawn over from above. When therefore we renounce the evils we have done and choose the goods we do, we draw over as a covering that thing which we blush to have seen.
Holy men still established in this life have something they should cover before God's eyes, because it is altogether impossible that they never sin either in deed, or in speech, or in thought. Through blessed Moses the Lord says: You shall not take as a pledge the upper or lower millstone—from Deuteronomy, chapter twenty-four, verse six.
The upper or lower millstone is hope and fear: for hope lifts up to the heights, while fear presses the heart down below. The upper and lower millstone are so necessarily joined to each other that one is uselessly held without the other. In the sinner's breast therefore hope and fear must be joined incessantly, because he hopes for mercy in vain if he does not also trust in mercy.
The upper or lower millstone is forbidden to be taken as a pledge, because whoever preaches to a sinner must compose the preaching with such dispensation that he neither removes fear by abandoning hope, nor abandons him in fear alone by removing hope. The upper or lower millstone is removed if through the preacher's tongue either fear is divided from hope or hope from fear in the sinner's breast.

Chapter 47: On Repentance

Every sinner in repentance ought to have double groaning. Certainly because he both did not do the good which he ought to have done, and did the evil which he ought not to have done. When we do not rise to good works, it is necessary that we weep for ourselves doubly, because we both did not do right things and we performed wrong things.
Through blessed Moses, one turtledove is commanded to be offered for sin, but another for a burnt offering—from Leviticus, chapter fifteen, verses twenty-nine and thirty. For burnt offering means totally burned. We therefore offer one turtledove for sin when we give groaning for guilt; we make a burnt offering of the other when, for those good things we neglected, we burn ourselves completely with the fire of pain.
Blessed Job, advancing through afflictions and dissenting from himself in great self-rebuke, says: Therefore I reprehend myself and do penance—from Job, chapter forty-two, verse six. To do penance is, having contemplated the highest essence, to recognize oneself as nothing other than dust and ashes.
In sackcloth there is the roughness and pricking of sins; in ashes the dust of the dead is shown. And therefore both of these are customarily applied to repentance, so that in the pricking of sackcloth we recognize what we did through guilt, and in the embers of ashes we weigh what we have become through judgment.
Therefore let the pricking vices be considered in sackcloth, let the just punishment of vices following through the sentence of death be considered in ashes. Because the insults of the flesh arose after sin, let man see in the roughness of sackcloth what he did through pride, let him see in ashes how far he arrived through sinning. The sinner is pricked as by a certain sackcloth when he is rubbed in his mind by the harsh goads of reproofs. He does penance in ashes because from the first sin he carefully attends to what he has become through just judgment.
We turn day into darkness when, strictly punishing ourselves, we torment the very allurements of wicked pleasure through the strict laments of repentance, and weeping we pursue whatever we sinned from pleasure silently in the heart.
Each faithful person, while not ignorant that thoughts are examined subtly in judgment, examining himself inwardly, examines himself vehemently before judgment, so that the strict Judge comes already tranquil to the extent that he sees his guilty one whom he desires to examine already punished for guilt.
Almighty God considers our deeds subtly, and yet mercifully relaxes these for the penitent. He perceives hardness in sinners, but nevertheless, with grace going before, he softens this toward repentance. God enumerates our faults when he converts us ourselves to weeping for individual things we have done. He mercifully relaxes them because while we punish them, he by no means judges them in the final examination—as Paul attests, saying: If we judged ourselves, we would certainly not be judged—from Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter eleven, verse thirty-one.
What we do outwardly, unless we wash it away with repentance intervening, is preserved in the secret of God's judgments under a certain concealment, so that sometimes it also comes out from the seal of secrecy to the public of judgment. But when we are worn away by the scourge of discipline for the evils we have done, and we weep for these through repentance, he seals and heals our iniquity because he neither abandons it here unavenged nor reserves it to be punished in judgment.
God therefore seals our sins because here he attends to them subtly in order to strike; he heals them because through the scourge he completely relaxes them. Blessed Job, assuming the person of the human race, says: You have sealed up my offenses as in a bag—from Job, chapter fourteen, verse seventeen. Our sins are sealed in a bag when we weigh with always anxious heart the evils we have done. For what is the heart of man but God's little bag?
We carry sealed things in God's bag, because the merciful creator relaxes for us the faults which by observing and repenting we recognize. Rightly after sins sealed in the bag it is said: But you have healed my iniquity—from the same place in Job—as if to say openly: Those things which you now seal so that I may see them in repenting, you do without doubt so that they may not be seen in retribution.

Chapter 48: On Confession

These are testimonies of humility: both to recognize one's own iniquity and to reveal what is recognized by the voice of confession. It is the customary vice of the human race both to commit sin by falling and to betray what is committed not by confessing but by denying and defending, and to multiply what is proven by defending it.
For from that fall of the first man we derive these increases of wickedness, from which we drew the very root of guilt. For this reason the first man was sought out: so that the sin he committed by transgressing he might delete by confessing; and he is asked where he is so that he might look back at the guilt perpetrated and by confessing might recognize how far he is from the face of his creator.
These are signs of true confession: if when anyone calls himself a sinner, he does not contradict another who also says this about him. It is written: The just person is an accuser of himself in the beginning—from Proverbs, chapter eighteen, verse seventeen. One does not desire to appear more as a sinner but as just when anyone confesses himself a sinner with no one accusing him.
The truth of confession is proven when another rebukes the evil we have done. But if we proudly defend it, it is clear that we were calling ourselves sinners from ourselves fictitiously. It must be cared for supremely that we both spontaneously confess the evils we have done and do not deny these when others argue against us.
For it is the vice of pride that what anyone deigns to confess as though of his own will, he disdains to have said of himself by others. The pledge of the debtor is the confession of the sinner; for a pledge is accepted from a debtor when the confession of sin is now held from the sinner.

Chapter 49: On the Gravity of Counsel

It is one thing to be weighty through counsel, another through sin; it is one thing to be weighty through constancy, another through guilt. For this gravity has the weight of burden, that one of virtue. In respect to heavenly reward, souls usually receive this prize: that they no longer leap away from the intention of God with light movement, but stand fixed in it with the gravity of constancy.
The Jewish people were moved lightly, concerning whom it is said through the prophet: He went wandering in the way of his heart; I saw his way and abandoned him—from Isaiah, chapter fifty-seven, verse seventeen. But the grave counsel of the heart expels all inconstancy of wandering. There are souls that with light movement desire now these things, now those. The almighty God therefore, because he does not weigh lightly these light fluctuations of minds, judges by abandoning the wandering of the heart.
But when through grace he looks upon a wandering mind, he fixes it in the stability of counsel. When almighty God mercifully deigns to look upon the light movements of man, he immediately forms him to the maturity of constancy, and by the regard of heavenly grace suddenly leads his heart to the gravity of counsel.

Chapter 50: On Hospitality

To love the hospitality of brotherhood, to cherish works of charity, is very necessary for us. Hence the distinguished preacher, commending both to us, says: Let the charity of brotherhood remain in you, and do not forget hospitality—from the letter to the Hebrews, chapter thirteen, verse one. Blessed Peter, prince of the apostles, commends to us that the grace of this hospitality be shown to brothers with kindness, saying: Be hospitable to one another without grumbling—from Peter's first letter, chapter four, verse nine.
Hence Truth itself says: I was a stranger and you welcomed me—from the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-five, verse thirty-five. With wonderful mercy our Redeemer speaks from compassion for his members, saying: Whatever you did for one of the least of these, you did for me—from the same place, verse forty. He therefore helps us through the fact that he is our head, who is helped through our good works in his members.
We must consider how much the hospitality of fraternal compassion is worth, how much the bowels of mercy join us to the almighty Lord. For from that place we draw near to him who is above all things, from which place we place ourselves even below ourselves through compassion for our neighbor. In bodily matters no one touches high things unless he is stretched; but in spiritual matters it is certain that the more we are drawn down through compassion, the more truly we draw near to the heights.
It does not suffice for the Redeemer of the human race for our edification that he declared he would say in the final judgment: As long as you did it for one of these my least brothers, you did it for me—from the Gospel of Matthew, chapter twenty-five, verse forty-two—unless he also showed in himself before judgment what he had said; namely to demonstrate that whoever now shows good works to the needy, he specially renders these to him for whose love he showed them.
And anyone receives a greater reward in proportion as he does not despise the one who seems more worthy of being despised.

Chapter 51: On the Corruption of Present Life

For the just established in this life, the very burden of their corruption is burdensome: vigils exhaust, sleep is sought so that the labor and anxiety of vigils may be tempered. Sometimes even sound kills, hunger wears away the body, and food is sought so that its necessity may be repelled. But often also food weighs down—food that had been sought to repel the weight of weakness.
Very heavy is the burden of corruption; unless it were vehemently burdensome, Paul would by no means say: Creation was subjected to futility not willingly, but because of him who subjected it in hope, because creation itself will be freed from the slavery of corruption into the freedom of the glory of the children of God. For we know that all creation groans and labors until now—from Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter eight, verse twenty.
As long as we are subjected to corruption, we scarcely correspond to our Maker, because while corruption is far from incorruption, there is no fitting likeness for our correspondence. Blessed Job, desiring the state of eternal incorruption, says: You will extend your right hand to the work of your hands—from Job, chapter fourteen, verse fifteen.
As if to say openly: Therefore the corruptible creature can persist toward incorruption because it is raised up by the hand of almighty God, and by the grace of his regard is held so that it persists. Human creation by the very fact that it is creation has in itself to flow down beneath itself; but from his creator man receives that he be both rapt above himself in contemplation and be held in incorruption in himself.
Human creation, subjected to corruption, lest it flow down beneath itself but persist in incorruption, is lifted up to the state of immutability by the right hand of its Maker.

Chapter 52: On Bodily Health

The healthy are to be admonished to exercise bodily health toward the health of the mind, lest if they incline the grace of received health to the use of wickedness, they become worse by the gift, and afterward merit graver punishments to the degree that now they do not fear to use badly God's more abundant goods.
The healthy are to be admonished not to despise the opportunity of meriting salvation in perpetuity. For it is written: Behold now is the acceptable time, behold now is the day of salvation—from Paul's second letter to the Corinthians, chapter six, verse two. It must be said to them lest, if when they are able they are unwilling to please God, when they are willing they are late unable.
Bodily health, when received for doing good it is despised, is felt to be of what great gift when it is lost; and it is sought fruitlessly at the end, which when granted at the fitting time is not held usefully.
Through the most wise Solomon it is said: Do not give your labor to strangers, and your years to the cruel one, lest perhaps strangers be filled with your strength and your labors be in a foreign house, and you groan in the end when you have consumed your flesh and body—from Proverbs, chapter five, verse nine and following.
Who indeed are strangers to us but evil spirits who are separated from the lot of the heavenly homeland? What truly is our honor but that even formed in clay bodies, we are nevertheless created in the image and likeness of our creator? Or who else is cruel but that apostate angel who both struck himself with the punishment of death through pride, and even though lost did not spare inflicting death on the human race?
He gives his honor to strangers who, formed in the image and likeness of God, administers the times of his life to the pleasures of evil spirits. He also delivers his years to the cruel one who expends the spaces of living received to the will of the adversary ruling badly.
Whoever through received bodily health, through wisdom of mind attributed to him, labors not in exercising virtues but in perpetrating vices, by no means with his own strength multiplies his own house, but the dwellings of strangers, that is, the deeds of unclean spirits, certainly acting either by lusting or by being proud, so that the number of the lost grows with himself added.
Often received health of the flesh is expended through vices; but when it is suddenly withdrawn, when the flesh is worn away with troubles, when the soul is now urged to depart, long badly possessed, as though the lost health is sought for living well. Then men rightly groan that they were unwilling to serve God, when they can by no means recover the losses of their negligence by serving. Hence elsewhere it is said: When he killed them, then they sought him—from Psalm seventy-seven, verse thirty-four.

Chapter 53: On Bodily Sickness

The sick are to be admonished to feel themselves to be God's sons by the fact that the scourges of discipline chastise them. For unless he were disposing to give an inheritance to corrected sons, he would not care to train them through troubles. It must be said to the sick that if they believe the heavenly homeland is theirs, it is necessary that they suffer labors in this one as in a foreign homeland.
The stones of the Lord's sanctuary were struck outside so that they might be placed in the construction of the temple without the sound of a hammer. Because clearly now we are struck outside through scourges, so that afterward we may be arranged inside in the temple of God without the percussion of discipline; so that whatever is superfluous in us the striking may now cut off, and then the concord of charity alone may bind us in the building.
The sick are to be admonished to consider, for receiving earthly inheritances, how hard are the scourges of discipline that chastise carnal sons. Why therefore is the punishment of divine correction heavy for us, through which both an inheritance never to be lost is received and punishments always to remain are avoided?
Let the sick consider what great salvation of the heart is bodily trouble, which recalls the mind to knowledge of itself, and what health often rejects, the memory of infirmity reforms, so that the soul which is led outside itself in elation may be reminded from the struck flesh which it sustains of what condition it is subjected to.
Often the flesh, slow through troubles, indicates God to the mind by its scourge—God whom the mind itself presiding over the flesh did not see—so that it impedes the anxiety of the spirit desiring to advance in this world, as of one extending a journey, until the invisible one who meets him becomes known to him.
The sick are to be admonished to consider of how great a gift is bodily trouble, which both washes away committed sins and restrains those which could be committed, which taken from exterior blows inflicts the wounds of repentance on the shaken mind. In the Proverbs of Solomon it is written: The bruise of a wound cleanses away evils, and blows in the inner parts of the belly—from Proverbs, chapter twenty, verse twenty.
For the bruise of a wound cleanses away evils because the pain of scourges washes away either thought or perpetrated wickednesses. When we are struck outwardly, we are recalled silent and afflicted to the memory of our sins, we bring back before our eyes all things that were done badly by us, and through this that we suffer outwardly we grieve more inwardly for what we have done.
Whence it happens that amid the open wounds of the body the secret blow of the belly washes us more fully, because the hidden wound of pain heals the wickednesses of evil work.
The sick are to be admonished to preserve the virtue of patience, so that they consider unceasingly how great evils the Redeemer endured from those he had created. Why therefore is it believed harsh that man endure scourges from God for evils, if God endured so great evils from men for goods? Or who would be ungrateful with sound understanding concerning his own striking, if he himself did not depart hence without scourge who lived here without sin?

Chapter 54: On Bodily Death

The days fixed for individuals by the internal foreknowledge of God can neither be increased nor diminished, unless it happens that they are so foreknown that either with the best works they are longer, or with the worst shorter, just as Hezekiah merited the increase of days by the outpouring of tears, and as it is written concerning the perverse: Death meets the undisciplined.
Everything that we labor according to the present age scarcely suffices until death. For death intervening cuts off the fruit of our labor; but what is done for eternal life is preserved even after death, and then begins to appear when the fruit of carnal labors begins not to be seen. There therefore that retribution begins where this one is terminated.
Our Lord wanted the last hour to be unknown to us for this reason: that it might always be able to be suspected; so that while we cannot foresee it, we may prepare for it without interruption. Because we do not know the time of coming death, and after death we cannot work, it remains that before death we seize the times granted.
For thus death itself when it comes will be conquered, if before it comes it is always feared. Our Redeemer underwent death so that we would not fear to die. He showed resurrection so that we might be confident that we can rise. Hence he wanted that same death to be no more than three days, lest if resurrection were deferred in him, it would be despaired of altogether in us.
We through the death of the flesh remain in dust until the end of the world; but Christ on the third day sprouted from the dryness of death, and showed to us the power of his divinity in the very renewal of his flesh.
The soul stripped of flesh no longer looks upon human sight, because it does not free after death the one whom grace does not reform to pardon before death. Blessed David says: Their spirit will go out and will return to its earth—from Psalm one hundred forty-five, verse four. Then the spirit is cast down to earth through knowledge of its guilt when the flesh, which it believes is its life, is urged to return to dust.
Man is strengthened a little in the present life because here he received the strength of living for a little, so that he may pass into perpetuity where the boundary does not close his life. But in this brevity where he is strengthened, he gathers from where in perpetuity he may find either to rejoice always or not to escape received punishments.
In the book of blessed Job it is written: You will change his face and send him forth—from Job, chapter fourteen, verse twenty. The face of man is changed when his appearance is worn away by death. He is sent forth because from those things which he held willingly, he is forced unwillingly to pass to eternal things. And while he is led to those things, he does not know how these things which he held thought about for a long time will be disposed when left behind.
Just as those who are still living do not know in what place the souls of the dead are held, so the dead do not know how the life in the flesh of those living after them is disposed, because the life of the spirit is far from the life of the flesh, and just as corporeal and incorporeal things are diverse in kind, so also they are distinct in knowledge.
This however is not to be thought concerning holy souls, because those which see within the brightness of almighty God, it is by no means to be believed that there is anything outside which they do not know.
The almighty Lord wanted our end to be hidden from us for this reason: so that while we are uncertain when we will die, we should always be found prepared for death. Why therefore does he lift himself up as though certain, whose life is held under the penalty of uncertainty?
Paul the distinguished preacher says: We who are in this dwelling groan, being burdened, because we do not wish to be unclothed but to be clothed over, so that what is mortal may be swallowed up by life—from Paul's second letter to the Corinthians, chapter five, verse four. Behold, he desires to die, and yet fears to be stripped of flesh. Why this? Because even if victory gladdens in perpetuity, nevertheless the punishment of death at present disturbs.
And although the love of the following gift conquers, nevertheless the impulse of pain touches the soul not without sorrow. For just as a strong man, when he girds himself with arms with the battle contest already near, palpitates and hastens, trembles and rages, is seen as though to fear through pallor, but is vehemently urged through anger: so the holy man, when he perceives himself approaching the passion of death, is shaken by the weakness of his nature and strengthened by the solidity of his hope.
He trembles indeed concerning nearby death, and yet exults that he will live more truly by dying. Each holy man cannot pass to the kingdom except through interposed death. And therefore trusting he as it were doubts, and as it were doubting he trusts, and rejoicing he fears, and fearing he rejoices, because he knows that he will not arrive at the prize of rest unless he transcends with labor this that lies between.
When we desire to repel diseases from our body, sad indeed we take the bitter drink of purgation, but certain we rejoice concerning subsequent health. For because the body cannot otherwise arrive at health, even what disgusts is pleasing in drink. And when the soul perceives life to be present in bitterness, troubled by sorrow it grows cheerful.
In the book of Solomon it is written: If a tree falls to the south or to the north, in whatever place it falls, there it will be—from Ecclesiastes, chapter eleven, verse three. For by the name of tree is expressed either a just man or also an unjust one, just as the Lord says through the prophet: I the Lord have humbled the lofty tree—from Ezekiel, chapter seventeen, verse twenty-four.
On the day of his death the just person falls to the south, the sinner to the north, because both the just person is led through fervor of spirit to joys, and the sinner is rejected in his cold heart with the apostate angel who said: I will sit on the mount of the covenant in the sides of the north—from Isaiah, chapter fourteen, verse thirteen.
The tree, in whatever place it falls, whether to the south or to the north, there it will be, because when at the time of human fall either a holy or an evil spirit receives the soul departing from the enclosures of the flesh, it will retain it with itself in eternity without any change. So that neither does the exalted one fall to judgment, nor does the one submerged in eternal punishments rise beyond to the remedy of rescue.
